Every transition between two homes is, from a child's perspective, a small disruption. Something is ending. Something else is beginning. The adults around them are managing their own emotions about what is happening.

Most children adapt to transitions over time. The patterns that make adaptation easier — and the ones that make it harder — are consistent enough across developmental stages to be worth naming.

## Why transitions are a distinct challenge

A transition between homes is not just a logistical handover. For a child, it means:

- Shifting attachment context: the parent they are about to leave, the parent they are about to be with
- Adjusting to different household rules, routines, and expectations
- Managing whatever emotional content surrounds the transition itself

Children do not fully separate the logistics from the emotional content. A transition that happens in a tense, conflicted atmosphere is a different experience than one that happens in a calm, predictable one.

Under *Divorce Act* section 16(3), courts consider the child's need for stability and each parent's ability to meet the child's needs. How transitions are managed is part of that picture.

## By developmental stage

### Infants and toddlers (under 3)

Very young children depend on predictability and on the physical presence of their primary attachment figures. Frequent, short periods with each parent — rather than long stretches with one followed by long stretches with the other — are generally more consistent with developmental needs at this stage.

Transitions for very young children are most stable when they are brief, calm, and consistent. The handover is not the place for a long goodbye or for the adults to discuss logistics at length. A child this age reads the emotional atmosphere around them more than the words being said.

If the parenting arrangement involves extended overnight time away from the primary caregiver at this age, the research suggests that consistency of the routine in each home matters significantly.

### Preschool age (3-5)

Children in this range are developing language but not yet a reliable understanding of time. "You'll see Daddy on Wednesday" has limited meaning for a 4-year-old. A visual schedule — pictures or a calendar with stickers — can make the rhythm of transitions concrete.

Transitions at this stage are easier when they are tied to predictable anchors: after school, after lunch, on the same day each week. Unpredictability about when transitions happen is distressing. Predictability about what happens next is reassuring.

Children at this age may express distress at transitions that they are managing adequately. A child crying at the handover and then running to play five minutes later is different from a child who is consistently dysregulated after transitions.

### School age (6-12)

Children in middle childhood can hold a more complex picture of two households and begin to understand weekly or biweekly schedules. They can use language to say what is hard. They also have a school life, friendships, and activities that exist independently of either parent.

At this stage, the practical logistics of transitions interact more with the child's social life. A schedule that requires a child to miss a birthday party or a sports practice every week creates conflict between the child's loyalties to the parenting arrangement and their need for a peer life. This is worth considering in parenting plan design.

Children at this age also notice conflict between their parents more acutely and are more likely to feel responsible for managing it. The research on children's adjustment after separation consistently identifies parental conflict — particularly conflict in which children feel caught — as the factor most associated with poor adjustment outcomes.

### Adolescents (13 and older)

Teenagers have strong views about their own schedules. A 15-year-old with a social life, a part-time job, and extracurricular commitments experiences a rigid, parent-determined parenting schedule differently than a 7-year-old.

What typically works better at this stage: a flexible framework with the teenager involved in the logistics, rather than a fixed schedule enforced regardless of what the teenager has on. The *Divorce Act* section 16(3)(e) recognizes the child's views and preferences, weighted by maturity, as a factor. At adolescence, that weight increases.

Flexibility at this stage is not abandonment of the parenting arrangement. It is recognition that the arrangement exists to serve the teenager's needs, not the other way around.

## What makes transitions harder

Consistent patterns across research on children's adjustment to separation include:

- Transitions that happen in conflicted, tense atmospheres between the parents
- Children being used to carry messages, objects, or emotional content between parents
- Unpredictable timing: the child does not know when the transition is coming
- Children being asked to take sides, report on the other household, or manage a parent's distress
- Transitions that frequently disrupt significant events (parties, performances, games)

These are not predictions about any specific child's outcome. They are patterns observed consistently across research on children of separation. The *Divorce Act* factor requiring courts to consider each parent's ability to meet the child's needs, and their willingness to support the other parent's relationship with the child, is directly connected to these patterns.

---

## What makes transitions easier

- Consistent timing: the child knows what to expect and when
- Calm handovers: brief, warm, without long goodbyes or adult logistics
- Predictable transition objects: favorite items travel with the child as anchors
- Agreement between homes about basic routines: bedtime, homework, screen time being broadly consistent reduces re-adjustment time
- Both parents expressing openness and warmth about the other parent's home

None of this requires the parents to like each other. It requires them to manage what they demonstrate to the child.
