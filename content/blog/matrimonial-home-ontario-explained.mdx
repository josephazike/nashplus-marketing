Every asset subject to Ontario's equalization framework follows the same basic rule: when calculating net family property, a spouse can deduct the net value of property they owned before the marriage.

There is one exception. The matrimonial home.

If a spouse owned the home that became the matrimonial home before they married, they cannot deduct its value at the date of marriage from their net family property calculation. The full value of the home at the date of separation goes into their NFP, without any offset for what it was worth when they brought it into the marriage.

This is one of the most consequential and least intuitive rules in Ontario property law. It warrants a careful read.

## What a matrimonial home is

Under section 18(1) of the *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3*, a matrimonial home is every property in which a person has an interest and that is, or was at the time of separation, ordinarily occupied by the person and their spouse as their family residence.

The definition has two key elements: the spouse must have an interest in the property, and the property must have been ordinarily occupied as the family residence.

A property used only as a vacation cottage is not a matrimonial home. A property one spouse owned before the marriage but that the couple never lived in is not a matrimonial home. A property the couple moved out of entirely before separation is not a matrimonial home — though the question of when they moved out may itself be disputed.

Where a couple owns more than one property in which they have an interest, and both were ordinarily occupied as family residences, both can qualify as matrimonial homes under section 18(2). This is less common but arises in cases involving a principal residence and a recreational property.

---

## The no-deduction rule

Under section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act*, when calculating net family property, each spouse deducts the net value of property they owned at the date of marriage. This prevents pre-marital wealth from being subject to equalization.

The deduction does not apply to the matrimonial home.

If a spouse owned a home before the marriage and that home became the matrimonial home, its value at the date of marriage cannot be deducted from their NFP. The full current value (at the date of separation) is included in their NFP without any adjustment for the value they brought to the marriage.

<Callout type="warning" title="The matrimonial home trap">
  A spouse who owned a home before marrying, and who lived in that home with their partner during the marriage, may find that their equalization obligation is significantly higher than expected. The entire appreciation in the home's value — including any appreciation that predated the marriage — is subject to equalization.
</Callout>

This asymmetry is intentional. The matrimonial home has a special status under the *Family Law Act* because both spouses have an equal right to possession of it during the marriage. That right to possession — held regardless of who is on title — carries a corresponding property consequence in the equalization calculation.

---

## Possession rights during and after separation

Section 19(1) of the *Family Law Act* gives each spouse an equal right to possession of the matrimonial home during marriage, regardless of who owns it. This right cannot be waived except by agreement or court order.

This means that even if the matrimonial home is registered solely in one spouse's name, the other spouse has a legal right to remain in the home during the marriage. They cannot be excluded without a court order (or their consent).

After separation, the equal right to possession continues until a court order or domestic contract provides otherwise. Section 24 of the *Family Law Act* gives courts the authority to make a possession order, giving exclusive possession of the matrimonial home to one spouse, regardless of ownership.

A possession order does not transfer ownership. It determines who can live in the home during the proceeding.

---

## Disposal and encumbrance restrictions

Section 21(1) of the *Family Law Act* prohibits a spouse who owns the matrimonial home from selling, encumbering, or otherwise disposing of the property without the other spouse's consent.

This applies even if the non-owning spouse's name is not on the title. A property listed only in one spouse's name cannot be sold or mortgaged without the other spouse consenting, as long as it is the matrimonial home.

The restriction applies at the time of the transaction. If a spouse sells the property without consent after separation has occurred and the property is still the matrimonial home, the transaction can potentially be set aside.

---

## The NFP calculation with and without the matrimonial home

The no-deduction rule's practical impact can be illustrated in the simplified example the existing article on [net family property in Ontario](/blog/net-family-property-ontario) works through. For a spouse who owned a home worth $300,000 before marriage and who now values it at $700,000 at separation:

- If the home were treated like any other asset: the date-of-marriage value of $300,000 would be deducted, and only $400,000 of appreciation would enter the NFP calculation.
- Because it is the matrimonial home: the full $700,000 enters the NFP with no deduction for the $300,000 brought to the marriage.

The difference — $300,000 in this example — directly affects the equalization payment.

---

## Excluded property and the matrimonial home

Section 4(2) of the *Family Law Act* lists categories of excluded property — gifts, inheritances, personal injury damages, and traceable proceeds — that do not count in the NFP calculation.

The exclusions explicitly do not apply to the matrimonial home. If an inheritance was used to purchase the matrimonial home, the home's value is still included in the NFP. The excluded character of the inheritance is lost when it is invested in the matrimonial home.
