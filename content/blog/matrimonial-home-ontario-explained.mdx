Of all the rules in Ontario's family property law, this one surprises people the most: if you owned the matrimonial home before the marriage, you cannot deduct its pre-marriage value when calculating what you owe your spouse at separation.

Every other asset owned before the marriage (RRSP savings, an investment account, a rental property, a vehicle) can be offset against its date-of-marriage value in the equalization calculation. The matrimonial home is the exception. Its full value at the date of separation enters your net family property calculation with no reduction for what it was worth on your wedding day.

## What is a matrimonial home

Section 18(1) of the *Family Law Act*, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3 defines the matrimonial home as every property in which a person has an interest and that is, or if the spouses have separated was at the time of separation, ordinarily occupied by the person and their spouse as their family residence.

Three aspects of this definition matter.

**Title is not the deciding factor.** A home owned solely by one spouse can be the matrimonial home. The matrimonial home rules apply regardless of whose name is on the deed.

**More than one property can qualify.** If the family ordinarily occupied a principal residence and a cottage, both may be matrimonial homes at the time of separation. Section 20 of the *Family Law Act* allows a spouse to designate one property as the matrimonial home by registration. Where no designation is made and the parties disagree, the court determines which property or properties qualify.

**Status is determined at separation, not at marriage.** A property the family moved into during the marriage and occupied as the family home qualifies. A property that was the family home earlier in the marriage but sold before separation does not.

---

## The no-deduction rule

The equalization formula established in section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act* deducts the net value of property owned by each spouse at the date of marriage. This reflects the principle that equalization captures what was built during the marriage, not what was brought in. A spouse who enters the marriage owning a rental property can offset that property's date-of-marriage value against their net family property calculation.

The matrimonial home is explicitly excluded from this deduction.

<Callout type="warning" title="The no-deduction trap">
  A home owned before marriage carries its full date-of-separation value into the equalization calculation if it was the matrimonial home at separation. A spouse who paid $200,000 for the home years before marrying, and whose home is now worth $800,000, cannot offset the $200,000 starting value. The full $800,000, minus any outstanding mortgage, enters their net family property. The entire appreciation across the pre-marriage and marriage periods is included.
</Callout>

This outcome applies even where the home was paid for entirely before the relationship began. The legislature chose to protect the non-owning spouse's equalization claim against the appreciation of the family home across the entire period of occupation as a family residence.

The excluded-property provision in section 4(2) of the *Family Law Act* is worth noting specifically. Gifts and inheritances received during the marriage are ordinarily excluded from net family property if they can be traced. The matrimonial home is an exception to that exception: if a gift or inheritance was used to purchase the matrimonial home, or if the inherited property itself became the matrimonial home, the exclusion does not apply. The matrimonial home rules override the gift-and-inheritance exclusion.

## Possession rights during proceedings

Section 19 of the *Family Law Act* gives both spouses an equal right to possession of the matrimonial home regardless of who holds title. This right exists as long as the parties are married. It does not transfer ownership; it is a possessory right that survives into separation proceedings until an order addresses it or the parties agree.

Section 21 of the *Family Law Act* prohibits a spouse from selling, mortgaging, charging, or otherwise encumbering the matrimonial home without the written consent of the other spouse or a court order. A disposition made without consent is voidable: the non-consenting spouse can apply to have the transaction set aside.

These provisions exist to prevent one spouse from unilaterally removing the other from the family home or depleting its value before a property proceeding is resolved.

---

## The section 6 election

Section 6 of the *Family Law Act* creates a specific situation on death. A surviving spouse who is entitled to receive the matrimonial home under the deceased's will or on intestacy may elect whether to take the inheritance or to claim equalization under Part I of the Act instead. The court may extend the deadline for making this election where circumstances require.

This provision applies in estate proceedings, not in separation while both spouses are alive. It is noted here for completeness.

## Common-law partners and the matrimonial home

Sections 18 to 26 of the *Family Law Act*, the entire set of matrimonial home provisions, apply to married spouses only. Common-law partners do not have the same possessory rights, disposal restrictions, or equalization entitlements under the Act. Property rights for common-law partners rest on different legal principles, explained in the article on [common-law property rights and Kerr v Baranow](/blog/common-law-property-rights-ontario-kerr-baranow).

## How this connects to your financial disclosure

When completing Form 13.1, Part 4 requires values at three dates: date of marriage, date of separation, and today. For most assets, the date-of-marriage column informs the deduction in the NFP formula. For the matrimonial home, the date-of-marriage column is still completed for disclosure purposes, but the value does not reduce your equalization exposure. Understanding this before filling out Part 4 prevents errors in the NFP summary.

The full structure of Form 13.1's three-column property table is explained in [the three-column property table on Form 13.1](/blog/three-column-property-valuation-13-1-ontario). The NFP formula is explained in [net family property in Ontario](/blog/net-family-property-ontario).
