A case conference is not a hearing. There is no testimony. No one wins, no one loses, and no binding decisions about the outcome of the case are made.

That is the first and most important thing to understand about a case conference — because most people who walk in for the first time are prepared for a very different kind of event.

What the case conference actually is: the first substantive meeting between the parties and a judge, designed to identify what is agreed, what is disputed, and what the path forward looks like. The judge's job is to help the parties understand the scope of the proceeding and move the case toward resolution as efficiently as possible.

## The purpose of a case conference

Under Rule 17(4) of the *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99*, the purposes of a case conference include:

- Exploring the chances of settling any or all of the issues
- Identifying the issues that are contested and those that are not
- Ensuring disclosure of the relevant evidence
- Facilitating a resolution of the case
- Setting a schedule for any steps remaining in the proceeding

The judge is not there to decide who is right. The judge is there to help the parties understand where they agree, where they disagree, and what information needs to be exchanged before the next stage.

---

## What happens in the room

The typical case conference follows a recognizable pattern.

The judge will have read the conference brief before arriving. They usually begin by summarizing their understanding of the issues, asking each party to confirm or correct it. They may ask questions about the case — not to probe for admissions but to clarify what is actually disputed.

The judge may then identify issues where agreement seems reachable and encourage the parties to discuss them, either in the conference room or in a brief recess. Where issues cannot be resolved, the judge records what remains in dispute.

At the close of the conference, the judge makes an endorsement — a written note in the court record summarizing what was discussed, what was agreed, what remains disputed, and what the next steps are. That endorsement goes into the endorsement volume of the continuing record.

## Required forms

Two forms are required for a case conference.

**Form 17A — Case Conference Brief.** This is the document each party files before the conference. It tells the judge: who the parties are, what the history of the family is, what the agreed issues are, what the disputed issues are, what financial disclosure has been exchanged, and what each party is asking the court to do.

The applicant files Form 17A at least **six days before** the conference. The respondent files at least **four days before**.

**Form 17F — Confirmation of Case Conference.** This short form confirms that the party intends to appear and is ready to proceed. It must be filed **three days before** the conference by each party. If neither party files Form 17F, the court may remove the conference from the list.

<Callout type="warning" title="Missing Form 17F">
  Failing to file Form 17F three days before the conference can result in the case conference being removed from the docket. This causes delay and may attract a costs consequence under Rule 24 of the Family Law Rules.
</Callout>

## What the judge can and cannot do at a case conference

A judge at a case conference **can**:

- Make procedural orders (setting timelines, ordering disclosure)
- Make temporary orders on consent (if both parties agree to something)
- Make recommendations about resolution
- Record what is agreed and what remains disputed

A judge at a case conference **cannot**:

- Make binding orders that determine the outcome of disputed issues (without consent)
- Hear evidence or receive sworn testimony
- Make final orders about parenting, support, or property based on contested facts

This is a critical distinction. If a party at a case conference says something that seems damaging, or if the judge makes a comment that seems unfavourable, it is not a final decision. The conference is an organizational meeting, not a determination.

## What to prepare

The most useful preparation for a case conference is a thorough, clear Form 17A and a clear understanding of three things:

1. What are the agreed issues?
2. What are the disputed issues?
3. What do you want the court to do next?
