Support calculations are built on income. Child support tables use the payor's income. Spousal support assessments turn on both parties' incomes. When a court finds that a party's reported income does not reflect their real earning capacity, it can substitute a different number. That substituted number is imputed income.

## The legal authority

The power to impute income for child support comes from section 19(1) of the *Federal Child Support Guidelines, SOR/97-175*. The provision allows a court to "impute such amount of income to a spouse as it considers appropriate in the circumstances."

Section 19(1) sets out a non-exhaustive list of circumstances that can justify imputing income:

- The spouse is intentionally under-employed or unemployed, except where the under-employment is required by the needs of a child or by the spouse's reasonable educational or health needs
- The spouse is exempt from paying federal or provincial income tax
- The spouse lives in a country with significantly lower effective income tax rates than Canada
- Income appears to have been diverted in a way that affects the child support amount
- The spouse's property is not reasonably utilized to generate income
- The spouse has failed to provide required income information
- The spouse unreasonably deducts expenses from income
- The spouse derives significant income from dividends, capital gains, or other lower-taxed sources
- The spouse receives income or benefits through a trust

The most frequently litigated ground is the first: intentional under-employment.

---

## Where imputation comes up

Imputed income arises in several common patterns.

**A parent who leaves paid work after separation.** Courts distinguish between a genuine career change and a strategic reduction in income to reduce support obligations. Evidence of prior income, qualifications, and available job opportunities is relevant.

**A self-employed parent.** When a self-employed person controls what they pay themselves, their reported income may not reflect the income actually available to them. Courts may look at business revenues, lifestyle, shareholder benefits, and corporate retained earnings.

**Income from a controlled corporation.** A parent who receives a salary from their own company, controls dividend timing, or routes personal expenses through the business may have income attributed to them beyond their T4.

**A parent receiving unreported benefits.** Housing, vehicles, or other benefits provided through an employer or corporation that are not reflected in taxable income can be included in imputed income.

---

## Why it matters

Child support under the *Federal Child Support Guidelines* is a direct function of income and the number of children. The difference between a reported income and an imputed income can translate to a significant difference in monthly support.

For spousal support, there is no equivalent statutory imputation provision, but the same underlying principle applies: courts assess what a party could reasonably earn in their circumstances and may factor that into the support analysis.

If you are the recipient asking for imputation, you will need evidence. If you are the payor and your income is being challenged, you will need documentation of why your reported income accurately reflects your capacity to earn.
