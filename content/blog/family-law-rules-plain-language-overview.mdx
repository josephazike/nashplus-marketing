Every procedural step in an Ontario family law proceeding — how to start a case, how to serve documents, what forms to file, how to bring a motion, what happens at a conference — is governed by the *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99*.

Most self-represented litigants encounter these rules as citations: "Rule 17," "Rule 13(3.1)," "Rule 6." They see the citation in a court document, a legal information guide, or a judge's endorsement — and they have no framework for finding what it means.

This article gives that framework.

## What the Family Law Rules are

The *Family Law Rules* are a regulation made under the *Courts of Justice Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.43*. They are Ontario Regulation 114/99 — meaning the regulation was first made in 1999 and has been amended many times since. The current version is available on the Ontario e-Laws website at ontario.ca/laws.

The Rules govern procedure, not substance. They do not say what the law is on property division or parenting. They say how the process works: which forms to use, when to file them, how to serve them, what happens at conferences, and how costs are awarded.

The substantive law — what a party is entitled to — comes from statutes: the *Family Law Act*, the *Divorce Act*, the *Children's Law Reform Act*, the *Federal Child Support Guidelines*. The Rules govern how parties get to a court decision on those substantive questions.

---

## How the Rules are organized

The Family Law Rules are organized into numbered rules, each covering a distinct procedural topic. Each rule may have sub-rules (numbered with parentheses) and sub-sub-rules.

A rule citation like **Rule 13(3.1)(1)** means:
- Rule 13 (the rule about financial disclosure)
- Subsection 3.1 (a sub-rule within Rule 13)
- Sub-paragraph 1 (a specific provision within that sub-rule)

Reading citations from left to right gives you the path: major rule, then sub-rule, then sub-paragraph.

## How to read the Rules

The *Family Law Rules* are available in full on the Ontario e-Laws website. Searching "Family Law Rules" on ontario.ca/laws brings up the current version of O. Reg. 114/99. The rules are organized numerically, and each rule has a title describing its subject.

When you encounter a rule citation — in a court document, a legal information guide, or a judicial endorsement — the pattern for reading it is:

1. **Find the rule number.** Rule 17 means "conferences."
2. **Find the sub-rule.** Rule 17(4) means the fourth sub-rule within Rule 17.
3. **Read the sub-rule** in the context of the rule as a whole.

Rules often refer to other rules. Rule 6 (service) is referenced throughout the Rules wherever documents must be served. Rule 24 (costs) is referenced in many places where the court has discretion to award costs. When you see a cross-reference to another rule, follow it.

## Practice directions

The Family Law Rules are supplemented by **practice directions** issued by the Ontario Court of Justice and the Superior Court of Justice. Practice directions address local procedures — specific timelines, filing conventions, and court-specific requirements that operate on top of the Family Law Rules.

When the Rules say something like "file the conference brief 6 days before," a local practice direction may modify that timeline or add a requirement specific to that courthouse. The practice directions for the relevant court are published on the court's website.

---

## The overriding objective

Rule 1 of the *Family Law Rules* states the overriding objective: the rules are to be interpreted to enable the court to deal with cases justly, in a timely and cost-effective way.

Courts apply this principle when procedural disputes arise. A party who is technically non-compliant with a rule but has not prejudiced the other party may receive different treatment than a party whose non-compliance created real delay or expense. Rule 2(3) gives the court authority to dispense with a rule where it would create an injustice.

This does not mean the Rules can be ignored. It means courts are not purely technical — they consider the purpose of the rule alongside its text. For an SRL navigating procedure, this principle is worth knowing: technical errors, promptly corrected and not prejudicial, are generally survivable.

## Where to find the current version

The *Family Law Rules* are amended periodically. The current version is the only authoritative version. When a rule is cited to you, always check the current e-Laws text rather than a secondary source that may be out of date. The URL is ontario.ca/laws/regulation/990114.
