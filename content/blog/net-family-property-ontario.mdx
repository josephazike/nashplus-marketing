When a marriage ends in Ontario, property is not automatically divided equally. Instead, Ontario law uses a calculation called **equalization of net family property** to determine whether one spouse owes the other a payment. Understanding this calculation is essential for anyone going through a property-related family law proceeding.

---

## The governing statute

Equalization is governed by Part I of the *Family Law Act*, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3. The relevant sections are 5 through 9. The core principle, set out in section 5(1), is that each spouse is entitled to equalization of the net family property that each accumulated during the marriage.

Note: equalization is a payment obligation, not a transfer of ownership. The law does not automatically vest property in either spouse's name. What it creates is a **debt obligation** from the spouse with the higher net family property to the spouse with the lower net family property.

---

## The formula

**Net Family Property (NFP)** is calculated separately for each spouse.

For each spouse:

```
NFP = (Total assets at valuation date)
    − (Total debts at valuation date)
    − (Excluded property)
    − (Net value at date of marriage)
```

Once each spouse's NFP is determined:

```
Equalization Payment = (Higher NFP − Lower NFP) ÷ 2
```

The spouse with the higher NFP owes that amount to the other.

---

## Valuation date

The **valuation date** is the reference point for measuring the assets and debts in the NFP calculation. Under section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act*, the valuation date is the earliest of:

1. The date the spouses separate and there is no reasonable prospect of reconciliation
2. The date a divorce is granted
3. The date the marriage is declared a nullity
4. The date one spouse is presumed dead under another Ontario statute
5. The date of an application for equalization (where there is no separation)

In practice, **the date of separation** is most often the valuation date. Parties sometimes disagree about this date, and that factual dispute can itself require judicial resolution.

---

## What counts as an asset

Assets at the valuation date include everything of value owned by that spouse on that date:

- **Real property**: the matrimonial home, other properties, fractional interests in land
- **Financial accounts**: bank accounts, GICs, TFSAs, non-registered investment accounts
- **Registered retirement savings**: RRSPs, RRIFs, LIRAs, and other registered plans
- **Pensions**: both defined contribution and defined benefit pensions, valued at the date of separation
- **Business interests**: shares in a corporation, partnership interests, sole proprietorship goodwill
- **Vehicles and personal property**: vehicles, boats, jewelry, art, collectibles above a threshold
- **Life insurance**: policies with a cash surrender value
- **Receivables**: money owed to that spouse

The test is ownership at the valuation date. Property transferred before that date — including strategic transfers — may be subject to scrutiny under section 5(6) of the *Family Law Act* (unequal division on grounds of unconscionability) or under trust law.

---

## Excluded property

Section 4(2) of the *Family Law Act* excludes certain categories of property from the NFP calculation. Excluded property does not accumulate during the marriage for equalization purposes.

The excluded categories under section 4(2) are:

1. **Gifts and inheritances received from a third person after the date of marriage**, other than a matrimonial home — provided the property can still be traced
2. **Income from those gifts or inheritances**, if the donor or testator expressly stated that income was to be excluded
3. **Damages for personal injuries**, nervous shock, mental distress, or loss of guidance, care and companionship — and the right to such damages
4. **Proceeds of a life insurance policy** payable on the death of the life insured
5. **Property traceable to any of items 1–4 above**, other than a matrimonial home
6. **Property agreed to be excluded** in a domestic contract

A separate and distinct mechanism — the **date-of-marriage deduction** in the NFP formula itself — removes the net value of property each spouse owned before the marriage (other than the matrimonial home). This is not an exclusion under s.4(2); it is a deduction built into the NFP calculation.

Exclusions require tracing. If an inheritance was deposited into a joint account and commingled with marital funds, the excluded character may be lost. Parties asserting an exclusion bear the burden of establishing it.

---

## Net value at date of marriage

The NFP formula deducts the net value of property the spouse brought into the marriage. This reflects the principle that equalization captures only what was *accumulated during the marriage*, not pre-marital wealth.

The deduction is:

```
(Total assets at date of marriage) − (Total debts at date of marriage)
```

This figure can only be positive or zero — section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act* provides that it cannot be less than zero. A spouse who entered the marriage deeply in debt does not get to apply a negative starting-point against their valuation-date NFP.

**Important**: the matrimonial home is excluded from this deduction even if owned at the date of marriage. Its full value at the valuation date is included in the NFP with no offset for its value at marriage. This asymmetry often produces significant equalization obligations for the spouse who owned the home before marrying.

---

## The matrimonial home

The matrimonial home deserves separate attention because it is treated differently from all other assets:

1. It is **not excluded** from NFP even if owned before marriage
2. Both spouses have an **equal right to possession** of the matrimonial home during the marriage, regardless of who is on title
3. A spouse cannot sell, mortgage, or otherwise encumber the matrimonial home without the other spouse's consent, under section 21 of the *Family Law Act*

Where a couple owns more than one property, the matrimonial home is the property that was ordinarily occupied as a family residence at the time of separation.

---

## Unequal division

Section 5(6) of the *Family Law Act* permits the court to order an amount other than half the difference if equal division would be unconscionable. The provision lists specific circumstances that can support a finding of unconscionability, including:

- A spouse's failure to disclose debts or property existing at the date of marriage
- Deliberate depletion of property in anticipation of separation
- A marriage of very short duration with significant inequity from equal division
- Reckless depletion of property

Unconscionability is a high threshold. Courts do not depart from equal division simply because the outcome seems harsh; the conduct or circumstances must be extreme.

---

## Pensions and equalization

Pensions are often the most valuable asset in a marriage and among the most difficult to value. Ontario's *Family Law Act* treats pensions as property subject to equalization. The Pension Benefits Act and rules under it govern how pension values are calculated and divided.

For defined benefit pensions, the value at the date of separation is typically calculated by the pension plan administrator using an actuarial formula. In some circumstances, the parties may agree to divide the pension at source rather than making a cash equalization payment.

If a defined benefit pension is involved, it is common to obtain a formal valuation from the pension plan administrator before completing Form 13.1.

## Working through the numbers

A simplified example illustrates the calculation.

**Spouse A at date of separation:**
- Assets: $480,000 (home) + $52,000 (RRSP) + $14,000 (bank) = $546,000
- Debts: $280,000 (mortgage) + $4,000 (credit card) = $284,000
- Excluded property: $48,000 (inheritance received during marriage, traceable)
- Net value at date of marriage: $8,500 (RRSP balance at marriage)

**Spouse A NFP:**
= $546,000 − $284,000 − $48,000 − $8,500 = **$205,500**

**Spouse B at date of separation:**
- Assets: $98,000 (RRSP) + $22,000 (bank) + $12,000 (vehicle) = $132,000
- Debts: $6,000 (car loan)
- Excluded property: $0
- Net value at date of marriage: $0 (no assets at marriage)

**Spouse B NFP:**
= $132,000 − $6,000 − $0 − $0 = **$126,000**

**Equalization payment:**
= ($205,500 − $126,000) ÷ 2 = **$39,750** payable by Spouse A to Spouse B
