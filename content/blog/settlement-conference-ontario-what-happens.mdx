The settlement conference is the moment in an Ontario family law case where the prospect of resolution is most real, the judicial involvement is most substantive, and the stakes of being unprepared are highest.

Most Ontario family cases settle. Research consistently shows that the majority resolve before trial. The settlement conference is often where that resolution happens — or fails to happen because one or both parties did not understand what they were walking into.

</Callout>

## What a settlement conference is

A settlement conference comes after the case conference. It is a more focused, more formal attempt at settlement. By the time a case reaches the settlement conference, both parties have disclosed their financial information, the issues are defined, and the case is ready for a serious resolution conversation.

Under Rule 17(6) of the *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99*, the purposes of a settlement conference include:

- Settling or narrowing the issues in dispute
- Ensuring the parties are prepared for trial if the case does not settle
- Exploring whether a hearing or trial can be avoided
- Encouraging the parties and their counsel to agree on facts or evidence

The most significant feature of the settlement conference — what makes it different from the case conference — is that the judge may offer a non-binding assessment of the likely outcome at trial.

---

## The non-binding assessment

At a settlement conference, a judge who has reviewed the materials may tell the parties, in plain terms, how they view the case. This is a non-binding assessment — it is not an order, it does not predetermine the outcome, and the same judge cannot conduct the trial afterward.

The value of this assessment, from a settlement perspective, is substantial. An SRL who has no experienced legal perspective on how a court might view their case can, at a settlement conference, hear directly from a judge. That assessment may confirm what the party already believed, or it may introduce information that changes the calculus.

Justice Canada's review of Canadian court data notes that self-represented litigants go to trial at higher rates than represented parties — a pattern that often reflects, at least in part, difficulty evaluating realistic settlement options. The settlement conference is the formal opportunity to get that evaluation from a judicial perspective.

<Callout type="note" title="The assessment is not binding">
  A judge's comments at a settlement conference are not a decision. If the case proceeds to trial, a different judge hears it. A judicial assessment at this stage is information — the weight to give it depends on the strength of that judge's reasoning and the facts of the case.
</Callout>

## What happens in the room

The settlement conference typically follows a similar pattern across Ontario family courts.

The judge will have read the settlement conference briefs before the conference. They may begin by summarizing the issues and asking each party to confirm their current position. They may then caucus with each party separately — meeting privately with one party, then the other — to explore settlement options without each party being required to make their positions public in front of each other.

If the parties reach a settlement or a partial settlement during the conference, the agreement may be recorded on the record as a consent order. What is agreed at a settlement conference can, with both parties' consent, be made into an enforceable court order on the spot.

If the case does not settle, the judge makes an endorsement setting out what was discussed, what remains in dispute, and what the next steps are. The case proceeds toward trial management conference and then trial.

---

## Required forms and timing

The settlement conference requires the same forms as the case conference, adapted for this stage.

**Settlement conference brief:** Each party prepares a brief setting out their current position, updated financial information, offers to settle, and a summary of what the case is about. The brief is filed according to the court's practice direction — typically six days before for the applicant and four days before for the respondent.

**Form 17F — Confirmation of Settlement Conference:** Filed three days before by each party. As with the case conference, failure to file may result in the conference being removed from the docket.

Financial statements must be current — under Rule 13 of the *Family Law Rules*, financial statements must be updated within 60 days before a conference.

---

## Offers to settle

Settlement conferences often involve formal offers to settle. Under the *Family Law Rules*, an offer to settle that is made in writing and not accepted may affect the costs award at trial. If one party makes a reasonable offer and the other refuses, and the refusing party does not do better at trial than the offer, the trial judge may order significant costs against that party under Rule 24.

This is the Rule 24 costs mechanism applied to settlement. It creates an incentive to engage seriously with settlement offers at this stage — not as a legal requirement to settle, but as a risk attached to unreasonable rejection.

---

## What to prepare

The settlement conference is where most family cases are decided — if not in the room, then as a result of what was learned there. The preparation that matters most:

- A clear, current understanding of your own financial position
- A realistic assessment of what a trial would produce on each issue
- A written settlement proposal that you have thought through
- Updated financial disclosure in compliance with the 60-day requirement
- A settlement conference brief that is specific, factual, and organized
