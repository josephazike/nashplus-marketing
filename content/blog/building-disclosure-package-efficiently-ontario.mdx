Form 13 is three pages of questions. It can take three weeks to answer correctly.

Not because the questions are hard. Because the supporting documents take time to gather, the numbers need to be internally consistent, and an error that passes at filing will surface under cross-examination at the worst possible moment. The SRL who understands what the financial statement is actually doing in the case will file a better one and negotiate from a stronger position.

This article covers what financial disclosure is for, which form applies, what supporting documents are required, the errors that appear most often, and how to stay current across the life of the case.

## What financial disclosure is actually for

The financial statement is not a formality. It is the financial foundation of the case.

Child support is calculated from the payor's income as reported on line 15000 of the income tax return. Form 13 is how that number enters the formal record. Spousal support is assessed against actual income streams, assets, and expenses. Form 13 is how those are documented. For married parties with property claims, equalization of net family property is built entirely on Form 13.1 values.

Every subsequent negotiation and court appearance is built on the financial statements filed by both parties. Gaps in the financial statement become gaps in the negotiation. Errors become credibility problems. Omissions — even innocent ones — are treated the same as concealment once they are discovered.

Rule 13 of the *Family Law Rules* establishes the full disclosure obligation. The courts treat it as one of the most significant obligations in a family proceeding. Incomplete disclosure does not stay incomplete; it becomes an issue at every subsequent stage until it is corrected.

This is the most common Form 13 error: married parties with property implications filing the support-only form because they are primarily focused on parenting or support. The form that is required is determined by what claims exist in the case, not by what you consider most important.

## The supporting documents: the real work

The form is a summary. The supporting documents make it credible and enforceable. They are what the other party and the judge can verify against.

<Checklist
  heading="Financial disclosure supporting documents"
  items={[
    "Income tax returns: last three years (Notice of Assessment preferred — it is CRA's official statement of your income and is harder to dispute than the T1 General)",
    "Pay stubs: most recent three to four stubs for salaried employees; for variable income, the last 12 months",
    "Bank statements: last three months for all accounts — chequing, savings, any account in your name",
    "Investment and retirement accounts: most recent statements for all RRSPs, TFSAs, pension accounts, brokerage or investment accounts",
    "Real property: MPAC assessment (available through mpac.ca) for assessed value; a more recent appraisal if market value differs significantly from the MPAC figure",
    "Mortgage statements: most recent statement showing the outstanding balance (used to calculate equity)",
    "Vehicle registration documents: for owned vehicles, registration showing year, make, model (value estimated from market sources)",
    "Business income (if self-employed or shareholder): corporate financial statements for last three years, corporate income tax returns, shareholder draws and dividends, any management fees paid to related parties",
    "Credit card and loan statements: most recent statements showing outstanding balances",
    "Form 13A (Certificate of Financial Disclosure): lists all supporting documents exchanged; must be filed at the case conference",
  ]}
/>

The supporting documents are not optional attachments. They are what transforms the financial statement from a self-reported declaration into a verified record. Clerks may refuse to accept a Form 13 or 13.1 that is filed without required supporting documents.

## Common errors that surface later

The following patterns appear in contested financial disclosure. They are observations about common errors, not predictions about specific cases.

**Leaving accounts off the assets list.** Investment accounts, RRSPs, or savings accounts missed through oversight. Courts treat omission the same way they treat concealment once it is discovered — the credibility consequence is the same regardless of intent.

**Using gross income where net is required, or the reverse.** The form specifies which figure is needed at each line. Using the wrong basis creates an inconsistency that surfaces in cross-examination.

**Valuing property at cost rather than current market value.** Equalization and support calculations use current market value, not what you paid. An MPAC assessment or current market appraisal is required for real property.

**Filing Form 13 when Form 13.1 is required.** The stalling effect on the case is significant. When the error is caught — at the case conference or later — it requires amending the form, regathering documents, and refiling.

**Filing without supporting documents.** The court clerk may refuse to accept it. The opposing party will note the omission. Duty counsel at the FLIC will identify this as a problem during a review.

<Callout type="warning" title="Disclosure and Rule 24">
  Under Family Law Rules, Rule 24, courts may award costs for litigation conduct that includes failures in disclosure obligations. Incomplete or misleading financial disclosure is one of the grounds on which costs orders arise in Ontario family proceedings. The obligation is ongoing, not one-time.
</Callout>

## The update obligation

Rule 13 requires updated financial statements within:

- **60 days before any conference** (case conference, settlement conference, trial management conference)
- **40 days before trial**

This is a planning trigger, not a deadline to scramble toward. The most efficient approach: build the supporting document folder completely at the start of the case. Updating means pulling new bank statements and a current pay stub, not rebuilding the package from scratch.

## Using the FLIC before filing

Before filing Form 13 or 13.1 for the first time, bring the draft to the Family Law Information Centre at your courthouse. Duty counsel, provided by Legal Aid Ontario, can review the form for completeness and identify missing supporting documents. They cannot provide ongoing representation and cannot advise on the legal implications of the numbers. But catching a missing account or a wrong income figure before filing is a quality check that most SRLs do not use.

## The SRL-strategic view

A complete, well-organized financial statement tells the other side and the judge something before negotiations begin: this person knows their numbers and can defend them. That is not a small signal. It closes off the most common line of challenge in contested financial cases and creates the conditions for a productive settlement conversation.
