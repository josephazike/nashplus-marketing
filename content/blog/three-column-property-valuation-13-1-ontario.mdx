Part 4 of Form 13.1 is the section that stops most people. Three columns, each asking for values at a different date: date of marriage, date of separation, and today. It is not immediately obvious why all three are needed, or what each column is actually being used for in the legal calculation.

They are not redundant. Each column serves a different legal purpose, and understanding that purpose changes how you approach gathering the information.

## Why Form 13.1 exists

Form 13.1 is the financial statement required when a property claim is part of the proceeding, equalization of net family property between married spouses, or a property claim between common-law partners. It is more detailed than Form 13 (which covers support-only matters) precisely because it needs to capture the full picture that the equalization or property calculation requires.

Part 4 is the core of that detail. It is where the net family property (NFP) calculation is built.

---

## The legal foundation: NFP and the valuation date

The equalization of net family property is governed by Part I of the *Family Law Act*, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3.

**Section 4(1)** of the *Family Law Act* defines net family property. For each spouse, NFP is calculated as:

```
NFP = (Assets at valuation date − Debts at valuation date − Excluded property)
     − (Net value of property owned at date of marriage)
```

The date used for the main calculation is the **valuation date**, defined in section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act* as the earliest of several triggering events, most commonly, the date of separation when there is no reasonable prospect of reconciliation.

**Section 5** establishes that the spouse with the higher NFP owes the other half the difference between their respective NFPs. This is the equalization payment.

The three columns in Part 4 are the instrument for capturing the numbers that feed this calculation.

---

## Column 1: Date of marriage (DOM)

The date-of-marriage column records the value of each asset and the balance of each debt at the time the parties were married.

Its purpose in the NFP formula is the deduction. Under section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act*, a spouse's NFP is reduced by the net value of property they owned at the date of marriage (assets minus debts at that date). This ensures that equalization only divides what was built during the marriage, not what was brought in.

**Important exception: the matrimonial home.** If a property was the matrimonial home at the date of separation, its date-of-marriage value is not deducted from the NFP calculation, even if it was owned before the marriage. The column is still completed for disclosure purposes, but the no-deduction rule means it does not reduce that spouse's NFP. This is the most significant exception in Part 4, and it is covered in detail in the article on [the matrimonial home in Ontario](/blog/matrimonial-home-ontario-explained).

**For common-law property claims.** Where the property claim arises from a common-law relationship rather than a marriage, the relevant starting date is typically the date cohabitation began, not a date of marriage. The form's structure accommodates this.

---

## Column 2: Date of separation (DOS)

The date-of-separation column records the value of each asset and the balance of each debt at the valuation date, the date of separation under section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act*.

This is the column that drives the equalization calculation. The NFP formula uses the DOS values as the primary snapshot: what did each spouse own and owe when the marriage ended? The difference between this column and the DOM column (net of the DOM deduction) produces each spouse's NFP. The difference between the two spouses' NFPs, divided by two, is the equalization payment owed.

Getting the DOS values right matters more than any other column. These are the numbers the court uses.

**Disputed valuation dates.** Sometimes parties disagree on the date of separation. This factual dispute can itself require judicial resolution, because the chosen date changes which asset values and debt balances appear in the DOS column. Where the date is disputed, the DOS column may require alternative scenarios or an agreed interim date pending resolution.

---

## Column 3: Today

The today column records the current value of each asset and the current balance of each debt at the time the financial statement is prepared and sworn.

This column does not feed the NFP equalization calculation directly. Its purpose is disclosure: parties are required to provide a current picture of their financial situation, not just a historical snapshot. Courts use the today column to assess credibility, identify changes between the DOS date and the present, and understand whether assets have been depleted or transferred since separation.

**Why today was added.** The disclosure obligation in family proceedings is ongoing. A financial statement that only showed DOS and DOM values would leave the court without current information about the party's financial state at the time of the proceeding. The today column ensures that each party's current financial reality is on the record.

**Today values and legal proceedings.** If the today value of an asset differs significantly from the DOS value, because the asset was sold, because a market changed, or because money was spent, that difference will be visible and may require explanation. Significant decreases in asset values after separation can prompt questions about whether property was deliberately depleted.

## When DOM values are unavailable or disputed

Establishing what an asset was worth at the date of marriage, often a decade or more before the proceeding, can be genuinely difficult. Common situations:

**Bank accounts.** Banks retain records for a limited period. Historical statements may require a formal request and a fee, and may not be available beyond a certain point. If a statement is unavailable, the party may need to use alternative evidence: CRA records, loan applications from that period, or a sworn statement explaining why the value cannot be established with documentation.

**Real property.** A historical property value can sometimes be reconstructed using MPAC assessment records, which have historical data, or by retaining an appraiser to provide a retrospective value.

**RRSPs and pensions.** Plan administrators and financial institutions often have historical records. Pension plan administrators can provide a valuation at the date of marriage on request, though timelines vary.

**Corporate interests.** Where a spouse held shares in a closely held company at the date of marriage, a business valuator may be needed to provide a retrospective value. Corporate financial statements from that period, if they exist, are the starting point.

Where a value cannot be established, the form should note this with an explanation. Guessing is not appropriate on a sworn document.

## Complex asset categories

Some assets require more than a number.

**Closely held corporations.** If a spouse has an ownership interest in a private company, shares in a holding company, an operating company, or a professional corporation, the value of those shares must be included at DOS. Business valuation is one of the most contested areas in property proceedings. Certified business valuators typically provide opinions using an asset approach, income approach, or market approach, and competing valuations from experts retained by each party are common in disputed cases. The form requires the best available estimate; the dispute over methodology comes later.

**Defined benefit pensions.** These require a formal valuation from the pension plan administrator. Processing times vary. Requesting the valuation early in the proceeding is worth doing because it is often the longest-lead-time item in the disclosure package.

**Bare trust property.** Where an asset is held in trust or through a nominee structure, the beneficial owner's interest, not the nominal legal title, is what the disclosure captures. The form requires disclosure of beneficial interests, not just registered ownership.

Form 13.1 Part 4 does not exist in isolation. The matrimonial home rules affect how the DOM column is applied to the family home. Excluded property listed in Schedule B affects the NFP total. And the NFP outputs from both parties' Form 13.1s are what determine the equalization calculation.
