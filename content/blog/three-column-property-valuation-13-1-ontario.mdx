Part 3 (Assets) and Part 4 (Debts and other liabilities) of Form 13.1 require you to enter values at three different points in time. Not all of those columns are used for the same purpose. Filling in the wrong values in the wrong columns — or leaving columns blank because their purpose is unclear — produces an incorrect equalization calculation.

This article explains what each column is for, why all three are required, and how to approach gathering the information.

## Why the DOS column does the equalization work

The *Family Law Act* section 4(1) defines the valuation date as the date the parties separated, in most cases. Section 5(1) directs that each spouse is entitled to equalization of the net family property accumulated during the marriage — measured at the valuation date.

That is why the DOS column is the operative column. The equalization calculation is a snapshot of each party's financial position at the moment of separation: assets owned on that date, debts owed on that date, less the starting-point wealth brought to the marriage, less excluded property.

The value of an asset today — higher or lower than at separation — does not change the equalization obligation. An RRSP worth $60,000 at separation and $75,000 today enters the NFP at $60,000.

---

## Why the DOM column matters

The date-of-marriage deduction is the mechanism that limits equalization to wealth accumulated during the marriage. Without it, a spouse who brought a $200,000 investment portfolio to the marriage would be equalizing that wealth as if it were created by the marriage — which would be inequitable.

The DOM column establishes the net value of what each spouse owned at the start of the marriage. The formula subtracts that net value from the DOS column result, leaving only the marital accumulation.

<Callout type="warning" title="The matrimonial home exception to the DOM deduction">
  The date-of-marriage deduction does not apply to the matrimonial home. A spouse who owned the matrimonial home before the marriage cannot deduct its DOM value. The home's full DOS value enters the NFP without any offset. See the article on the matrimonial home for a full explanation.
</Callout>

Two practical challenges arise with the DOM column:

**The data may not exist.** Bank statements from a decade or more ago may not be readily available. Reconstruction from other sources — CRA records, historical investment statements, old mortgage documentation — may be required.

**Zero is the floor.** Section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act* provides that the net value at the date of marriage cannot be less than zero. A spouse who entered the marriage with more debt than assets cannot reduce their NFP below what the DOS column produces. The deduction is capped at zero.

## Why the "today" column is required

The "today" column does not affect the equalization calculation. Its purpose is disclosure completeness.

A form sworn today reflects the current state of affairs. The court and the opposing party are entitled to know: are the assets that existed at separation still there? Have debts been paid down? Have new assets been acquired or new debts taken on?

The "today" column also matters for support determinations, which are forward-looking. Spousal support addresses current and future need. Child support is calculated from current income and expenses. The "today" snapshot provides the financial context for those calculations.

Where assets have been sold, transferred, or materially changed since the date of separation, the "today" column highlights that change. Courts pay attention to significant disparities between the DOS and "today" values, particularly where assets have been transferred or depleted.

## When values are estimated or uncertain

Not every DOS value can be established with a bank statement. Where documentation no longer exists for the date of marriage or date of separation, parties may use:

- CRA records (T4 slips, RRSP contribution records, investment income slips)
- Pension plan administrator statements for past valuations
- Historical real property assessments from MPAC
- Declarations based on best available recollection, noted as estimated

Form 13.1 permits estimated values where exact documentation is unavailable, but estimated values are subject to challenge. The more documentation that supports a value, the less vulnerable it is.
