A final family law order is not automatically revisable when life changes. Courts make final orders to create stability. The Family Law Rules recognize that circumstances do change — but to open a final order for reconsideration, the change must meet a legal threshold.

That threshold is a **material change in circumstances**.

Understanding what counts as a material change — and what does not — is essential before investing time and money in a variation proceeding.

## What a motion to change is

A motion to change is a procedural mechanism under Rule 15 of the *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99* that allows a party to ask the court to vary, suspend, or terminate a final order in a family law proceeding.

For orders under the *Divorce Act*, the authority to vary comes from section 17 of the *Divorce Act*. For orders under the *Family Law Act*, section 37 of the FLA governs variation of support orders.

The motion to change is not a new proceeding. It is a step within the existing proceeding, using the same court file number, the same continuing record, and the same court where the original order was made.

---

## The material change threshold

Not every change in circumstances justifies reopening a final order. The legal test is a **material change in circumstances** — a change that, had it been known at the time the original order was made, would likely have produced a different order.

The change must be:

- **Real.** Not anticipated, not speculative, and not a normal fluctuation in an already-variable situation.
- **Significant.** Not a minor shift but a change of sufficient weight that it affects the basis on which the order was made.
- **Unforeseen.** If the change was anticipated when the order was made — for example, a job change that both parties knew was coming — it may not qualify as a material change.

Courts assess this on the evidence. The party seeking the variation bears the burden of establishing that a material change has occurred.

## What can be varied

The most common subjects of a motion to change are:

**Child support.** A significant change in the payor's income — upward or downward — is the most common ground for a child support variation. Under the *Divorce Act* section 17 and the *Family Law Act* section 37, changes to income that affect the Federal Child Support Guidelines amount are a recognized basis for variation.

**Spousal support.** A material change in either party's financial circumstances — job loss, remarriage, significant increase in earnings, a health change affecting earning capacity — may justify a variation of a spousal support order.

**Parenting arrangements.** A material change in circumstances affecting the child's best interests can justify a variation of a parenting order. Courts are careful here: the desire to spend more time with a child, or dissatisfaction with the existing arrangement, is not itself a material change. Something must have genuinely changed in the child's circumstances or the parents' ability to meet the child's needs.

## Consent variations

If both parties agree to the variation, the process can be considerably simpler. Parties who agree to new terms can prepare a consent order setting out the varied terms and file it for the court's approval. The court reviews the proposed consent order — particularly parenting arrangements, where the best interests of the child must still be satisfied — and may approve it without a full hearing.

---

## The Rule 24 costs risk

A motion to change filed without meeting the material change threshold — or filed as a litigation tactic rather than based on genuine changed circumstances — can attract a costs order under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*. Courts have denied variation motions and awarded costs where the moving party failed to establish a genuine material change, or where the motion was found to be unreasonable in the circumstances.

Before filing a motion to change, understanding whether the circumstances genuinely meet the threshold is the most important assessment a party can make.
