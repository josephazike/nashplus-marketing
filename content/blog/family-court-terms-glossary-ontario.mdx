Family law proceedings in Ontario come with their own vocabulary. This glossary defines the terms you are most likely to encounter, in plain language, with a citation to the statute or rule that uses each term. Where a deeper article exists, there is a link.

This is a reference document. If a term appears in a court document and you are not sure what it means, start here.

<Callout type="legal">
  This glossary provides legal information about Ontario family law terminology. It is not legal advice. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed Ontario lawyer.
</Callout>

---

## A

**Affidavit**
A written statement of facts, sworn or affirmed under oath before a commissioner for taking oaths. Affidavits are the primary way parties place evidence before the court in family proceedings. The person who swears the affidavit is called the deponent. Affidavits must be based on personal knowledge, with hearsay information (things you were told by others) clearly identified. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 14.*

**Affidavit of service**
A sworn document confirming that another document was served, setting out who served it, how, when, and where. Required after serving most court documents. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 6(18).*

**Answer**
The document a respondent files in response to an application. It sets out which claims the respondent agrees with, which they dispute, and any counter-claims the respondent is making. The deadline to file is 30 days from service in Ontario, or 60 days if served outside Ontario. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 10.*

**Applicant**
A person who starts an application in family court. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 2(1).* See: [what do "applicant" and "respondent" mean in Ontario family court?](/blog/applicant-vs-respondent-family-court-ontario)

**Application**
The document that formally starts a family law proceeding. Most family cases begin with Form 8 (general application) or Form 8A (application for divorce). Filing the application opens the court file and triggers the obligation to serve the respondent. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 8.*

## B

**Best interests of the child**
The overriding legal standard courts apply when making any decision about parenting time, decision-making responsibility, or contact with a child. Both the *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), s. 16(1)* and the *Children's Law Reform Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.12, s. 24* require courts to consider only the child's best interests. The statutes list specific factors courts must consider; neither parent's interests are part of the test. See: [best interests of the child in Ontario](/blog/best-interests-of-the-child-ontario)

## C

**Case conference**
The first substantive court event in most Ontario family law proceedings. A judge meets with both parties (and their lawyers, if any) to identify the issues in dispute, explore settlement, and set a plan for what happens next. A case conference brief (Form 17A) must be filed at least six days before the conference. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 17.*

**Child support**
Financial support paid by one parent to another for the benefit of their children following separation. For most cases, child support is calculated using the *Federal Child Support Guidelines, SOR/97-175*, based on the paying parent's income and the number of children. Special expenses (section 7 expenses) are shared in proportion to income. See: [child support in Ontario: the guidelines, the tables, and the exceptions](/blog/child-support-guidelines-ontario)

**Conditional order**
A court order that becomes effective only when a specified condition is met. A divorce judgment is typically a conditional order: it becomes final 31 days after it is pronounced, provided no appeal is filed. *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), s. 12.*

**Consent order**
A court order that both parties have agreed to. The court reviews and signs it, giving it the same legal force as any other court order. Also called an order on consent. Consent orders can be made at any stage of a proceeding.

**Continuing record**
The official, bound court file maintained throughout a family law proceeding. The applicant is responsible for creating it. It has two volumes: an endorsement volume (yellow cover) for judicial endorsements and orders, and a documents volume (red cover) for applications, financial statements, affidavits, and motions. Both parties are responsible for adding their documents to the record in the correct format. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 9.* See: [organizing your family court documents in Ontario](/blog/organizing-family-court-documents-ontario)

**Costs**
A court order requiring one party to pay some or all of the other's legal expenses or out-of-pocket costs related to the proceeding. Costs can be awarded after a motion, after a conference, or at the end of a trial. Courts consider which party was more reasonable in their conduct and offers to settle. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 24.*

**Custody (historical term)**

<Callout type="note" title="This term was replaced in 2021">
  Under the *Divorce Act*, "custody" was replaced by "decision-making responsibility" and "parenting time" on March 1, 2021. Courts no longer make custody orders under the *Divorce Act*. Older orders that use the word "custody" remain in effect. The *Children's Law Reform Act* uses its own terminology for non-divorcing parties.
</Callout>

The former legal term for who a child lived with and who held authority over major decisions about the child's life. "Sole custody" meant one parent held decision-making authority; "joint custody" meant both parents shared it. The 2021 *Divorce Act* amendments replaced this framework entirely. See: [decision-making responsibility and parenting time in Ontario](/blog/decision-making-responsibility-parenting-time-ontario)

## D

**Decision-making responsibility**
The legal authority to make significant decisions about a child's life, including decisions about education, health care, religion, and extracurricular activities. Replaced "custody" in proceedings under the *Divorce Act* as of March 1, 2021. Can be allocated to one parent (sole) or shared between both (joint). *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), s. 16.1.* See: [decision-making responsibility and parenting time in Ontario](/blog/decision-making-responsibility-parenting-time-ontario)

**Dependant**
A person to whom another owes a legal obligation of support. Children and spouses can qualify as dependants under the *Family Law Act*. The Act defines "dependant" in section 1(1) as a person to whom another has an obligation of support under Part III of the Act. *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3, s. 1(1).*

**Disclosure**
The mandatory exchange of financial information between parties in proceedings involving support or property claims. Both parties must serve and file a sworn financial statement (Form 13 or Form 13.1) and supporting documents. The disclosure obligation is ongoing: material changes to financial circumstances after filing must be updated. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 13.* See: [financial disclosure in Ontario family law](/blog/financial-disclosure-family-law)

**Divorce**
The legal dissolution of a marriage, granted by the court. In Canada, divorce is governed exclusively by the federal *Divorce Act*. The most common ground is that the spouses have lived separate and apart for at least one year. No fault is required. *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), s. 8.*

**Duty counsel**
A lawyer funded by Legal Aid Ontario who provides brief, on-the-day legal help at the courthouse. Duty counsel can review documents, answer procedural questions, and assist self-represented parties at case conferences and settlement conferences. Income eligibility applies; contact the LAO Contact Centre at 1-800-668-8258 before your court date to check eligibility. See: [what Legal Aid Ontario covers in family law](/blog/what-legal-aid-ontario-covers-family-law)

## E

**Equalization**
Ontario's mechanism for dividing wealth accumulated during a marriage. Each spouse's net family property is calculated separately. The spouse with the higher net family property owes the other half the difference. The right to equalization is created by *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3, s. 5* and applies to married spouses only. See: [what is equalization in Ontario family law?](/blog/equalization-explained-ontario)

## F

**Family Law Information Centre (FLIC)**
A staffed resource available at every Ontario family courthouse. FLIC provides general information about the family law process, help with identifying and understanding forms, and referrals to mediation, duty counsel, and other services. FLICs provide legal information, not legal advice. See: [Family Law Information Centres in Ontario](/blog/family-law-information-centres-flic-ontario)

**Financial statement**
A sworn document disclosing a party's income, expenses, assets, and debts. Required in most proceedings involving support or property claims. Form 13 is used for support claims only; Form 13.1 is used when property division is also at issue. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 13.* See: [understanding Form 13 in Ontario](/blog/understanding-form-13-ontario)

**Form 13 / Form 13A / Form 13.1**
Three related financial disclosure forms used in Ontario family proceedings.
- **Form 13** (Financial Statement, Support Claims): for proceedings where only support is claimed.
- **Form 13A** (Certificate of Financial Disclosure): a companion document confirming that required supporting documents have been exchanged.
- **Form 13.1** (Financial Statement, Property and Support Claims): for proceedings where property division is also at issue. It includes three date columns: the date of marriage, the valuation date, and today's date.
*Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 13.*

## I

**Imputed income**
Income attributed to a party by the court in excess of what they actually reported, where the court finds the party is deliberately earning less than their capacity. Used primarily in child support calculations. *Federal Child Support Guidelines, SOR/97-175, s. 19.* See: [what is imputed income in Ontario family court?](/blog/imputed-income-family-court-ontario)

---

## J

**Joint family venture**
An equitable doctrine developed by the Supreme Court of Canada in *Kerr v. Baranow, 2011 SCC 10* that allows a common-law partner to claim a share of property accumulated through a shared enterprise where it would be unjust for one party to retain it all. It is not a statutory right under the *Family Law Act*; it is an unjust enrichment claim. Unlike married spouses, common-law partners do not have equalization rights.

---

## M

**Matrimonial home**
The property ordinarily occupied by both spouses as their family residence at the time of separation. The matrimonial home is treated differently from all other property under the *Family Law Act*: both spouses have equal rights of possession during the marriage regardless of whose name is on title, neither spouse can sell or mortgage it without the other's written consent, and its full value at the valuation date is included in net family property with no deduction for its pre-marriage value. *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3, ss. 18-21.* See: [the matrimonial home in Ontario: equalization and possession](/blog/matrimonial-home-ontario-explained)

**Motion**
A request to the court for an order during the course of a proceeding, used to resolve issues before the full case is heard. A motion typically requires a notice of motion and a supporting affidavit. Urgent motions (such as requests for emergency orders protecting children or property) can be brought without notice in exceptional circumstances. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 14.*

**Motion to change**
A formal court process to vary an existing order because there has been a material change in circumstances since the order was made. Used to change child support, spousal support, or parenting arrangements. The party asking for the change must establish that a material change has occurred before the court will consider whether to modify the order. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 15.*

## N

**Net family property (NFP)**
The value of all property a spouse owns on the valuation date, minus debts and liabilities, minus excluded property under section 4(2) of the *Family Law Act*, minus the net value of property owned at the date of marriage. NFP is calculated separately for each spouse and used to determine the equalization payment. *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3, s. 4.* See: [net family property in Ontario](/blog/net-family-property-ontario)

---

## O

**Office of the Children's Lawyer (OCL)**
A provincial office that may be appointed by the court to represent a child's interests or to conduct an assessment of the child's views and preferences in a family proceeding. The OCL's involvement requires a court order or referral; it is not automatic. *Children's Law Reform Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.12.* See: [the Office of the Children's Lawyer in Ontario](/blog/office-of-the-childrens-lawyer-ontario)

---

## P

**Parenting time**
The time a child spends with each parent under a parenting arrangement or order. Replaced "access" in proceedings under the *Divorce Act* as of March 1, 2021. The parent exercising parenting time is entitled to make day-to-day decisions for the child during that time. *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), s. 2(1).* See: [decision-making responsibility and parenting time in Ontario](/blog/decision-making-responsibility-parenting-time-ontario)

---

## R

**Respondent**
A person against whom a claim is made in an application, answer, or appeal. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 2(1).* See: [what do "applicant" and "respondent" mean in Ontario family court?](/blog/applicant-vs-respondent-family-court-ontario)

**Restraining order**
A court order prohibiting a person from contacting another person, attending at a specified location, or engaging in specified conduct. In family law, restraining orders are available under *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3, s. 46* and *Children's Law Reform Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.12, s. 35.* Restraining orders under the Family Law Act can be enforced by police and carry consequences if breached.

---

## S

**Self-represented litigant (SRL)**
A party to a family law proceeding who is not represented by a lawyer. Also referred to as an unrepresented or self-acting party. The majority of Ontario family court appearances involve at least one self-represented party. See: [representing yourself in Ontario family court](/blog/representing-yourself-family-court-ontario), [guide for self-represented litigants in Ontario](/blog/self-represented-litigants-ontario-guide)

**Separation**
The end of cohabitation between spouses, with at least one spouse having formed the intention to live separate and apart permanently. There is no document to file and no legal process to trigger: separation is a factual state, not a legal status. After one year of separation, either married spouse may apply for divorce under the *Divorce Act*. The date of separation is also, in most cases, the valuation date for equalization purposes under the *Family Law Act*.

**Service**
The formal delivery of court documents to another party according to rules governing how, by whom, and within what time frame. Different documents have different service requirements: some must be served personally; others may be served by mail or email. Proof of service is required in the form of an affidavit of service. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 6.*

**Settlement conference**
A court event, typically following a case conference, at which a judge actively helps the parties work toward resolution. More directive than a case conference: the judge may provide a frank assessment of how a matter is likely to be decided at trial. If the case does not settle, the settlement conference judge does not hear the trial. *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, r. 17.*

**Spousal support**
Financial support paid by one spouse to another following separation or divorce. It is not automatic: a claim for spousal support requires establishing entitlement based on compensatory, non-compensatory, or contractual grounds. Both the *Divorce Act* (for divorcing married spouses) and the *Family Law Act* (for married spouses not seeking divorce) govern spousal support in Ontario.

---

## V

**Valuation date**
The date at which assets and debts are measured for the equalization calculation. Defined in *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3, s. 4(1)* as the earliest of several specified dates; in most cases it is the date of separation. See: [what is a valuation date in Ontario family law?](/blog/valuation-date-family-law-ontario)

---

## W

**Without prejudice**
A label applied to settlement communications or offers indicating that they cannot be introduced as evidence against the party who made them in a court proceeding. Parties use "without prejudice" to encourage candid negotiation without the risk that their concessions or proposals will be used against them if the case proceeds to a hearing. Courts enforce the without-prejudice protection seriously.
