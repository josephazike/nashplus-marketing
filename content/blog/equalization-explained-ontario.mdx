When a marriage ends in Ontario, property is not automatically split down the middle by title or ownership. Instead, the law measures what each spouse accumulated during the marriage and, if one spouse accumulated more, requires a payment to the other. That payment is called equalization.

## The legal right

The right to equalization is created by section 5(1) of the *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3*. It provides that when a marriage ends, the spouse whose net family property is the lesser of the two has a right to payment from the other spouse of half the difference between them.

Two things about this right matter enormously.

First, it applies to married spouses only. Common-law partners do not have equalization rights under the *Family Law Act*. The property regime for common-law partners is entirely different, and in most cases far less protective. If you were not married, this provision does not apply to you.

Second, equalization is a claim that must be made. It does not happen automatically. The limitation period is six years from the date of separation or two years from the date of divorce, whichever comes first.

---

## Net family property: the measure behind equalization

Equalization is calculated using each spouse's **net family property (NFP)**: the net value of what that spouse owned and accumulated during the marriage. Section 4 of the *Family Law Act* defines net family property as, in simplified terms, the value of all property a spouse owned on the valuation date, minus debts, minus excluded property, minus the net value they owned at the date of marriage.

The result for each spouse is calculated separately. Then:

> Equalization payment = (Higher NFP minus Lower NFP) divided by 2

The spouse with the higher NFP owes that amount to the other.

---

## What equalization is not

Equalization is a debt, not a transfer of title. The law does not automatically re-register property in either spouse's name. What it creates is a payment obligation. How that payment is made is a separate question: cash, a transfer of a specific asset, an offset against other claims, or a structured arrangement.

It is also not always equal in outcome. Section 5(6) of the *Family Law Act* permits the court to order an amount other than half the difference if an equal division would be unconscionable. That is a high threshold; courts apply it in exceptional circumstances only.
