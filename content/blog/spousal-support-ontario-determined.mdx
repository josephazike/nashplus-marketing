Spousal support in Ontario is not calculated by a single formula applied the same way in every case. Two different statutes can apply, depending on whether a divorce is part of the proceeding. Courts reference a set of guidelines that are persuasive but not binding. And the factors courts weigh are deliberately broad, giving judges meaningful discretion. Understanding how all of this fits together is the starting point for making sense of any spousal support claim.

## Two governing frameworks

Whether a divorce is involved determines which statute governs the spousal support claim.

**If a divorce is sought**, the governing legislation is federal: the *Divorce Act*, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.). Section 15.2 of the *Divorce Act* gives courts the authority to make spousal support orders in divorce proceedings. Only the Superior Court of Justice can grant a divorce and make orders under the *Divorce Act*.

**If no divorce is sought**, where married spouses are separating without pursuing a divorce, or where proceedings involve other family law claims between married spouses, spousal support is governed by Part III of the *Family Law Act*, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3. Section 33 of the *Family Law Act* sets out who may apply for support and the framework for determining it.

Common-law partners seeking spousal-type support from each other also use the *Family Law Act* (s. 29 defines "spouse" for Part III purposes to include common-law partners who have cohabited continuously for at least three years, or in a relationship of some permanence if they have a child together).

## What courts consider: the FLA s. 33 factors

Section 33(9) of the *Family Law Act* sets out the factors a court considers when determining whether to award support and in what amount. These include:

- The dependant's and respondent's current assets, means, and earning capacity
- Assets and means likely to be available in the future
- The dependant's capacity to become self-supporting, and the time needed to do so
- The standard of living the parties enjoyed during the relationship
- Any legal obligation the respondent has to support another person
- Contributions the dependant made to the respondent's career or career potential
- Reasonable needs, having regard to the level of support the dependant is accustomed to
- A course of conduct that is so unconscionable as to constitute an obvious and gross repudiation of the relationship (which may reduce or eliminate a support obligation)

These factors reflect two broad purposes that run through spousal support law: compensating a spouse for economic disadvantage arising from the relationship or its breakdown, and addressing genuine financial need.

## What courts consider: the Divorce Act s. 15.2 objectives

Section 15.2(6) of the *Divorce Act* sets out the objectives for spousal support orders in divorce proceedings:

- Recognize economic advantages or disadvantages arising from the marriage or its breakdown
- Apportion between the spouses financial consequences arising from the care of any child of the marriage
- Relieve economic hardship arising from the breakdown of the marriage
- Promote the economic self-sufficiency of each spouse within a reasonable period of time, to the extent practicable

Courts balance these objectives, which sometimes pull in different directions. A short marriage with no children may produce a strong self-sufficiency objective and a time-limited order. A long marriage where one spouse stepped back from employment to raise children may produce compensatory support with no fixed end date.

## The Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines

The Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines (SSAG) were prepared by Professor Carol Rogerson and Professor Rollie Thompson for the Department of Justice Canada. They are not law. They are not binding on courts. They are a reference tool that Ontario courts use regularly, and they have become an important part of how spousal support is negotiated and argued.

<Callout type="warning" title="The SSAG is not law">
  The SSAG is a persuasive reference, not a legal formula. A court can depart from the SSAG ranges where the circumstances call for it. The guidelines themselves identify exceptions and situations where the standard formulas do not apply well. Using SSAG ranges as a starting point for negotiation is reasonable; treating them as a guaranteed outcome is not.
</Callout>

The SSAG provides two main formulas:

**The "without child" formula** applies where the spouses do not have children together or child support is not a factor. It calculates a range (low, mid, and high) for both the quantum of support and its duration, based primarily on the difference in the spouses' gross incomes and the length of the marriage.

**The "with child" formula** applies where child support is also being paid. It uses a different calculation because child support obligations affect the support payor's available income. The formula is more complex and the ranges tend to be higher for the support recipient.

The SSAG low/mid/high range is where most of the negotiation happens in practice. The statutory factors and objectives determine which end of the range is appropriate, or whether the case falls outside the range altogether.

## Entitlement before quantum

Courts address entitlement before turning to quantum or duration. These are separate questions.

**Entitlement** asks: is this a case where one spouse has a recognized legal basis to receive support from the other? Courts consider the length of the relationship, the roles each party played, the economic positions of each party after separation, and any economic interdependency that arose during the relationship. A party who would be self-sufficient without support, and who did not sacrifice career or financial independence during the marriage, may not be entitled to support even if there is an income gap.

**Quantum** asks: if entitlement is established, how much should the support be? This is where the SSAG ranges become most directly relevant.

**Duration** asks: for how long should support be paid? Time-limited orders require the recipient to work toward self-sufficiency within a defined period. Indefinite orders, which are not the same as permanent orders, leave the end date open but allow either party to return to court to vary the order as circumstances change.

## Variation of support

Support orders are not always final. Where there has been a material change in circumstances, a change in either party's income, a change in need, the recipient becoming self-sufficient, or other significant changes, either party may apply to vary the order.

A written separation agreement that sets spousal support can also be varied by the court in some circumstances where the agreement was made without full financial disclosure or where the statutory threshold for variation is met.

## Connecting the broader picture

Spousal support claims require financial disclosure regardless of which statute applies. The financial statement filed is Form 13 for support-only proceedings, or Form 13.1 if property claims are also in issue.
