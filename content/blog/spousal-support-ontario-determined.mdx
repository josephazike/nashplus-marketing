Spousal support is not automatic. It is not determined by a single formula. And the document that most practitioners use to calculate amounts — the Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines — is not law.

Understanding how spousal support is actually determined requires understanding three separate questions, asked in order: Is there an entitlement to support? If so, how much? And for how long?

Each question has its own analysis. Courts work through them sequentially.

## Two legal frameworks, depending on the parties

The governing legislation depends on who is claiming support.

**For married parties proceeding under the *Divorce Act***, spousal support orders are made under section 15.2 of the *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.)*. Section 15.2(4) lists the objectives a court must try to achieve, including recognizing each spouse's contribution, promoting economic self-sufficiency, and addressing economic advantages or disadvantages arising from the marriage or its breakdown.

**For non-divorcing married parties and common-law partners**, support claims proceed under Part III of the *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3*. Section 33 of the FLA sets out the factors courts must consider, including the dependant's means and needs, the respondent's ability to pay, the duration of cohabitation, the role each party played during the relationship, and the effect of the caring responsibilities each party has assumed.

The two frameworks are similar in structure and produce similar analyses, but they are distinct statutory regimes. A claim under the *Divorce Act* is a federal matter; a claim under the *Family Law Act* is provincial.

## Step 1: Entitlement

Before any amount is discussed, the claiming party must establish entitlement to spousal support.

Ontario courts have recognized three grounds for spousal support entitlement: compensatory, non-compensatory, and contractual.

**Compensatory entitlement** arises where one spouse made economic sacrifices or contributions that benefited the other or the family unit — career interruptions, reduced earning capacity, unpaid domestic work. The idea is that the marriage created an economic disadvantage that should be addressed on breakdown.

**Non-compensatory entitlement** addresses need, independent of economic sacrifice. Where the marriage created a degree of financial dependence, the higher-earning spouse may have an obligation to support the other during the transition to self-sufficiency, regardless of whether any specific sacrifice can be identified.

**Contractual entitlement** arises from a domestic contract (separation agreement, cohabitation agreement) that addresses support.

A party who cannot establish entitlement on one of these grounds will not receive spousal support, regardless of the income disparity between the parties.

## Step 2: Amount and duration — the SSAG

Once entitlement is established, courts turn to amount and duration.

<Callout type="warning" title="The SSAG is not law">
  The Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines are not legislation and are not binding on Ontario courts. They were developed by two family law professors — Carol Rogerson of the University of Toronto and Rollie Thompson of Dalhousie Law School — with funding from the federal Department of Justice. Courts frequently refer to the SSAG ranges, and parties typically negotiate within or around them, but a court may depart from the SSAG where the circumstances justify it.
</Callout>

The SSAG provides two formula tracks:

**The Without Child Support formula** applies where there are no dependent children in the picture. Support amount is expressed as a percentage of the income difference between the spouses, multiplied by the duration of the marriage. The ranges are expressed as low, mid, and high.

**The With Child Support formula** applies where child support is being paid simultaneously. The calculation is more complex because child support is paid first, and spousal support is calculated on net disposable incomes after child support.

For both formulas, the SSAG produces a range — a low, mid, and high for both amount and duration. The range is not a single answer; it is a zone within which the parties negotiate or the court decides.

The question of where within the range an appropriate amount falls — and that range can be significant — is where most of the substantive dispute lives.

## Duration

Duration of spousal support depends on the length of the cohabitation, the age of the parties, and whether there are dependent children.

The SSAG provides duration ranges correlated with the length of the relationship and the entitlement formula. For long marriages, the upper end of the duration range may be indefinite (no set end date), which does not mean permanent — it means the obligation continues until varied or terminated by agreement or court order.

Under both the *Divorce Act* and the *Family Law Act*, either party can apply to vary a support order where there has been a material change in circumstances.

---

## Variation

A spousal support order is not permanently fixed. Under the *Divorce Act* s.17 and the *Family Law Act* s.37, either party can apply for a variation where there has been a material change in circumstances since the order was made.

Material changes that may justify variation include:

- A significant change in either party's income
- Cohabitation or remarriage of the support recipient
- The passage of a defined review date in the order itself
- A significant health change affecting earning capacity

The party seeking variation bears the burden of establishing the material change.

## The role of a lawyer in a spousal support claim
