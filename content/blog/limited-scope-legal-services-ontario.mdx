Most people assume hiring a lawyer means handing over the whole case. It does not have to. Ontario law explicitly recognizes a model where a lawyer handles a single document, a specific hearing, or a strategy call — and nothing else. This model has a formal name: a limited scope retainer.

This article explains what limited scope services are, the four types available in Ontario, when each one changes outcomes most, and how to engage one correctly.

  This article provides legal information about limited scope legal services in Ontario. It is not legal advice. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed Ontario lawyer.
</Callout>

## What the Law Society says about limited scope retainers

The Law Society of Ontario formally recognizes limited scope (unbundled) retainers. Under this model:

- The scope of the lawyer's work is defined and limited in writing in the retainer letter
- The lawyer is professionally responsible only for the specific task they were retained to do
- The client handles everything outside that defined scope

This is not informal. It is a governed professional engagement with defined obligations on both sides. The lawyer who reviews your financial statement is not your lawyer for anything else — not your court appearance, not your negotiations, not your conference brief — unless those tasks are separately retained.

That clarity matters. It is also what makes the model usable for SRLs on a specific budget.

Each type addresses a different gap. A strategic consultation gives you a map. Document review catches errors before they become credibility problems. Coaching prepares you for what happens in court. ## When each type changes outcomes most

**Strategic consultations** matter most at the beginning of a case and before a settlement conference. An SRL who overestimates their legal position tends to decline settlements a lawyer would have advised accepting. One who underestimates it may settle for less than the law provides. One hour with a family lawyer who can map your specific facts against how Ontario courts decide cases like yours is rarely wasted.

**Document review** matters most for any document that is difficult to correct after it is filed. Financial statements are sworn documents — errors in them do not stay errors at filing. They surface under cross-examination. An affidavit filed with unsupported claims damages credibility before you have said a word. A draft order signed without review can create enforcement problems that require a return to court to fix.

**Coaching** matters most when the case is complex, approaching trial, or when the other party has recently retained counsel. The dynamic of a case changes when one side has a lawyer and the other does not. That is not the moment to be without professional guidance on how to present and respond.

**Limited appearances** matter most at procedural motions and trial management conferences. These hearings turn on legal argument and procedural rules. A lawyer who appears for one specific hearing — at a flat fee or hourly rate — can handle that moment without a full retainer.

## How to find a limited-scope lawyer in Ontario

Three sources cover most of the field:

- **Law Society of Ontario lawyer directory.** Available at [lso.ca](https://lso.ca). Filter by family law. Ask any family lawyer directly: "Do you offer limited-scope or unbundled services?" Many do; not all advertise it prominently.
- **JusticeNet.** Reduced-fee legal services for middle-income Ontarians who do not qualify for Legal Aid Ontario. Income-tested. Available at [justicenet.ca](https://justicenet.ca).
- **Family Law Information Centre (FLIC) at your local courthouse.** Duty counsel, provided by Legal Aid Ontario, are available to answer questions and review documents on the day of your appearance. Not ongoing representation, but not nothing.

<Callout type="note" title="LAO eligibility">
  Legal Aid Ontario family duty counsel is available at no cost to those who meet the income threshold (net annual income approximately $45,440 for families up to four, asset threshold $15,000, as of March 3, 2025). Call LAO at 1-800-668-8258 to confirm eligibility before your court date.
</Callout>

## What the retainer letter must say

When a lawyer is retained for a limited scope task, a retainer letter governs the engagement. Before signing it, the article below describes what it should contain. That is not legal advice. It is a description of a document you will be asked to sign.

A limited scope retainer letter should specify:

- Exactly what the lawyer is retained to do (the defined scope)
- What they are not retained to do
- What happens if the scope needs to expand and whether additional retainer is required
- The fee structure for the defined scope
- How the engagement ends

The absence of any of these elements is worth asking about before the session begins.

## What limited scope does not cover

A coaching lawyer who helped you prepare for a settlement conference is not your lawyer at that conference unless separately retained for it. If you appear at that conference, you appear as a self-represented litigant. The judge, the opposing party, and the court record will reflect that status.

That distinction has practical consequences. A lawyer who prepared you is not available to intervene if something unexpected happens at the conference. If the proceeding goes to a place you did not prepare for, you navigate it alone.

This is not an argument against coaching. It is context for what coaching is and what it is not.

## The Rule 24 connection

One of the highest-value uses of limited-scope services is not preparation — it is risk management. Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, courts may award costs against a party for unreasonable litigation conduct. Filing a motion without reasonable cause, missing disclosure obligations, and conducting the case in a way that generates unnecessary cost for the other side can all produce cost orders.

<Callout type="warning" title="Rule 24 costs">
  Ontario Family Law Rules, Rule 24 gives courts the authority to award costs for unreasonable litigation conduct. SRLs are not exempt. A single consultation before filing a motion can identify whether the motion is well-grounded — before the other side's costs are on the table.
</Callout>

A lawyer does not need to represent you throughout a case to tell you whether a specific step carries Rule 24 risk. That is the kind of question a single session can answer.

## The SRL-strategic view

Limited scope services are most powerful at leverage points. The start of the case, for strategic orientation. Before any document you cannot easily correct after filing. Before a settlement conference, to calibrate your position. Before trial, for coaching on evidence rules and presentation.
