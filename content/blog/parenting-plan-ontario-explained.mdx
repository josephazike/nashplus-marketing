When parents separate, the question of how their children will live does not resolve itself. It has to be worked out — in writing, and in enough detail to be useful on an ordinary Tuesday when one parent needs to know who picks up from school.

A parenting plan is that working-out, put on paper.

It does not require a court. It does not require a lawyer. And it is not the same as a court order. Understanding those distinctions is useful before going further.

## What a parenting plan is

A parenting plan is a written document that describes how parents will share the care and decision-making for their children after separation. It covers the practical details: when each parent has the children, how decisions about school and health will be made, how the parents will communicate, and how the plan will be reviewed if circumstances change.

The 2021 amendments to the *Divorce Act* (in force March 1, 2021) added a provision, in section 16.6, specifically recognizing parenting plans. A court can incorporate a parenting plan into a parenting order where it considers it appropriate to do so. A plan agreed to by both parents and filed with the court becomes part of the enforceable order.

<Callout type="note" title="The 2021 terminology change">
  The 2021 Divorce Act amendments replaced "custody" and "access" with "decision-making responsibility" and "parenting time." Ontario courts now use this language. This article uses the current terms throughout. The article on [decision-making responsibility and parenting time](/blog/decision-making-responsibility-parenting-time-ontario) explains the shift in detail.
</Callout>

## How a parenting plan differs from a court order

A parenting plan created by agreement is not automatically enforceable in the same way a court order is. If one parent does not follow a written agreement, the other parent cannot call the Family Responsibility Office or file for contempt without a court order to back it up.

A court order — whether made on consent after the parents agree, or after a contested hearing — is enforceable. Violations can result in a motion to enforce, a cost order, or in serious cases, contempt proceedings.

## What makes a plan actually useful

The legal framework matters. But what determines whether a parenting plan works in practice is something different: whether it describes the children's lives with enough specificity to resolve the most common disputes before they happen.

A plan that says "the children will spend roughly equal time with both parents" leaves every detail open. Who picks them up on Friday? What happens when a holiday falls mid-week? Who attends the parent-teacher interview when both parents are available?

A plan written for the children's lived experience rather than for the court's record answers those questions. It describes a Tuesday in the children's lives, not an abstract allocation of time.

That is not a legal requirement. It is a practical one. The article on [how to write a parenting plan](/blog/how-to-write-parenting-plan-ontario-factors) covers what that detail looks like and the factors courts consider when they review one.

## When a plan becomes necessary

Not all separations require formal parenting plans. Some parents communicate well and manage parenting arrangements informally without conflict. Others need more structure.

A formal parenting plan is typically necessary when:

- The parents will be returning to court, and the court will want to know the proposed arrangement
- One or both parents want a written, reviewable document that protects against future misunderstanding
- The separation is contested and communication is strained
- Children are moving between two very different households and consistency matters

For parents in an Ontario family law proceeding, the case conference process under Rule 17 of the *Family Law Rules* will ask each parent to describe their proposed parenting arrangements. Having a written plan ready, even a draft, is useful at that stage.
