Every year, tens of thousands of Ontarians attend family court without a lawyer. Some cannot afford representation. Others have straightforward matters they believe they can handle independently. Some prefer to retain a lawyer only for specific tasks — drafting agreements, reviewing documents, or appearing at particular hearings — while managing the rest themselves.

Whatever the reason, self-represented litigants (SRLs) are a significant and recognized presence in Ontario's family court system. This guide covers what you need to know if you are navigating the process on your own.

---

## You have the right to represent yourself

There is no requirement that you have a lawyer to participate in Ontario family court proceedings. Section 7 of the *Courts of Justice Act* establishes that a party to a proceeding may act in person.

Courts are required to treat self-represented parties fairly. Judges and court staff cannot provide legal advice — the law does not permit this, and it would compromise impartiality — but they can provide procedural information and explain court processes.

---

## The two courts that handle Ontario family matters

**Ontario Court of Justice**
Handles child custody and access, child support, child protection proceedings, and spousal support claims under the *Family Law Act*. The Ontario Court of Justice cannot grant divorces, and it has jurisdiction over property matters only in limited circumstances.

**Superior Court of Justice (Family Court Branch)**
Handles divorce, property claims (equalization of net family property), adoption, and all matters that can be addressed by the Ontario Court of Justice. In most regions, the Superior Court of Justice has jurisdiction over the full range of family law matters.

In some areas of Ontario, the **Unified Family Court** combines both courts in a single location. The courthouse serving your area determines where to file.

## How proceedings start

Most family proceedings are started by filing an **Application** (Form 8A) with the court. The person who files first is the Applicant. The other party is the Respondent.

After the Application is filed and served, the Respondent has an opportunity to file an **Answer** (Form 10) and, if they have their own claims, a **Counter-Application**.

At this stage, both parties are typically required to file **financial statements** — Form 13 or Form 13.1 — and serve them on the other party. These documents are the foundation of any proceeding involving money.

---

## The case management pathway

Ontario family proceedings generally follow a structured path after the initial documents are filed.

**First Appointment (Case Conference)**
A judge meets with both parties to identify the issues, explore the possibility of settlement, and set timelines. The judge does not make final orders at a case conference, but may make procedural orders — for example, requiring a party to produce documents.

**Settlement Conference**
A later meeting focused on resolving as many issues as possible before trial. A judge reviews the parties' positions and may provide non-binding views on how they see the issues. Many matters settle at or after a settlement conference.

**Trial Management Conference**
A final pre-trial meeting where the parties confirm they cannot settle and prepare for trial. The judge may set the trial schedule and limit the issues.

**Trial**
If the matter cannot be resolved, a trial is held before a judge. Each party presents evidence, calls witnesses, and makes submissions. The judge issues a final order.

Not every proceeding goes to trial. The majority of family law matters in Ontario are resolved by negotiated agreement before trial.

## What you are responsible for as a self-represented litigant

Courts do not lower their procedural standards for SRLs. You are expected to:

- **File the correct forms** — the court will not tell you which forms you need; you are responsible for knowing
- **Meet deadlines** — failing to file on time can result in consequences including dismissal or default
- **Serve the other party correctly** — documents must be served in the manner the rules require
- **Prepare for each court event** — arriving at a case conference without required documents or a financial statement is not an acceptable approach
- **Understand the rules** — the *Family Law Rules* are publicly available; self-represented parties are expected to understand them

Judges will often be patient with procedural unfamiliarity, but they cannot act as your advocate or advise you on strategy.

## Limited scope retainers: getting help for specific tasks

You do not have to choose between full representation and complete self-representation. Many lawyers in Ontario accept **limited scope retainers** — arrangements where the lawyer assists with specific tasks only.

Common examples:
- Reviewing a separation agreement before you sign it
- Drafting a particular motion or responding document
- Appearing at one specific court event
- Coaching: advising you on strategy before a court appearance, without appearing on your behalf

Limited scope representation can significantly reduce costs while ensuring you have professional input at critical decision points. Ontario's *Rules of Professional Conduct* permit and regulate limited scope representation.
