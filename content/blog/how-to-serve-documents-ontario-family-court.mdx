Service is not a formality. It is a legal requirement that gives the other party notice of what is being filed and what is being asked for. A document that was filed but not properly served may, in certain circumstances, be treated as if it was never served at all — and any court order obtained without proper service may be set aside.

For a self-represented litigant, service is among the most procedurally consequential tasks in the proceeding. Getting it right from the start prevents significant delays.

## The governing rule

Service in Ontario family law proceedings is governed by Rule 6 of the *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99*. Rule 6 sets out who can serve documents, how service must be carried out, and what proof is required.

---

## Who can serve documents

The party who filed the document generally cannot serve it themselves. Service must be carried out by someone other than the party — typically a process server, a friend, an adult family member, or any other adult who is not a party to the proceeding.

There is no requirement to use a professional process server for most documents in Ontario family court. Any adult who is not a party can carry out service. What matters is that the person serving the document can swear an affidavit about how, when, and where service was carried out.

---

## Methods of service

Rule 6 distinguishes between **personal service** and **alternatives to personal service**.

**Personal service** means delivering the document directly to the person being served — handing it to them in person. Personal service is required for the initial application in a proceeding and for certain other originating documents. It is the most reliable method because it is the hardest to dispute.

**Alternatives to personal service** are available for most documents after the proceeding has started. Alternatives include:

- Leaving the document with an adult who lives at the person's residence, and mailing a copy the same day
- Mailing the document to the person's last known address (service is deemed to occur on the fifth day after mailing)
- Sending the document by email, if the person has consented to electronic service or if the court has ordered it
- Fax, where the person's fax number is known and accessible

## Proof of service: the affidavit of service

After service is carried out, the person who served the document swears an **affidavit of service** — Form 6B under the *Family Law Rules*. This sworn document records:

- Who served the document
- What was served (document name and date)
- When service was carried out (date and time)
- Where service was carried out
- How service was carried out (personal delivery, mail, email, etc.)

The affidavit of service is filed with the court. Without it, the court has no record that service occurred. Documents without proof of service may be treated as unserved for procedural purposes.

## Common service traps

**Serving yourself.** A party cannot serve their own documents. This is the most frequent error. If you need documents served, arrange for another adult to do it — not you.

**Using the wrong method for originating documents.** The initial application must be served by personal service. Mailing it to the other party, or emailing it without a court order permitting email service, is not valid service of an originating document.

**Failing to file proof of service.** Carrying out service is only half the task. If the affidavit of service is not filed, the court has no record that service happened. A conference may be adjourned because proof of service is missing.

**Missing the deemed-service timing.** When service is by mail, it is not complete on the day of mailing. It is deemed to occur on the fifth day after mailing. Deadlines that run from the date of service must be calculated from that deemed date.

**Service on a representative.** Once a party is represented by a lawyer, documents must generally be served on the lawyer, not directly on the party. Service directly on a represented party may not be valid unless the lawyer has consented.

<Callout type="warning" title="Improper service consequences">
  An order obtained without proper service can be set aside. Rule 6 of the Family Law Rules governs service because courts require certainty that all parties received notice before being bound by proceedings or orders.
</Callout>

## When service fails or the other party evades

Sometimes service cannot be completed because the other party cannot be located or is evading service. In those cases, a party may bring a motion for **substituted service** — asking the court to permit an alternative method of service that is likely to bring the document to the other party's attention.

Substituted service might involve serving a family member, serving at a last-known address, or serving through social media if the other party is known to use a specific platform. Courts have discretion to authorize substituted service and specify how it must be carried out.

The order for substituted service must itself be obtained before the alternative service is used. The party seeking it must demonstrate that ordinary service has been attempted and has failed.
