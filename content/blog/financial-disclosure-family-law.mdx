Financial disclosure is not optional in Ontario family law proceedings. It is a **mandatory obligation** under the Family Law Rules, and courts enforce it seriously. Understanding what you must disclose, when, and in what form is essential whether you are navigating proceedings on your own or working with a lawyer.

---

## The legal basis for disclosure

Rule 13 of the Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99, governs financial disclosure. The rule requires any party to a proceeding that involves a claim for support or property to serve and file a sworn financial statement — Form 13 or Form 13.1, depending on the claims involved.

Beyond the financial statement itself, the rules require parties to disclose **supporting documents** — the underlying records that verify the numbers in the statement.

The obligation is ongoing. If your financial situation changes materially after you have filed your financial statement, you are required to update your disclosure.

---

## What you must disclose

### Income documents

For the current year and the two preceding years:

- **T1 General income tax return** — the complete return, all schedules
- **CRA Notice of Assessment** — the confirmation document issued by the Canada Revenue Agency after processing your return
- **T4 slips** — employment income statements from each employer
- **Pay stubs** — typically the three most recent

If you are self-employed, a business owner, or receive income from a corporation:

- Business financial statements for the most recent three years
- Corporate tax returns (T2) where income flows from a corporation you control
- Shareholder loan account statements
- Dividend records

### Asset documents

For real property:
- MPAC assessment notice (current year)
- A recent independent appraisal if the parties dispute the value
- Mortgage statement showing the outstanding balance at the valuation date

For financial accounts (bank, investment, TFSA, RRSP, RRIF):
- Account statements as of the **date of separation** (the valuation date)
- Account statements as of the **date of marriage** (for equalization purposes)
- If the account did not exist at the date of marriage, a statement confirming this is not needed, but the opening date may be relevant

For pensions:
- Annual benefit statement from the pension plan administrator
- For defined benefit pensions, a formal actuarial valuation is typically required

For vehicles:
- A current valuation (Canadian Black Book or equivalent)
- Vehicle ownership document

### Debt documents

- Mortgage statement (current balance)
- Line of credit statements
- Credit card statements
- Loan agreements and current balances
- Any other obligations

## When disclosure is due

Deadlines for financial statements are tied to specific court events rather than a fixed period after the claim is filed. Updated financial disclosure must generally be served before each case conference, settlement conference, and trial — the exact lead time depends on whether you are the moving party or responding party and which court is hearing the matter.

Supporting financial documents must generally be served within 30 days after the date the financial statement itself was due to be served, under Rule 13 of the Family Law Rules.

The specific timelines depend on the type of proceeding and the court's scheduling orders. The practice directions of the specific court — Superior Court of Justice or Ontario Court of Justice — govern local requirements.

## The sworn statement requirement

A financial statement is not simply a form you fill out and submit. It is a **sworn document**. Before filing, you must sign the statement in front of a commissioner of oaths or notary public, attesting that the information in the statement is true.

Signing a sworn document that contains false or misleading information is a serious matter. Under the *Criminal Code*, making a false statement under oath is perjury, an indictable offence. Under the Family Law Rules, providing false financial disclosure can result in costs orders, adverse findings, or other court orders.

## Consequences of inadequate disclosure

Courts have several tools to address parties who fail to provide complete and accurate financial disclosure.

**Motion to compel.** A party may bring a motion requiring the other party to produce specific documents. Courts routinely grant these motions where disclosure is incomplete.

**Deemed income.** If a court finds that a party has deliberately hidden or undervalued income, it may **impute income** — attribute an income figure to that party that is higher than what they have declared. This affects both support calculations and the NFP assessment.

**Adverse inference.** Where a party refuses to disclose or provide documentation, the court may draw an adverse inference — concluding that the undisclosed information would be unfavourable to that party.

**Costs.** A party who forces the other party to bring motions to obtain disclosure may be ordered to pay their costs. In egregious cases, courts have awarded substantial costs against non-disclosing parties.

**Setting aside agreements.** If a party entered into a separation agreement or consent order on the basis of incomplete disclosure by the other party, that agreement or order may be set aside under section 56(4) of the *Family Law Act*.

## Practical challenges in disclosure

Several categories of financial information are commonly problematic.

### Valuation-date bank statements

Many people do not preserve their financial statements from the date of separation, particularly if it occurred years before the proceeding was commenced. Banks are required to maintain records and can generally provide historical statements, though fees may apply and retrieval can take time.

### Date-of-marriage values

Establishing what an asset was worth at the date of marriage — sometimes a decade or more ago — requires documentary reconstruction. Statements that no longer exist may need to be reconstructed from other records, CRA data, or historical valuations.

### Business interests

When one spouse has an ownership interest in a business, valuing that interest is among the most contested issues in property proceedings. Business valuations are typically conducted by certified business valuators. The methodology used — asset approach, income approach, or market approach — can produce significantly different values, and courts often receive competing valuations from experts retained by each party.

### Pension values

Defined benefit pensions require a valuation from the pension plan administrator. Processing times vary by plan. Requesting the valuation early in the proceeding is recommended, as delays in receiving the pension value can delay the entire proceeding.

## Continuous disclosure obligation

Once a financial statement is filed, it does not remain accurate indefinitely. If your income, assets, or debts change materially between filing and trial or settlement, you are required to serve and file an updated financial statement.

Material changes include:
- A change in employment or income
- Receipt of an inheritance or gift
- Sale or acquisition of a significant asset
- Taking on new debt

Under the Family Law Rules, the obligation to update arises as soon as a party discovers that a document they served is incorrect, incomplete, or out of date. There is no grace period — correction and re-service should happen promptly upon discovery.
