Most self-represented litigants in Ontario start by searching for a form online and finding a PDF that requires a version of Adobe they do not have, on a government website that was last updated in 2019.

That experience is common. It does not reflect what is actually available.

The Ontario family court system has more free, well-maintained public resources than most people navigating it know about. This article maps them in one place.

<Callout type="legal">
  This article provides information about free and low-cost resources for Ontario family court litigants. It is not legal advice. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed Ontario lawyer.
</Callout>

## Ontario Court Forms

The official Ontario court forms — including Form 8A (application), Form 13 (financial statement, support), Form 13.1 (financial statement, property and support), Form 14B (motion), and Form 17A (case conference brief) — are available for free at [ontario.ca/page/court-forms](https://www.ontario.ca/page/court-forms).

Forms are available in both fillable PDF and Word format. The Word versions can be typed and saved. The PDFs can be completed on-screen and printed.

This is the authoritative source. Third-party sites that offer Ontario court forms may be outdated or incorrect. Use the official Ontario court forms site.

## Steps to Justice

Steps to Justice ([stepstojustice.ca](https://stepstojustice.ca)) is a CLEO-produced plain-language guide to Ontario's legal system, maintained specifically for people navigating legal matters without a lawyer. The family law section includes:

- Plain-language explanations of Ontario family law processes
- Step-by-step guides for specific procedures (e.g., how to serve documents, how to prepare for a case conference)
- Interactive pathways that walk through scenarios
- Links to the relevant court forms at each stage

Steps to Justice is one of the clearest procedural guides available in the public domain. The SRL Playbook article on [representing yourself in family court](/blog/representing-yourself-family-court-ontario) references it for this reason.

## CLEO Plain Language Resources

CLEO — Community Legal Education Ontario — produces plain-language legal information guides specifically for people without legal training. Materials are available at [cleo.on.ca](https://www.cleo.on.ca) in multiple languages.

CLEO materials cover family law topics including separation and divorce, spousal support, child support, and family court processes. These are legal information documents, not legal advice — they describe the law and the process, not what any individual should do in their specific situation.

## Family Law Information Centres (FLICs)

FLICs are free at every Ontario family courthouse. They are staffed by Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel and information and referral coordinators. No income threshold. No application required.

At a FLIC, you can:

- Ask procedural questions about your case
- Have a duty counsel lawyer review a document before filing
- Get referrals to mediation, legal clinics, and other services
- Find out which court forms apply to your situation

The article on [Family Law Information Centres](/blog/family-law-information-centres-flic-ontario) in the Legal Aid cluster covers what FLICs can and cannot do in detail.

## Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel

For those who meet the financial eligibility threshold (net annual income at or below $45,440 for families of up to 4 as of March 3, 2025; assets at or below $15,000), Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel at the courthouse provides free brief legal assistance on the day of a court appearance.

Call LAO at **1-800-668-8258**, Monday to Friday, 8am to 5pm, to confirm eligibility before a court date.

The article on [Legal Aid Ontario eligibility](/blog/legal-aid-ontario-eligibility-explained) covers the full eligibility picture, including the difference between duty counsel and a legal aid certificate.

## Law Society Referral Service

The Law Society of Ontario offers one free 30-minute consultation with a licensed Ontario lawyer or paralegal. This is not ongoing representation — one session for your most urgent questions. Available through [lso.ca](https://lso.ca).

This is worth using before any major step in the proceeding: before filing the application, before the settlement conference, before signing a draft order.

---

## Ontario court file lookup

Case information for Ontario Superior Court of Justice proceedings is available through the public court file system. For Ontario Court of Justice family matters, court locations can be found via the [Ontario court finder](https://www.ontario.ca/page/find-ontario-court-location).

Knowing the location and court file number for your proceeding is basic orientation. The courthouse counter can provide information about how to access your file.

## What these resources do not replace

Free resources describe the law, explain the process, and help with forms. They do not provide legal advice about your specific case. They do not tell you whether your proposed parenting arrangement is likely to be approved, whether your financial statement is complete, or whether the draft order you are about to sign has problems.

Those questions require a lawyer who has read your specific documents. The article on [how to optimize your legal spend as an SRL](/blog/optimize-legal-spend-srl-ontario) maps where that targeted professional input matters most in a family law case.
