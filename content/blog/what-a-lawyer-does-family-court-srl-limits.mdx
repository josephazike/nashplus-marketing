This article is not about discouraging anyone from representing themselves. Plenty of people do it, every day, in Ontario family courts.

It is about something more useful than discouragement: honesty. Knowing what a lawyer knows that you do not — and specifically which parts of your case depend on that knowledge — is one of the most important strategic inputs available to an SRL.

## What lawyers actually know: the honest list

Legal training is not a general education in fairness. It is specific training in a narrow set of skills. Not all of those skills apply to every case. But knowing which ones apply to yours is itself a capability.

**Rules of evidence.** What is admissible at trial. How to object to evidence. The hearsay rule and its exceptions. How to get a document admitted into evidence. How to challenge evidence the other side has introduced. Almost no SRL has this training. At trial, the rules of evidence determine what the judge actually considers. A document that is not properly admitted is not evidence, regardless of what it says.

**Legal strategy.** What outcomes are legally achievable, as distinct from what you want. How a judge is likely to apply the law to your specific facts — not the law in general, but your facts. This is a judgment built from years of watching courts decide cases. It is not available from statutes or from research.

**Negotiation with legal knowledge.** Knowing what a trial would likely produce changes what you negotiate toward. An SRL who does not have a realistic sense of what a court would decide in their case is negotiating without the information that shapes every professional negotiation in family law.

**Costs risk calibration.** Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, courts may award costs against a party for unreasonable litigation conduct. A lawyer calibrates the costs risk of every action they take in a proceeding — often invisibly to the client. An SRL bears that judgment themselves, without the training that makes it routine.

**Order drafting.** A poorly worded order creates enforcement problems that require a return to court to fix. Lawyers draft orders with enforcement mechanics in mind — which clauses go to the Family Responsibility Office, which require a motion to enforce, which trigger dates matter. A consent order signed without this lens can contain ambiguities that produce conflict for years.

## Where the gap is genuinely small

An honest account includes the tasks where the capability gap between a lawyer and an organized SRL is small.

Filing and serving documents, once you understand the rules, is a procedural task. The rules are clear. The forms are available. The court counter staff can answer procedural questions.

Updating and maintaining the continuing record requires diligence and organization, not legal judgment.

Written communication with the other party — factual, dated, documented — is something any organized adult can do well.

Gathering and organizing financial records is time-consuming. It is not legally complex.

Routine conference briefs (Form 17A), after the first one, follow a pattern. Duty counsel at the Family Law Information Centre will review a draft for completeness.

Simple consent motions — where both parties agree and no legal argument is required — do not demand legal training to navigate.

The principle is consistent: tasks governed by a clear rule with a clear answer have a small gap. Tasks that require judgment about what the law would produce in your specific factual circumstances have a large one.

## Cases where the gap is largest

The NSRLP research and Justice Canada's review of court data both document that SRLs, on average, fare worse in court than represented parties and are more likely to end up at trial. Not in every case. On average. The cases where that outcome gap is most concentrated are predictable.

**Contested parenting cases where the other party has counsel.** The dynamic of a case changes when one side has a lawyer and the other does not. This is documented in the NSRLP findings and reflects the difference in negotiation information and procedural sophistication.

**Cases involving a history of domestic violence.** Section 16 of the *Divorce Act* requires courts to consider family violence as a factor in parenting decisions, among 16 specified best-interest factors. The evidentiary dimension of documenting, presenting, and responding to domestic violence claims involves legal complexity that affects outcomes significantly.

**Complex property cases.** Business valuations, pension division, trust interests, shareholder structures — these require expertise that goes well beyond reading the *Family Law Act*. The equalization calculation alone can be contested at multiple stages.

**Cases that reach trial.** The rules of evidence at trial — the *Ontario Evidence Act* and, in Superior Court proceedings, the *Canada Evidence Act* — govern what the judge actually considers. Cross-examination is a skill developed over years. Submissions require legal knowledge of the law being applied.

**Cases with significant costs exposure.** Where Rule 24 conduct findings are on the table, the judgment about what to file and when is a legal judgment, not a procedural one.

## The signals that suggest getting legal input

Not every case development requires a lawyer. Some specific developments change the risk profile of a case in ways that warrant reassessment.

**A motion you do not understand is filed by the other side.** You need to understand what is being asked, what the legal grounds are, and what the consequences of a successful motion would be before you respond.

**A legal concept appears that you cannot research to your own satisfaction.** Uncertainty that cannot be resolved by research belongs in a professional consultation, not in working memory running as background anxiety.

**The other side retains counsel mid-case.** The information dynamic, the procedural sophistication, and the costs risk calculation all change when one side has a lawyer.

**A judge makes a comment you do not understand.** An informal view at a case conference is information. If you do not understand what it means for your case, that is worth addressing before the next appearance.

These are signals. They are worth naming as such.
