In a family law proceeding, the messages you send the other parent do not stay between the two of you. They become exhibits. They are read by judges, lawyers, and assessors. They are attached to affidavits and filed with the court.

This is not a reason to stop communicating about the children. It is a reason to communicate differently.

<Callout type="legal">
  This article provides legal information about co-parent communication in the context of Ontario family law proceedings. It is not legal advice. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed Ontario lawyer.
</Callout>

## Why the communication record matters legally

Under the *Divorce Act* section 16(3), one of the factors courts must consider when assessing parenting arrangements is "the ability and willingness of each person to communicate and cooperate with the other parent on matters affecting the child."

This factor is assessed from the evidence available. The communication record is evidence.

A parent whose messages are factual, focused on the children, and calm demonstrates — to a court — what that factor looks like in practice. A parent whose messages are escalating, accusatory, or designed to provoke demonstrates something different.

Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, courts can award costs against a party who conducts litigation unreasonably. Communication outside of court that escalates the litigation, forces unnecessary motions, or creates procedural problems has been found relevant to the costs analysis. Self-represented litigants are not exempt.

That is not a judgment about what is justified or what the other parent deserves. It is a description of how communication choices affect legal outcomes.

## What judge-readable communication looks like

The standard to apply before sending any message is: would I be comfortable if a judge read this?

That standard produces a specific communication style:

**Factual.** The message describes what happened, what was agreed, or what is needed. It does not characterize the other parent's motives. It does not include commentary on the other parent's parenting, personality, or choices.

**Dated and specific.** Include the date and specific details. "On Tuesday, May 27, pickup was scheduled for 3pm. I arrived at 3:05pm and no one was home" is more useful to a court than "you are always late and never follow the schedule."

**Low temperature.** Messages sent in frustration, anger, or grief produce a different record than messages sent after 24 hours have passed. The content of the first may be accurate. The tone of the second is more likely to hold up.

**Child-focused.** The message addresses a matter that actually concerns the children. A judge reading a communication thread wants to see that the children are the subject of the exchange, not the parents' conflict.

**Brief.** Long messages that contain multiple issues, relitigating past events, and expressing grievances are harder to use as evidence and easier to respond to selectively.

## Communication tools and platforms

Some co-parents use dedicated co-parenting apps — platforms designed specifically for separated parents to communicate, share schedules, and document exchanges. These apps can create a permanent, timestamped record of communication that is easier to organize and present as evidence than a text thread.

Whether to use one is a practical question, not a legal requirement. If the current communication channel is producing escalation or disputes about what was said, a dedicated platform with message logging may reduce that friction.

<Callout type="warning" title="What not to use">
  Using the children to communicate messages to the other parent, or asking the children to report on the other parent's household, affects the children and creates evidence of the opposite of what factor (c) of the best interests test requires. Courts take this seriously.
</Callout>

## When communication breaks down completely

Some parenting disputes reach a point where direct communication between the parents causes consistent harm — to the children, to the litigation, or both. Options at that stage include:

**Parallel parenting.** Both parents operate independently in their own time with the children, with minimal communication except for essential matters. Communication is limited to writing, through a defined channel, about defined topics. This is a documented and recognized arrangement for high-conflict situations.

**A communication protocol in the parenting plan or order.** Some parenting orders specify how the parents must communicate — what channel, what response time, what topics. A protocol reduces the opportunity for escalation and creates clear evidence when it is violated.

**Family therapy or co-parenting coaching.** A trained professional can work with both parents, separately or together, to establish communication norms. This is not legal advice and not legal representation. It is a practical intervention that some families find reduces the temperature in a way that legal proceedings alone do not.
