The research on children's adjustment after parental separation has become increasingly clear about what causes harm and what does not.

Separation itself — the reorganization of a family into two households — does not reliably predict poor outcomes for children. What does predict poor outcomes, consistently across decades of research, is sustained exposure to parental conflict. Children who are caught in the middle, who witness hostility, who are used to carry messages or information, or who feel responsible for managing their parents' relationship with each other fare worse on every measured outcome than children in low-conflict separated families.

This is not a moral claim about either parent. It is a description of what the research shows. And it has direct relevance to what the law requires.

## What the law says about conflict and children

The *Divorce Act* section 16(3) factor on each parent's "willingness to support the development and maintenance of the child's relationship with the other parent" is directly connected to conflict. A parent who fuels conflict between the child and the other parent, uses the child to gather information, or undermines the other parent's relationship with the child is demonstrating the opposite of what this factor asks for.

This is not hypothetical. Ontario courts have made costs orders against parties whose litigation conduct, including co-parenting conduct documented in communication records, reflected a failure to prioritize the children's interests. Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, unreasonable conduct — including conduct that escalates proceedings or undermines the opposing parent's relationship with the child — is relevant to costs.

## The patterns that cause harm

Research on children in high-conflict separated families identifies specific patterns consistently associated with worse outcomes. These are descriptions of patterns, not characterizations of either parent's intentions.

**Direct exposure to conflict.** Children who witness arguments, hostile exchanges, or threatening behavior between parents carry the stress of that exposure into school, friendships, and sleep. The conflict does not have to be physical to be harmful. Sustained emotional hostility is enough.

**Being caught in the middle.** A child who is asked to take sides, relay messages, report on the other household, or manage a parent's emotional distress about the other parent is carrying a weight that belongs to adults. This pattern — triangulation — is documented as one of the most consistently harmful dynamics in high-conflict families.

**Loyalty conflicts.** A child who loves both parents and is exposed to signals, direct or indirect, that choosing one parent's side is required, experiences a conflict of loyalty that has nowhere to resolve. These conflicts affect identity and attachment development in ways that persist long after the legal proceedings end.

**Inconsistency and unpredictability.** High conflict often produces unreliable schedules, contested transitions, and last-minute changes. Children in these environments show higher rates of anxiety than children with consistent routines, even when the routine involves significant time apart from each parent.

## What does not harm children: a common misunderstanding

Parents often come to family law proceedings with a specific fear: that the amount of time the child spends with the other parent will determine the child's outcome. The research does not support this as the primary variable.

The quality of each parent's relationship with the child — the attentiveness, the warmth, the reliability — predicts outcomes more consistently than the distribution of parenting time. A child who spends every other week with a parent who is engaged, present, and emotionally available has a different experience than a child who spends the same amount of time with a parent who is preoccupied with the conflict.

This matters because the energy spent on conflict — legal and otherwise — is energy not available for the children. That is an observation about how human attention and emotional capacity work, not a judgment.

## What the structural literature suggests

Research on high-conflict separated families identifies some approaches that reliably reduce harm to children. These are not prescriptions for any individual family.

**Parallel parenting.** Reducing direct interaction between parents, communicating through a written channel only, and operating independently within each parent's time with the child. This model reduces the opportunities for conflict to be witnessed and reduces the child's experience of being caught between two parents who cannot be in the same room.

**Written communication through a dedicated channel.** Co-parenting apps, email, or similar platforms create a record of communication and impose a delay between impulse and message. The article on [communicating with a difficult co-parent](/blog/communicating-with-difficult-coparent-ontario) addresses the discipline of judge-readable communication, which applies in high-conflict situations regardless of whether a court is currently involved.

**Reducing children's role as information carriers.** Both parents making a conscious and consistent decision that the children will not be asked what happens in the other home, will not relay messages, and will not be used to negotiate logistics. This requires effort from both parents. Where one parent maintains the pattern regardless, a parenting order with explicit provisions may reduce the practice.

**Professional co-parenting support.** A trained co-parenting mediator or family therapist can sometimes establish communication structures and norms that the parents cannot establish on their own. This is not couples therapy. It is specifically oriented toward the children's wellbeing and the co-parenting relationship as a functional entity.

## The legal record and conduct

In high-conflict proceedings, the communication record matters. A parent whose record shows escalating messages, repeated litigation of resolved issues, or consistent interference with the other parent's relationship with the child is creating evidence of a pattern that courts assess under the best interests standard.

The article on [documenting communication for family court](/blog/documenting-communication-family-court-ontario) covers what makes a record useful. The article on [best interests of the child in Ontario](/blog/best-interests-of-the-child-ontario) explains the full legal standard, including the factors courts use to assess each parent's conduct.
