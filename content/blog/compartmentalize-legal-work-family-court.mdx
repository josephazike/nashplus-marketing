Representing yourself in family court means being three things at once. The person the case is about. The person doing the legal work. And the person who still has to show up for work, their children, and themselves the next morning.

Nobody tells you about the overlap. Most people experiencing it think something is wrong with them. This article names the structure instead.

## The three-role burden

A lawyer carries the legal work. The emotional weight of the case stays with the client. Those are two separate roles, held by two separate people.

When you represent yourself, both roles collapse into one. You are:

- **The party** — the person with an emotional stake in the outcome, whose family, finances, and relationship with your children are what the case is about
- **The legal worker** — the person responsible for every procedural deadline, every document that must be filed correctly, every rule that must be followed
- **The evidence source** — the person whose own life, income, conduct, and memory are the raw material of the case

Lawyers spend years learning the second role. The first and third are not taught anywhere. And unlike a lawyer, you cannot close the file at the end of the day and leave.

Research by the National Self-Represented Litigants Project (NSRLP) at the University of Windsor documents that stress and a sense of overwhelm are consistent features of self-representation across the system, not exceptions to it. Justice Canada's review of court data notes that SRLs are more likely than represented parties to end up at trial — a pattern that reflects, in part, the difficulty of evaluating settlement options clearly when you are the person most affected by them.

That is not a character flaw. It is a structural reality. And structure can be worked with.

## Why it escalates

The NSRLP research documents SRLs piecing together information, making procedural mistakes, and returning to the courthouse repeatedly to correct them. Each mistake generates a new task. Each new task generates anxiety. Anxiety degrades the next piece of work.

The feedback loop is real. Naming it is more useful than catastrophizing it.

There is also a legal dimension. Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, courts may award costs against a party for unreasonable litigation conduct. The sequence that produces costs problems most reliably is this one: a difficult court document arrives, it produces an angry or frightened response, and something is filed before the emotional state has cleared enough to ask whether filing it is a good idea.

<Callout type="warning" title="Rule 24 costs">
  Ontario Family Law Rules, Rule 24 allows courts to award costs for unreasonable litigation conduct, including motions brought without reasonable cause. SRLs are not exempt. A 24-hour delay before filing anything reactive is not a strategy; it is a default protection.
</Callout>

## Time-boxing: the practical core

The most effective structural intervention available to most SRLs is simple: legal work happens in defined windows, not all the time.

A case that is always open occupies background processing continuously. A case that has a scheduled window — and closes outside that window — does not.

Observable approaches that reduce the ambient drain:

**A legal calendar, not just a deadline tracker.** Mark court dates and filing deadlines, but also mark the specific hours each week when legal work happens. Outside those hours, the case is closed. This is not denial — it is scheduling.

**A dedicated physical space.** Case materials in one place, closed when legal hours end. The visual cue matters. When the binder is on the desk, the case is open. When it is in the drawer, it is not.

**A single case notebook.** Everything — questions, observations, things to research, things to ask — goes in one notebook. When a thought about the case surfaces outside legal hours, it goes in the notebook and closes. It does not stay in working memory.

## The decision queue

Most SRL anxiety is not about the case itself. It is about unresolved questions sitting in working memory: *What do I do about this letter? What does that clause mean? What is supposed to happen next?*

The decision queue converts those questions from background noise into a managed list.

A decision queue is a written list of open case questions, each with a date by which a decision or answer is needed. When a question is on the list with a deadline attached, it does not need to live in your head. It has a place. It has a date. It is managed, not suppressed.

<Checklist
  heading="Decision queue: what belongs on the list"
  items={[
    "Questions with a known deadline: write the question, write the date, note what happens if it is not answered",
    "Questions without a deadline: write the question, assign a review date (when will you need to know this?)",
    "Documents received but not yet understood: note the document, the question it raises, and when you need to act",
    "Items that require a professional answer: flag these for your next FLIC visit, consultation, or duty counsel session",
    "Do NOT include items that have already been decided — completed items leave the list",
  ]}
/>

## Two hazards with legal consequences

The article on [managing the emotional load](/blog/emotional-load-self-representation) addresses the deeper emotional dimension of self-representation. Two specific patterns from that article are worth naming here because they have concrete legal consequences.

**Reactive filing.** A motion or letter sent from an angry state. Under Rule 24, courts may award costs for motions brought without reasonable cause. The legal risk is attached to the action, regardless of the emotional state that produced it. The delay between impulse and filing matters.

**Avoidance.** Not opening court mail. Missing a deadline. Failing to file a required document. Avoidance is a documented stress response. Its consequences in litigation are more serious than the documents being avoided. A default may be noted. An order may be made without your input. The things being avoided do not go away; they accumulate.

The practical frame for avoidance is not moral. It is this: the consequences of avoidance are almost always worse than the cost of addressing the thing being avoided, even partially.

## The SRL who compartmentalizes well

The SRL who can separate their role as a party from their role as a legal worker — even imperfectly, even some of the time — is better positioned in court than one who cannot.

Not because they feel less. Because the legal documents are cleaner. The decisions are made from a steadier place. The brief filed at midnight reads differently from the brief filed after three deliberate evenings of work.
