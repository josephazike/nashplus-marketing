If you are going through a separation or divorce in Ontario and support or property issues are in dispute, there is a near-certain chance you will encounter **Form 13** or **Form 13.1**. These are the financial statements that family courts require to understand each party's financial situation. Without them, proceedings cannot advance.

This article explains what these forms are, who files them, and what the court expects to find in them.

---

## Two forms, one purpose

Ontario's Family Law Rules provide for two versions of the financial statement:

**Form 13 — Financial Statement (Support Claims)**
Filed when the proceeding involves only support claims — child support, spousal support, or both — and does not involve an equalization of net family property. Form 13 captures income, expenses, and property only in summary.

**Form 13.1 — Financial Statement (Property and Support Claims)**
Filed when the proceeding involves a claim for the equalization of net family property — the calculation that determines whether one spouse owes the other a payment based on what each accumulated during the marriage. Form 13.1 is substantially more detailed than Form 13, requiring a full accounting of assets, debts, and excluded property at multiple dates.

The court file, not the parties' preference, determines which form is required. If equalization is claimed or may be claimed, Form 13.1 is the appropriate form.

---

## Who files it

The obligation to file a financial statement applies to both parties in most cases. Under Rule 13 of the Family Law Rules, a party who receives a claim for support, or who makes a claim for support or property, is required to serve and file a financial statement.

The form must be **sworn or affirmed** — meaning you sign it before a commissioner of oaths, and you attest that the information is true. Providing false information in a sworn financial statement has serious legal consequences.

---

## What Form 13.1 contains

Form 13.1 captures your complete financial picture across five main sections and three schedules.

**Part 1 — Income**
Total income from all sources for the current year and the two preceding years: employment income, self-employment income, investment income, rental income, and government benefits. Income is broken down by source and entered as monthly figures for support calculation purposes. Schedule A captures supplemental income categories — partnership income, rental income, dividends, capital gains, RRSP and RRIF withdrawals, and other sources.

**Part 2 — Expenses**
Monthly living expenses for you and any dependants, organized across categories: housing, utilities, transportation, health, personal, household, childcare, and other. This part supports claims for spousal support and informs the court about each party's financial needs. Schedule B captures household and partner information relevant to the expense picture.

**Part 3 — Assets**
A complete listing of all assets owned as of the **valuation date** (usually the date of separation) and their current value. This includes real property, financial accounts, pensions, vehicles, business interests, life insurance with cash value, and personal property. For equalization purposes, the form also records the value of assets at the **date of marriage** where applicable.

**Part 4 — Debts and other liabilities**
All debts owed as of the valuation date, including mortgages, lines of credit, credit card balances, and loans. As with assets, the form records balances at the date of marriage for debts that existed then.

**Part 5 — Summary**
The totals from Parts 3 and 4 feed into the net family property calculation — the figure used to determine whether an equalization payment is owed. Schedule C records special or extraordinary expenses for children, which are relevant to the child support calculation.

## Why accuracy matters

A financial statement is not an estimate. It is a sworn document. Courts treat incomplete or inaccurate financial statements seriously, and opposing parties may bring a motion to compel disclosure or to have a financial statement struck.

Every figure should be supported by documentation: tax returns, CRA Notices of Assessment, bank statements, investment account statements, property assessments, mortgage statements, and pension statements. Courts expect this supporting material to be served along with the financial statement or available for inspection.

Where a value is genuinely uncertain — for example, the value of a pension — the form permits approximations, but those approximations should be based on the best available information and should be disclosed as approximations.

---

## Common sources of difficulty

Several sections of Form 13.1 cause consistent difficulty for self-represented litigants:

**Pension valuation.** Defined benefit pensions are among the most complex assets to value. An actuarial valuation is often required. The Ontario Court of Justice and Superior Court of Justice have guidance on when an actuarial report is needed.

**Self-employment income.** When income comes from a business or professional corporation, the three-year income history required by Part 1 can be difficult to extract from financial statements. Courts may look behind corporate structures to assess the controlling spouse's true income.

**Date-of-marriage values.** Establishing what an asset was worth at the date of marriage — often years or decades earlier — can require documentary reconstruction. Parties who lack records for this period may need to use appraisals, historical records, or other evidence.

**Excluded property tracing.** Property that qualifies for exclusion must be traced continuously. A gift or inheritance that was mixed with joint funds may lose its character as excluded property.

## The role of supporting documents

Each assertion in Form 13.1 must be capable of verification. The most commonly required documents include:

- T1 General income tax returns for three years
- CRA Notices of Assessment for three years
- Pay stubs (most recent three)
- Bank statements at the date of separation and date of marriage
- Investment account and RRSP statements at the date of separation and date of marriage
- MPAC property assessment and any recent appraisal
- Mortgage statement at the date of separation
- Pension benefit statements
- Marriage certificate (to establish the date of marriage)
- Birth certificates for children (where child support is claimed)
