Most people assume that hiring a lawyer means handing over the entire case. It does not have to.

Ontario law explicitly recognizes a model where a lawyer handles one document, one court appearance, or one strategic consultation — and nothing outside that scope. The Law Society of Ontario calls this a limited scope retainer. It is also called unbundled legal services.

This is not informal. It is a governed professional engagement with defined obligations on both sides.

## What makes it "unbundled"

In a traditional full retainer, a lawyer handles everything: reviewing all documents, attending all court dates, drafting all correspondence, advising at every stage. The entire legal matter is "bundled" into one ongoing relationship.

In an unbundled arrangement, the scope is broken apart. The client selects specific tasks to have professionally handled. The lawyer is retained only for those tasks. Everything else stays with the client.

The scope is defined in writing, in the retainer letter. The lawyer is professionally responsible only for what is in that retainer. The client is responsible for everything outside it.

## How the retainer letter works

In any unbundled engagement, the scope must be defined in writing before the lawyer begins work. A properly structured retainer letter specifies:

- Exactly what the lawyer is retained to do
- What they are explicitly NOT retained to do
- What happens if additional work becomes necessary
- How the engagement ends

Reading the retainer letter carefully before signing is the most important step in a limited-scope engagement. If the letter is vague about scope, ask for specificity before signing. A clearly defined scope protects both you and the lawyer.

## What limited scope does not make the lawyer responsible for

A lawyer retained for document review is not responsible for your court strategy. A lawyer retained for a consultation is not your lawyer the next day. A lawyer who helped you prepare for a settlement conference is not representing you at that conference unless separately retained for that appearance.

This boundary matters practically: when you appear at a settlement conference after receiving coaching, you appear as a self-represented party, not as a represented party. The court and the other side will treat you accordingly.

<Callout type="note">
  If a lawyer provides coaching to prepare you for a court event but is not retained for the appearance itself, you remain self-represented at that event. This affects how the court interacts with you and what the other party knows about your preparation.
</Callout>

## How to find a limited-scope lawyer in Ontario

Not every family lawyer in Ontario offers unbundled services, but many do. The most direct approach is to contact family lawyers and ask directly: "Do you offer limited-scope or unbundled services?"

The Law Society of Ontario's lawyer directory at [lso.ca](https://lso.ca) allows you to search for family lawyers by location and can identify those on the LAO roster. Many lawyers who offer limited-scope services advertise this specifically on their websites.

JusticeNet ([justicenet.ca](https://www.justicenet.ca)) connects middle-income Ontarians with lawyers offering reduced fees, including for specific tasks.

## When it is most useful

Unbundled services are most effective at leverage points: the moments in a case where professional legal judgment changes the outcome most.

Research by the National Self-Represented Litigants Project at the University of Windsor documents that self-represented litigants are more likely than represented parties to end up at trial, and fare worse when they get there. The pattern often traces back to earlier stages: declining a reasonable settlement because they did not have a realistic view of likely outcomes; filing a financial statement with errors that surface under cross-examination; signing a draft order without understanding its enforcement implications.

A single consultation at the settlement conference stage, specifically to understand the realistic range of outcomes, addresses the most common of those failure points. That is not legal advice about your case. It is a documented pattern in the research.
