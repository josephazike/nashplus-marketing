At a settlement conference, a judge asks: "Can you take me to the financial statement?" One party opens a binder to a numbered tab and points to the document. The other searches through a folder, then a bag, then apologizes.

The judge has already formed a view, and neither party has spoken yet.

The continuing record is the court's official permanent file for a family law case. Under Rule 9 of the *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99*, the applicant is responsible for creating it. After that, all parties share responsibility for maintaining it. A well-maintained continuing record makes every court appearance smoother. A disorganized one creates friction at the moments when you can least afford it.

## What the continuing record is

The continuing record is a shared binder system maintained at the courthouse. It has two volumes, each with a distinct colour and a distinct purpose.

**The endorsement volume (yellow cover)** contains all orders made in the case and all written directions or endorsements from the judge. When a judge makes a court order, a signed copy goes into the endorsement volume. When a judge writes a direction at the end of a conference, it goes in there too. This volume is the authoritative record of everything the court has decided.

**The documents volume (red cover)** contains everything else: the application, the answer, financial statements, motions, affidavits, and other materials filed in the proceeding. Documents in this volume are arranged in chronological order, with the most recent documents at the back. Each document has a numbered tab.

Case conference briefs and settlement conference briefs are generally not part of the continuing record — they are used at the conference and then set aside — unless a judge specifically orders that a brief be included.

---

## Who creates it and who maintains it

The applicant creates the continuing record when they file the application. At that moment, a binder is opened, a table of contents is created, and the application becomes the first document under the first tab.

After that, every party — applicant and respondent — is responsible for adding documents they file to the continuing record. When a document is served and filed, the filing party adds it to the record, assigns the next available tab number, and updates the table of contents.

Court staff at the courthouse maintain the official copy. Parties keep their own working copies — a personal binder that mirrors the court's file. ## The table of contents

The table of contents is the navigation tool for the entire file. Every document in the record — every tab in both volumes — must appear in the table of contents with:

- The volume it belongs to (endorsement or documents)
- The tab number
- A description of the document
- Which party filed it
- The date it was filed

The table of contents is updated every time a document is added. A judge using the continuing record at a conference will often go to the table of contents first. If it is current and accurate, they can find what they need. If it is out of date, the conference starts with confusion.

## What goes in the continuing record

  heading="Documents that go in the continuing record"

    "Application (Form 8 or 8A) — goes in documents volume, Tab 1",
    "Proof of service of the application",
    "Answer (and if applicable, Answer and Claim)",
    "Financial statements (Form 13 or 13.1) — served by both parties",
    "Form 13A (Certificate of Financial Disclosure) — at conference stage",
    "Motions and supporting materials (notice of motion, affidavits)",
    "Any court orders — go in the endorsement volume",
    "Judicial endorsements and written directions — go in the endorsement volume",
    "Any other materials the court directs to be included",

## Why this matters more for SRLs than for represented parties

A lawyer who appears regularly in family court knows the continuing record system instinctively. Their paralegal maintains the record. Their client rarely touches it.

An SRL is responsible for creating the record, maintaining it, updating the table of contents every time a document is added, and bringing the right binder to every court appearance. That is a real administrative obligation that runs for the duration of the proceeding.

The practical consequence of getting it wrong: a judge who cannot locate a document during a conference may move on, note the disorganization, or express frustration. None of those outcomes help the party whose materials cannot be found.

The practical consequence of getting it right: a judge who can navigate an organized record navigates the case more smoothly, and the party who maintained it has demonstrated organizational competence without saying anything.

## The personal parallel system

The court's copy of the continuing record lives at the courthouse. An SRL needs their own working copy at home.

The most useful personal system mirrors the continuing record exactly — same tab numbers, same order — and adds a separate folder for documents in progress (drafts, notes, incoming documents not yet filed).
