When a party appears before a judge, the judge is forming a view before the first sentence is spoken. Not a view of the legal merits. A view of the person in front of them.

Clarity, organization, and composure are all observable within 30 seconds. This is not about suppressing what you feel. It is about not letting the hardest day of your life speak louder than your evidence.

## What judges observe and can act on

Credibility is part of fact-finding. When two parties give conflicting accounts, the judge must decide which account is more reliable. Observable markers inform that decision.

**Organization.** Can you find the document when asked? An SRL who opens to the correct tab immediately communicates that they know their case. An SRL who searches through papers communicates the opposite — before making any argument.

**Consistency.** Do you say the same thing twice? Judges hear the same case from both sides, sometimes over multiple appearances. Inconsistent statements between your brief and your oral submissions, or between appearances, are noted.

**Tone.** Reactive, sarcastic, or contemptuous language directed at the other party is visible and affects credibility. It does not need to rise to misconduct to matter. A judge who observes that one party stays factual while the other escalates has information about both parties.

**Proportionality.** Short answers to short questions. Substantive answers to substantive ones. An SRL who gives a four-paragraph answer to a yes-or-no question is communicating something about how they process and present information.

The NSRLP research documents that SRLs often struggle to present organized, focused cases under the stress of proceedings. That struggle is real and structural. It is also something that targeted preparation can address.

## The structural distinction most SRLs miss: witness vs. party

At trial, an SRL speaks in one of two modes — never both simultaneously.

Combining them is the error. Making submissions that introduce new facts not already in evidence — "and another thing the judge should know is..." — is a procedural error. It is also a credibility problem. It signals to the judge that the SRL does not understand the distinction between testifying and arguing, which affects how they evaluate everything else the SRL says.

If you want the judge to know a fact, that fact needs to be in your affidavit (in evidence) before the submissions stage. If you are making a legal argument, it is based on evidence already in the record.

## Practical techniques for staying composed

These are not therapy. They are observable strategies that work in a courtroom environment.

**Before speaking: one breath.** A deliberate pause before responding is professional, not weak. Judges do not expect instant responses to complex questions. "May I have a moment?" is an acceptable thing to say.

**When the other party says something false: write it down.** Do not respond immediately. The impulse to correct something in the moment almost always produces a reaction that is disproportionate to the moment's actual stakes. You will have a chance to respond. The note gives you that chance without the heat of the immediate moment.

**When your voice begins to rise: slow down, not faster.** Speeding up when emotional is a natural response. The court environment requires the opposite. Slowing down forces a pause between thought and speech that improves both clarity and composure.

**At a conference: "I'd like a moment to find that document."** This is not a weakness. It is professional. The pause while you find the document is infinitely preferable to giving an answer before you have located the evidence for it.

## Responding to an unexpected judicial view

At a case conference, a judge may give an informal view that differs from your position. That view is not binding. It is the judge's preliminary impression based on what they have read. It can change.

The way you respond to an unexpected view matters as much as what you say.

**Appropriate response:** Calm and factual. "I understand, Your Honour. My position is [X], which is supported by the document at tab [Y]." Then stop. You have heard the judge's view, acknowledged it without dismissing it, and pointed to your evidence. That is the professional response.

**What damages credibility:** Expressing frustration with the view. Disputing the judge's authority to have it. Making emotional statements about the other party in response to a legal observation. Repeating an argument the judge has already received.

A non-binding view at a case conference is an opportunity. It tells you how the case looks to someone with authority to decide it. The SRL who treats it as information — rather than a verdict to fight — is getting something valuable.

## The things that specifically damage credibility

The Ontario Court of Justice guidance for self-represented litigants documents what courts observe and expect. The patterns that consistently harm credibility are:

- Long narrative answers to short questions
- Emotional outbursts directed at the other party (not toward the judge, but visible to the judge)
- Interrupting the judge or opposing counsel
- Referring to documents, events, or facts that are not in the record
- Repeating an argument the judge has already received

All of these are visible. All are noted. Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, unreasonable litigation conduct can produce costs orders. Conduct in the courtroom contributes to the judge's assessment of how the parties have managed the litigation.

<Callout type="warning" title="Costs under Rule 24">
  Ontario Family Law Rules, Rule 24 gives courts authority to award costs for unreasonable conduct in the proceeding. Conduct at a conference or hearing is part of the record the judge considers when assessing conduct-based costs at the end of a stage.
</Callout>

## The SRL-strategic view

Credibility is built by separating what the judge needs to know from what you need to express. Those are two different tasks, and the courtroom is not the place for the second one.

The judge needs: clear, organized, consistent, factual presentation of your position and the evidence for it.

You need: to be heard, to have your situation understood, to have the injustice acknowledged.

Those needs are real. But the courtroom addresses the first set, not the second. The SRL who goes into court knowing that distinction — and has another container for the second set — is in a structurally better position than one who tries to meet both needs from the counsel table.
