One of the most important things a common-law partner needs to understand about Ontario property law is what it does not provide.

Ontario's *Family Law Act* equalization scheme — the calculation that determines whether one spouse owes the other a payment based on net family property accumulated during the marriage — applies only to married spouses. Common-law partners are not married spouses for the purposes of Part I of the *Family Law Act*. They do not have an automatic right to equalization of the property accumulated during their relationship.

This does not mean they have no property claims. It means their claims proceed under a different legal framework, and that framework is more complex, more contested, and more expensive to pursue.

## The governing framework: unjust enrichment

When a common-law relationship ends and one partner believes they have a claim to property held in the other's name — or a greater share of jointly held property — the legal framework is not the *Family Law Act* equalization scheme. It is the equitable doctrine of unjust enrichment.

The Supreme Court of Canada addressed this directly in *Kerr v Baranow*, 2011 SCC 10. The court confirmed that unjust enrichment is the appropriate framework for common-law property claims and clarified how it applies.

The three-part test for unjust enrichment has been established in Canadian law since the 1980s and was applied and refined in *Kerr v Baranow*:

1. **An enrichment of one party** — the defendant received a benefit
2. **A corresponding deprivation of the other** — the plaintiff suffered a loss
3. **The absence of a juristic reason for the enrichment** — there is no legal reason (contract, statute, or other legal basis) that justifies the enrichment

All three must be established. If any one fails, the claim for unjust enrichment fails.

---

## The joint family venture doctrine

*Kerr v Baranow* added a significant analytical tool to how unjust enrichment is assessed in long-term common-law relationships: the joint family venture doctrine.

Where the parties have functioned as a joint economic unit — pooling resources, making joint decisions, contributing to the household and to each other's financial advancement over an extended period — the court may treat the relationship as a joint family venture. In that context, the unjust enrichment may be assessed on a proportionate basis: what share of the accumulated wealth did the claimant's contributions produce?

The joint family venture framework does not apply in every case. It is most relevant where the relationship was long-term, contributions were intermingled, and the parties functioned as genuine economic partners. The court in *Kerr v Baranow* identified four factors courts consider:

- **Mutual effort:** whether the parties worked collaboratively
- **Economic integration:** whether their financial lives were integrated
- **Actual intent:** expressed and inferred
- **Priority of the family:** whether family interests were prioritized over individual interests

These factors are assessed from the evidence of what the relationship actually looked like, not from what the parties say it was.

---

## The remedy: constructive trust

Where unjust enrichment is established, the court selects a remedy. The two main remedies are a monetary award (a dollar payment) and a constructive trust (a proprietary interest in specific property).

A constructive trust is typically granted where a monetary award would be inadequate — usually because the unjust enrichment is connected to specific property that the claimant's contributions helped build, and in which a proprietary interest is appropriate.

The result of a constructive trust is that the claimant is recognized as having a beneficial interest in the property — even though their name is not on title. That interest can then be satisfied through sale of the property, a buyout, or transfer.

---

## What this means in practice

The practical consequence of the unjust enrichment framework is that common-law property claims require:

- Evidence of contribution — financial and non-financial — over the course of the relationship
- Evidence that the contributing party suffered a deprivation
- Evidence that there is no legal justification for the other party retaining the entire benefit
- Assessment of what remedy is appropriate

This is substantially more contested than a married spouse's equalization claim, which proceeds on a defined formula. Common-law property claims require fact-specific analysis and are often determined after a full trial.

## Common-law partners and support

Spousal support obligations for common-law partners do exist under Ontario law. A common-law partner who was in a relationship of some permanence may have a support claim under Part III of the *Family Law Act* — the support provisions that apply to spouses, which include common-law partners who meet the definition in section 1(1) of the Act.

That definition includes two people who are not married but have lived together continuously for at least three years, or who have lived together in a relationship of some permanence and are the parents of a child.

Support claims by common-law partners are a separate analysis from property claims. The article on [spousal support in Ontario](/blog/spousal-support-ontario-determined) covers both the FLA and *Divorce Act* frameworks.

## Which form to file

A common-law respondent who is facing a property claim — including a constructive trust claim — files Form 13.1, not Form 13. The form is driven by the nature of the claims in the proceeding, not by whether the parties were married. The article on [Form 13 vs Form 13.1](/blog/financial-statement-13-vs-13-1-which-do-you-need) explains this logic in detail.
