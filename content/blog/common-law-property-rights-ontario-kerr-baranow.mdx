The most important thing to understand about property after a common-law separation in Ontario is this: the *Family Law Act* equalization scheme does not apply.

Equalization of net family property, the mechanism that calculates a payment between spouses based on what each accumulated during the marriage, is Part I of the *Family Law Act*, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3. Part I applies to married spouses only. Common-law partners are not within its scope.

This does not mean common-law partners have no property rights when a relationship ends. It means those rights rest on different legal principles, recognized in a different legal framework, and pursued through a different type of claim.

## The default rule

When a common-law relationship ends in Ontario, the default legal position is that each person keeps what they own. Property follows title. If the house is in one partner's name, that partner owns the house. If savings are in one partner's account, those savings belong to that partner.

This is a materially different starting point from the one that applies to married spouses, where the equalization formula adjusts for the difference in what each accumulated, regardless of whose name things are in.

The consequence is that property division between common-law partners is not automatic or formula-driven. A partner who contributed to assets held in the other partner's name, financially or through contributions of time, labour, or other kinds, must make a legal claim to establish an entitlement to share in those assets. That claim is not created by the Family Law Act. It arises under common law and equitable principles.

## Kerr v Baranow: the governing framework

The Supreme Court of Canada's decision in *Kerr v Baranow*, 2011 SCC 10, is the authoritative statement on common-law property rights in Canada. The decision established the legal framework that courts across the country, including in Ontario, apply when a common-law partner seeks a share of property held in the other partner's name.

The framework rests on two related concepts: unjust enrichment and the joint family venture.

---

## Unjust enrichment

Unjust enrichment is the foundation of the claim. For a court to find unjust enrichment, three elements must be established:

**First: an enrichment of the defendant.** The partner holding the assets was enriched. This enrichment can be financial (the claimant's contributions helped build, pay for, or preserve the asset) or it can take other forms, including contributions to household and family life that freed the other partner to build wealth.

**Second: a corresponding deprivation of the claimant.** The claimant suffered a corresponding deprivation. Their contributions benefited the other partner without compensation.

**Third: no juristic reason for the enrichment.** There is no legal justification (no contract, no gift, no statutory provision) that explains why one partner should retain the full benefit at the other's expense.

These three elements come from the broader law of unjust enrichment, which applies across many areas of law. The Supreme Court's contribution in *Kerr v Baranow* was to clarify how the doctrine operates in the context of domestic relationships, and to introduce the joint family venture analysis as a tool for quantifying the remedy.

---

## The joint family venture

*Kerr v Baranow* established that where common-law partners have conducted their relationship as a joint family venture, that context is relevant both to the finding of unjust enrichment and to the remedy.

Courts look at four broad factors to assess whether a joint family venture exists:

- **Mutual effort:** Did the parties pool their energy, work, and resources toward common goals?
- **Economic integration:** Were their financial lives substantially intertwined across the relationship?
- **Actual intent:** Did the parties conduct themselves in ways that reflected an expectation of sharing?
- **Priority of the family:** Did one or both partners subordinate individual advancement to the family's shared goals, for example, by managing the household and raising children while the other built a career?

Where a joint family venture is found, the court does not limit the remedy to a narrow accounting of specific financial contributions that can be traced dollar-for-dollar. Instead, the court may assess what proportionate share of the accumulated wealth properly reflects what was built through the joint venture.

This distinction matters because many long-term relationships involve contributions that cannot be easily quantified: one partner managing the home and caregiving while the other earns and invests, with both supporting the family's shared life and future. A strict financial accounting often understates what was contributed and built together.

---

## Constructive trust as remedy

Where unjust enrichment is established, the court determines an appropriate remedy. In many cases, the remedy is a monetary award: the defendant pays the claimant a sum reflecting the unjust enrichment.

Where a monetary award is inadequate, typically because the property has appreciated substantially, because the defendant cannot pay a cash sum, or because justice requires a share of the specific property itself, the court may impose a **constructive trust**. A constructive trust gives the claimant a recognized interest in the property, not merely a debt claim against the other partner.

The constructive trust remedy is particularly relevant where the contested asset is a family home that grew significantly in value across the relationship.

## How this connects to your proceeding

If you are a common-law partner pursuing a share of property held in the other partner's name, the claim is a property claim. It is not an equalization claim. In an Ontario family proceeding, this kind of claim is filed in the Superior Court of Justice, which has jurisdiction over property claims between common-law partners. The Ontario Court of Justice does not.

Under Rule 13 of the *Family Law Rules*, O. Reg. 114/99, a party with a property claim is required to file Form 13.1 rather than the simpler Form 13. The form determination follows the claims in the case: a property claim means Form 13.1. This is explained in the article on [Form 13 vs Form 13.1](/blog/financial-statement-13-vs-13-1-which-do-you-need).

## What this framework does not resolve

Understanding the legal framework does not tell you whether a specific person has a viable claim or what a court would award. Unjust enrichment claims are fact-intensive. The strength of the claim depends on the length of the relationship, the nature and history of contributions, how finances were managed, and what the evidence shows. Courts weigh all of these factors, and outcomes vary.

This article describes the doctrine courts apply. Whether the doctrine produces a remedy in a specific case, and what that remedy would be, is a question of applying law to particular facts.
