Not qualifying for Legal Aid Ontario does not mean facing a family court proceeding with nothing.

It means the publicly funded pathway is closed. It does not mean all other pathways are closed. A substantial number of Ontarians fall into the gap between legal aid eligibility and the ability to pay full retainer fees. That gap is not empty. This article maps what is available in it.

## First: confirm you actually don't qualify

Before treating legal aid as a closed door, confirm the specific numbers. The eligibility thresholds are often misremembered or outdated.

Family duty counsel at the courthouse — available on the day of a court event — has a net annual income threshold of $45,440 for families of up to four people (effective March 3, 2025). Many people assume they don't qualify without checking the current figure.

A legal aid certificate for ongoing representation has lower thresholds: $18,795 for a single person, rising to $45,289 for a family of four. So it is possible to qualify for duty counsel but not for a certificate.

The article on [LAO eligibility explained](/blog/legal-aid-ontario-eligibility-explained) has the full table. A call to the LAO Contact Centre at 1-800-668-8258 confirms your specific situation.

## The free baseline: available regardless of income

Some resources have no income threshold at all.

**Family Law Information Centres (FLICs).** At every Ontario family courthouse. Free. Staffed by information and referral coordinators and LAO-funded duty counsel. They provide legal information, help with forms, and can connect you with mediation. They do not provide legal advice. That distinction matters and is discussed in detail in the article on [FLICs](/blog/family-law-information-centres-flic-ontario).

**Law Society Referral Service.** One free 30-minute consultation with a licensed Ontario lawyer or paralegal. Available through [lso.ca](https://lso.ca). This is a single session, not ongoing representation. It is worth using for your most important question at the start of a case. No income test.

**Steps to Justice.** Free plain-language legal information on Ontario family law processes. Available at [stepstojustice.ca](https://stepstojustice.ca). Produced by CLEO (Centre for Law in the Public Interest).

These resources do not replace a lawyer. They reduce the cost of proceeding without one.

## JusticeNet: reduced-fee services for middle-income Ontarians

JusticeNet is a not-for-profit organization that connects people who earn too much for legal aid and too little to afford standard legal fees with lawyers, mediators, and paralegals who offer services at reduced rates.

It is specifically designed for the middle-income gap. The directory is searchable by location, practice area, and whether the professional offers remote services. JusticeNet is available at [justicenet.ca](https://justicenet.ca).

Eligibility is income-tested but at a higher threshold than LAO. It is worth checking before assuming that reduced-fee services are unavailable.

## Unbundled legal services: targeted help at specific moments

Unbundled legal services, also called limited-scope retainers, allow you to hire a lawyer for a single defined task rather than for the full case.

Examples: paying for a lawyer to review your financial statement before you file it, to coach you before a settlement conference, or to draft a specific document. You pay for that task only. You handle everything else.

The per-task cost is a fraction of a full retainer. The strategic value depends on where in the case you deploy it.

## Representing yourself: the realistic picture

Representing yourself in family court is common. Research by the National Self-Represented Litigants Project at the University of Windsor found that the majority of Ontario family court appearances involve at least one self-represented party.

It is manageable for many matters. It is more difficult for trials and complex property cases. Knowing which parts are high-risk and which are genuinely manageable is the starting question.

The SRL Playbook on this site covers the strategy of self-representation in detail. The article on [what a lawyer does that you can't](/blog/what-a-lawyer-does-family-court-srl-limits) names the specific capability gaps honestly.

## The cost-risk calculation

Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules* allows courts to award costs against parties for unreasonable litigation conduct. An SRL is not exempt. Filing motions without merit, missing disclosure obligations, and escalating unnecessarily all carry cost risk.

That is not an argument against self-representation. It is context for the cost-risk calculation. Spending $500 on a consultation before filing a contested motion is not the same as spending $500 for nothing. Sometimes it is the most efficient expenditure in the case.

## What not qualifying for legal aid is not

Not qualifying for LAO does not mean the case is unwinnable. It does not mean you are unentitled to help. And it does not mean the only option is a full retainer or nothing.

The path from here is choosing which combination of self-representation, free resources, targeted professional help, and reduced-fee services makes sense for the specific demands of your case.
