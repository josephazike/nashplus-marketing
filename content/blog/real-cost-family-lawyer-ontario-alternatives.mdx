Most people searching for a family lawyer in Ontario face a version of the same situation. They earn too much to qualify for legal aid. They cannot afford a full retainer. And they are not sure what exists in between.

There is a range. It is worth mapping clearly, because most people do not know it exists.

## The honest cost picture

Ontario family lawyers typically charge $300 to $500 per hour for fully retained representation. These are approximate figures — rates vary by location, experience, and the complexity of the matter. They reflect what most Ontario families encounter in a contested case.

A case that reaches trial can cost $30,000 to $100,000 or more in total legal fees, across both sides. Most cases do not reach trial. Many settle at or before the settlement conference. But the cases that do go to trial are almost always more expensive than the parties anticipated.

A contested family law case does not have to go to trial to be expensive. Motions, case conferences, settlement conferences, document production, and correspondence all generate legal fees at the hourly rate. A case that settles at the settlement conference after 12 months of proceedings can still cost $10,000 to $25,000 in lawyer fees.

These figures are not presented to discourage anyone from getting legal help. They are the context that makes the alternatives worth understanding.

## Free options first

Before spending anything on legal services, three free resources are available to every Ontario family law litigant.

**Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel.** At every Ontario family courthouse, Legal Aid Ontario provides duty counsel lawyers who assist on the day of your court appearance. If your net annual income is at or below $45,440 (for families of up to 4) and your assets are below $15,000, this service is free. It covers case conferences, settlement conferences, and procedural guidance. It does not cover trials or property division. Call LAO at 1-800-668-8258 to confirm current eligibility before your court date.

**Family Law Information Centres (FLICs).** Free at every Ontario family courthouse, staffed by information and referral coordinators and LAO-funded duty counsel. No income threshold. Staff can answer questions about process, forms, and next steps. They do not provide legal advice. See the [FLIC article in this cluster](/blog/family-law-information-centres-flic-ontario) for what to expect.

**Law Society Referral Service.** One free 30-minute consultation with a licensed Ontario lawyer. Not ongoing representation — one session to ask your most urgent questions. Available through the Law Society of Ontario at [lso.ca](https://lso.ca).

Use all three before spending anything.

## The middle-income gap: options when you don't qualify for legal aid

Research by the National Self-Represented Litigants Project at the University of Windsor documents that the majority of Ontario family court appearances involve at least one unrepresented party. The income gap — too high for legal aid, too stretched for a full retainer — is where most people find themselves.

Several options exist in this gap.

**JusticeNet.** A not-for-profit organization that connects middle-income Ontarians with lawyers, mediators, and paralegals who offer reduced fees. The service is for people whose income is above legal aid thresholds but who cannot afford standard legal fees. Details at [justicenet.ca](https://www.justicenet.ca).

**Unbundled legal services (limited scope retainers).** A lawyer retained for one specific task only — reviewing a document, advising before a settlement conference, drafting an order — rather than full representation. The Law Society of Ontario formally recognizes this model. A document review by a lawyer typically runs $200 to $500. A single consultation is often $150 to $500. See the [unbundled services article](/blog/unbundled-legal-services-ontario-explained) for how this works.

**Paralegal services.** Ontario paralegals are licensed by the Law Society and can represent clients at certain Ontario Court of Justice proceedings. They typically charge lower rates than lawyers. Their scope is limited — they cannot appear at the Superior Court or handle all types of family matters. Confirm whether a paralegal can appear for your specific matter before retaining one.

**Family mediation.** A mediator does not represent either party but facilitates negotiation. For cases where both parties are willing to engage, mediation can resolve issues at far lower cost than litigation. Court-connected mediation programs at some Ontario courthouses are income-tested and may be subsidized. Ask at the FLIC whether mediation services are available at your courthouse.

## What strategic spending looks like

The SRL Playbook article on [optimizing legal spend](/blog/optimize-legal-spend-srl-ontario) maps this in detail. The short version: lawyer value is highest at specific moments in a case — a strategic consultation at the start, a document review before Form 13 is filed, a pre-settlement-conference consultation to calibrate your position, a review of a draft order before you sign it.

Spending $500 on a targeted consultation before the settlement conference — specifically to understand the realistic range of outcomes in your case — addresses the moment where self-represented litigants most often make costly mistakes. The data shows SRLs go to trial at higher rates than represented parties. A realistic view of likely outcomes, obtained early, changes that trajectory.

That is not legal advice. It is an observed pattern in the research, documented by Justice Canada and the NSRLP.
