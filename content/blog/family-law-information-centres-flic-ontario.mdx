There is a free service at every Ontario family courthouse that most people in family law proceedings either do not know about or do not fully use.

Family Law Information Centres, called FLICs, are funded by the Ontario government and staffed by Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel and information and referral coordinators. They are not a backup option. They are a primary resource. They are available at no cost, regardless of income.

This article explains what they actually provide — and where the limits of that help are.

  This article provides legal information about Family Law Information Centres. It is not legal advice. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed Ontario lawyer or speak directly with duty counsel at your local FLIC.
</Callout>

## What FLICs are

A FLIC is a walk-in service centre located at an Ontario family courthouse. The exact format varies by location — some operate in a dedicated room, others from a desk in the courthouse lobby. Services may be available in person, by phone, or online, depending on the courthouse. Contact your local courthouse to confirm current hours and format.

Two types of staff work at FLICs:

**Information and referral coordinators.** These are not lawyers. They provide general information about family law processes, help people understand court forms, explain how the court system works, and provide referrals to other services like mediation and community supports. They cannot provide legal advice.

**Duty counsel lawyers.** These are licensed lawyers, funded by Legal Aid Ontario, available to provide brief legal assistance. They are governed by the LAO income and asset thresholds. For family duty counsel, the income threshold is $45,440 net annual for families of up to four people (as of March 3, 2025), with an asset threshold of $15,000.

<Callout type="note" title="Coordinators vs. duty counsel">
  The information-and-referral coordinators at a FLIC help anyone, regardless of income. The duty counsel lawyers operate under LAO eligibility thresholds. When you arrive at a FLIC, it helps to know which service you are there for.
</Callout>

## What FLICs can help with

For anyone (no income test — coordinator services):

- Explaining the family law court process
- Identifying which forms apply to your situation
- Providing general information about your rights and options
- Referring you to mediation, community legal clinics, and other services
- Providing information about court schedules and procedures

For those who meet LAO eligibility thresholds (duty counsel services):

- Reviewing forms before you file them
- Answering specific legal questions about your matter
- Explaining what is likely to happen at your court event
- Providing brief assistance on the day of a case conference, settlement conference, or first appearance
- Helping you understand an order or document you have received

---

## What FLICs cannot do

**Provide legal advice.** This distinction matters more than it might seem. Legal information tells you how the law works generally. Legal advice applies the law to your specific facts and tells you what to do. A coordinator can tell you what Form 17A is and what it requires. They cannot tell you what position to take in your conference brief.

**Provide ongoing representation.** Duty counsel at the FLIC assists with a specific court event. They are not your lawyer for the proceeding.

**Help with matters outside LAO's scope.** If your matter falls outside what family duty counsel covers — trials, property division, support matters where you receive government benefits and have assigned support entitlement to the government — duty counsel at the FLIC cannot assist with those components.

## How to use FLICs effectively

The most effective use of a FLIC is specific. Going in with a clear question produces better results than going in hoping for general orientation.

## FLICs and mediation

FLICs can refer you to family mediation services. Many Ontario courthouses have court-connected mediation programs that are free or subsidized. Mediation is not appropriate in all situations — particularly where there is a significant power imbalance or history of domestic violence — but where it is appropriate, it can resolve disputes faster and with less cost than litigation.

A FLIC coordinator can explain what mediation options are available at your courthouse and what the process looks like.

## Finding a FLIC

FLICs are located at Ontario family courthouses. To find the one nearest to you, contact LAO at **1-800-668-8258** (Monday to Friday, 8am to 5pm, available in over 300 languages) or use the service finder at [legalaid.on.ca](https://www.legalaid.on.ca). The ontario.ca website also has a court location finder.

For the broader picture of free and low-cost help available in Ontario family law, the article on [what to do if you don't qualify for legal aid](/blog/dont-qualify-legal-aid-ontario-options) maps the full range of options. The article on [LAO eligibility explained](/blog/legal-aid-ontario-eligibility-explained) covers the duty-counsel income thresholds that govern who can access the duty counsel component of FLIC services.
