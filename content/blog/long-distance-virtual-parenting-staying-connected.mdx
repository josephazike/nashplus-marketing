Distance does not end a parenting relationship. It changes its shape.

When one parent lives far from the children — in another city, another province, or another country — the logistics of parenting time change significantly. The research on what children need from a parent at a distance, and what keeps those relationships strong, points in a consistent direction.

## What the research says

A 2013 meta-analysis by Adamsons and Johnson, examining 52 studies on father involvement and child outcomes, found that the quality and consistency of involvement mattered more to child outcomes than the quantity of contact time. A parent who is reliably, attentively present during scheduled contact — even infrequent contact — is more connected to the child's life than one who is physically proximate but disengaged.

That finding has implications for how long-distance parenting is approached. The question is not only "how often" but "how meaningfully." A long weekend visit where the parent is genuinely present — in conversation, in play, in attention — is different from a week together where the parent is distracted and the child is left to their own devices.

This is information about patterns across research, not a prediction about any specific family's outcome.

## How courts approach long-distance parenting

The *Divorce Act* section 16.1 permits courts to make parenting orders that address how parenting time is structured when distance is a factor. The Act explicitly recognizes that virtual parenting time — video calls, phone calls, online communication — is a form of parenting time a court can include in an order.

Where one parent seeks to relocate with the children, the *Divorce Act* sections 16.9 and 16.93 impose a notice requirement and a process. A parent wishing to move with a child must give the other parent at least 60 days' written notice. The other parent can object. The relocating parent must then apply to court for permission, and the court applies the best interests standard to determine whether relocation with the child is appropriate.

This article addresses maintaining connection across existing distance. The relocation framework is a separate legal process that warrants its own legal advice.

## Designing parenting time across distance

Parenting time for a distant parent looks different from local parenting time. Extended blocks of time — longer school holidays, summer breaks, spring break — often form the backbone of the arrangement, supplemented by regular virtual contact.

A parenting plan or order that addresses long-distance parenting specifically should include:

<Checklist
  heading="Elements of a long-distance parenting arrangement"
  items={[
    "Extended parenting time blocks: which holidays, how long, with defined start and end dates",
    "Travel logistics: who books travel, who pays, what age the child can travel independently",
    "Virtual contact schedule: how often, what platform, what times, and what happens if a session is missed",
    "Communication during the extended visits: how the child stays in contact with the other parent",
    "Notice for schedule changes: how much advance notice is required for changes to extended visits",
    "Emergency contact: how each parent will be reached and what happens in a medical situation during a visit",
    "Review mechanism: when and how the arrangement will be reviewed as the child grows",
  ]}

## Virtual parenting time in practice

Video calls are now a standard part of long-distance parenting. Courts include virtual contact provisions in parenting orders as a matter of course.

What makes virtual contact meaningful varies by age. For a young child, a video call that is child-directed — the child shows the parent their drawings, their toys, their new book — is different from a call that feels like surveillance of the child's other household. For an older child, a call that is genuinely conversational, rather than interrogative, maintains the relationship.

<Callout type="note">
  Virtual parenting time is a recognized form of parenting time under the Divorce Act. It is typically in addition to in-person parenting time, not a substitute for it where in-person time is possible. Courts assess both in light of the child's best interests.
</Callout>

The consistent patterns in families where virtual contact works well: the calls happen on a predictable schedule, the child knows when to expect them, the parent is fully present during the call, and the other parent facilitates without hovering or directing.

## The child's experience of distance

Distance is experienced differently at different developmental stages.

Young children have limited capacity to hold a parent in mind during long absences. Frequent, brief contact — even a five-minute video call — can maintain the relationship's warmth in a way that a three-month absence followed by a two-week visit does not. Transitional objects — a stuffed animal, a recording of the parent reading a book — can bridge the gap at this stage.

School-age children can understand why a parent lives far away and can maintain a more cognitive sense of the relationship during absences. Written communication — cards, emails, letters — supplements calls and provides something tangible to return to.

Adolescents are navigating their own identity and social world. A long-distance parenting relationship at this stage depends heavily on whether the parent is genuinely interested in the teenager's life — their interests, their friendships, their concerns — rather than primarily focused on the parenting schedule.

## The emotional weight on parents

Parenting at a distance carries its own particular emotional load. The feeling of missing ordinary moments — the school play, the first lost tooth, the Tuesday dinner — accumulates differently than the challenges of day-to-day co-parenting conflict.
