Consider two people with the same case — same facts, same claims, same court. One retains a lawyer for the entire proceeding. The other spends roughly a tenth of that, targeted at the three moments in the case where professional legal judgment changes outcomes most, and handles everything else.

The outcome difference is not always what people expect.

This is not an argument against lawyers. It is an argument for thinking about legal spend the way a strategist thinks, not the way a frightened person thinks. When you are afraid, you want all the help or none of it. When you are strategic, you ask a different question: **which specific moments in this case are changed by having professional legal input?**

## The honest picture on costs

Ontario family lawyers typically charge hourly rates in the range of $300 to $500 for fully retained representation. These figures are approximate — rates vary by location, experience level, and the complexity of the matter — but the range reflects what most Ontario families with contested family law cases encounter.

A case that reaches trial can cost $30,000 to $100,000 or more, in total fees for both sides combined. Most cases do not reach trial. Many settle at or before the settlement conference. But the ones that do reach trial are almost always more expensive than the parties anticipated, and they frequently arrive there because earlier settlement opportunities were not used well.

The economic reality is not a reason to avoid lawyers entirely. It is the context that makes the strategic question necessary.

## Where professional legal help changes outcomes most

Not all legal work in a family case requires the same depth of expertise. Some tasks involve legal judgment calls that years of training make materially better. Others are administrative tasks with clear rules that any organized person can perform.

The moments where professional help tends to change outcomes most fall into a distinct pattern.

**Early strategic assessment.** At the start of a case, a single consultation with an experienced family lawyer can clarify what is legally realistic and what is not. This has practical value: SRLs who overestimate their position tend to decline settlements that a lawyer would have advised accepting. SRLs who underestimate their position sometimes settle for less than the law would have provided. One hour with someone who knows Ontario family law well enough to map your specific facts is rarely wasted.

**Financial disclosure review.** Form 13 and Form 13.1 — the financial statements required in most Ontario family proceedings — are sworn documents. Errors in them do not stay errors; they become credibility problems under cross-examination. Having a lawyer review a completed Form 13 or 13.1 before it is filed, specifically to identify errors and gaps, is a targeted task with a targeted value. It does not require a full retainer.

**Settlement conference preparation.** The settlement conference is typically the highest-leverage moment in an Ontario family case. It is where judges sometimes give non-binding assessments of likely outcomes, where parties are expected to have a real settlement conversation, and where cases most often resolve. An SRL who arrives without a realistic sense of their best alternative to a negotiated agreement — informed by how courts actually decide cases like theirs — is at a structural disadvantage. A pre-conference consultation to calibrate that position is low-cost relative to what a poor outcome at this stage means for the rest of the case.

**Reviewing a draft order before signing.** A signed order is difficult to undo. The language of each clause determines how it is enforced — by the Family Responsibility Office for support orders, by a return to court for everything else. Having a lawyer review a draft order for enforceability and precision, before consent is given, is one of the highest-value limited tasks available to an SRL.

**Any trial preparation.** If a case reaches trial, the evidence rules alone — what is admissible, how to object, how to get a document into evidence, how to cross-examine — require genuine legal training. This is where the gap between SRL and represented parties is most concrete.

## Where the gap is genuinely small

An honest map of legal spend includes the tasks where the capability gap between a lawyer and an organized SRL is small.

Filing and serving documents, once you have done it once, is a procedural task with clear rules. Updating the continuing record requires diligence and organization, not legal judgment. Written communication with the other party — factual, dated, calm — is something any capable adult can do well. Gathering and organizing financial records is time-consuming but not legally complex.

Routine conference briefs, after the first one, follow a pattern. The Family Law Information Centre at your courthouse can review a draft conference brief for completeness; duty counsel is present specifically to help SRLs with documents like these.

The principle is: if the task is governed by a clear rule with a clear answer, the gap is small. If the task requires judgment about what the law would produce in your specific factual circumstances, the gap is large.

## The Rule 24 risk you may not know about

There is a cost exposure that most self-represented litigants are unaware of until it materializes.

Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, courts have authority to award costs against a party whose litigation conduct is unreasonable. This includes bringing motions without reasonable cause, failing to comply with disclosure obligations, and conducting proceedings in a way that causes unnecessary delay or expense for the other side.

<Callout type="warning" title="Costs orders under Rule 24">
  Rule 24 of the Family Law Rules gives courts authority to award costs for unreasonable litigation conduct. SRLs are not exempt. A motion filed without sufficient basis, or procedural non-compliance that causes the other party additional cost, can result in a costs order against you.
</Callout>

A lawyer calibrates the costs risk of every action they take in a proceeding, often invisibly to the client. An SRL bears that judgment themselves. This is not a reason to be passive — it is a reason to be deliberate. Before filing a motion or taking an escalating step, the question worth asking is whether the step is proportionate and legally grounded, not just emotionally justified.

## The trial pipeline problem

Research by the National Self-Represented Litigants Project at the University of Windsor, and confirmed in Justice Canada's review of Canadian court data, shows that self-represented litigants are more likely than represented parties to end up at trial rather than settling. They also fare worse at trial, on average, than parties with counsel.

Both of these findings reflect, at least in part, a common pattern: SRLs who lack a realistic assessment of what a court would decide find it harder to identify and accept a reasonable settlement. Early legal spend — a single consultation at the settlement conference stage, specifically to calibrate the likely range of outcomes — can break that pattern.

A $500 consultation before the settlement conference that helps an SRL understand the realistic range of possible outcomes, and settle within that range, may be the most efficient legal expenditure available to them. The alternative is a trial that costs many times more, with a worse expected outcome.

## The free baseline — before you spend anything

Before spending on limited-scope services, three free options are worth using fully.

**Family Law Information Centres (FLICs).** Present at Ontario family courthouses, staffed by information and referral coordinators and Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel. Free. Duty counsel can review documents and answer procedural questions — not provide ongoing representation, but answer the specific question in front of you on that day.

**Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel.** For those who meet the eligibility threshold (net family income approximately $45,440 for families up to four, asset threshold $15,000 as of March 3, 2025), duty counsel at the courthouse provides free brief legal assistance at case conferences and settlement conferences. Call LAO at **1-800-668-8258** to confirm eligibility before your court date.

**Law Society Referral Service.** One free 30-minute consultation with a licensed Ontario lawyer. Use it for your highest-priority question. Available through [lso.ca](https://lso.ca).
