There is a particular friction in trying to remember, in the middle of a court hearing, exactly what was agreed about a pickup time six weeks ago. Whether the agreement was in a text, in an email, or in a conversation that one parent says happened differently than the other recalls.

Co-parenting apps exist to address that friction. They are communication platforms designed specifically for separated parents — with logging, timestamping, and exportable records built in by design.

This article explains what these tools do, what features exist across the category, and the questions worth asking before choosing one.

## What co-parenting apps do

At the core, all co-parenting apps do a version of the same thing: they host communication between two parents in a logged, timestamped environment. Messages cannot be deleted after being sent. The platform keeps an unalterable record.

That basic function — an unalterable, exportable record — is the feature that distinguishes these tools from a standard text conversation.

Most platforms expand on that core with some combination of:

**Shared calendar.** A visual calendar where both parents can see scheduled parenting time, school events, activities, and important dates. Changes are logged.

**Expense tracking.** A system for documenting child-related expenses, requesting reimbursement, and keeping a record of payments made or disputed.

**Secure messaging.** In-app messaging that is separate from SMS or email. All messages are timestamped and stored on the platform's servers.

**Communication tone tools.** Some platforms include features that flag or rewrite messages in inflammatory language before sending. The specific implementations vary by platform.

**Calling with logging.** Some platforms offer in-app calling where call duration and sometimes content is logged.

**Certified records.** Most platforms produce exportable PDF records of communication history. Some describe these as "certified" or "tamper-resistant" records. What that means in practice varies by platform and has not been specifically adjudicated in Ontario courts.

**Third-party access.** Some platforms allow lawyers, therapists, or mediators to view communication records with the parents' consent.

## The pricing model varies significantly

Some platforms price per parent — each parent pays a separate monthly subscription. Others price per family — one subscription covers both parents. The practical difference: a per-parent model costs both parents, which matters when one parent is unwilling or unable to subscribe. Some platforms have solo modes that let one parent use the platform to log their own records even without the other parent's participation.

Pricing also varies by tier. Entry-level plans typically include messaging, calendar, and basic record export. Higher tiers add features like in-app calling, extended storage, and premium record formats.

Fee waiver programs exist on several platforms for people experiencing financial hardship or domestic violence. These are worth looking for if cost is a barrier.

## Ontario courts and co-parenting apps

Some Ontario courts have referenced co-parenting communication tools in parenting orders — requiring parties to use a specific platform, or noting the use of one as a factor in the communication history. There is no Ontario rule or standard that mandates a specific platform. The platforms are tools, not legal documents.

What matters legally is the record the platform produces and whether that record is reliable evidence. A communication log that can be exported as a PDF, with timestamps, and where neither party can alter or delete messages after sending, has observable evidentiary value. Whether a specific court will admit a specific platform's records, and how much weight to give them, depends on the circumstances of the specific case.

Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, courts can take into account unreasonable litigation conduct when awarding costs. A platform that reduces escalation and maintains a clear record may reduce the litigation friction that generates that conduct. That is not a guarantee of any outcome. It is information about what the record can show.

## When a co-parenting app may not be the answer

Platforms help with the record. They do not resolve the underlying dynamic.

A parent who is willing to use a co-parenting app to document every disagreement as evidence may be doing something different from a parent using it to reduce communication friction. A judge reading the record can often tell the difference.
