Child support in Ontario follows a two-part structure. The first part, the table amount, is largely mechanical: a number set by the Federal Child Support Guidelines based on the payor's income and the number of children. Look it up, and that is the amount.

The second part is section 7 special and extraordinary expenses. These are the costs over and above the table amount, child care, medical costs, education, extracurricular activities, that parents share in proportion to their incomes. This is where most child support disagreements actually happen, because these expenses require ongoing decisions about what is reasonable, what is extraordinary, and how income is measured.

Understanding both parts is essential for anyone dealing with child support in Ontario.

## The legal framework

Child support orders in divorce proceedings are made under section 15.1 of the *Divorce Act*, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.). Child support for children of common-law partners or in non-divorce proceedings is addressed under Part III of the *Family Law Act*, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3.

The **Federal Child Support Guidelines**, SOR/97-175, govern the calculation of child support amounts in both divorce and non-divorce proceedings in Ontario. The Guidelines set the table amounts and establish the framework for special expenses.

---

## The table amount: how it works

Section 3 of the Federal Child Support Guidelines requires the paying parent to pay the amount set out in the applicable table. The table amount depends on:

- **The payor's annual income** (gross income, with adjustments described below)
- **The number of children** for whom support is owed
- **The province of the payor's residence** (Ontario has its own schedule within the federal tables)

The Department of Justice Canada provides a child support table lookup tool at [justice.gc.ca](https://www.justice.gc.ca) that calculates the current table amount for any income and number of children. No specific dollar figures are cited here because the tables change over time and the exact amount depends on the current year's schedule and the specific income figure.

**How payor income is determined.** The starting point is total income as reported on line 15000 of the payor's most recent Notice of Assessment. The Guidelines then require adjustments for certain items, for example, adding back depreciation claimed on a business, or adjusting for RRSP withdrawals that are not recurring income. Where the payor is self-employed or earns income through a corporation, courts may look beyond the declared income figure to assess what the payor actually has available. Imputed income, where a court attributes a higher income than what is declared, is possible where income appears to have been artificially reduced.

---

## Shared parenting and section 9

Where the child or children spend 40 percent or more of their time with each parent, section 9 of the Federal Child Support Guidelines gives courts discretion to depart from the straight table calculation. Courts may consider the amounts in the table for each parent based on their respective incomes, the increased costs of shared parenting arrangements, and the conditions and means of the parties and children.

The 40 percent threshold is calculated over the course of a year. It is not enough to average out to 40 percent on paper; the actual pattern of parenting time matters. Where shared parenting is found, courts may set off the respective table amounts, order a reduced amount, or make other arrangements that reflect the actual division of child-related costs.

---

## Section 7: special and extraordinary expenses

Section 7 of the Federal Child Support Guidelines is the second layer of child support. It covers specific categories of expenses that go beyond what the table amount is intended to cover.

    "Child care expenses incurred as a result of the paying parent's employment, illness, disability, or education or training for employment",
    "That portion of medical and dental insurance premiums attributable to the child",
    "Health-related expenses that exceed insurance reimbursement by more than $100 annually, including orthodontic treatment, professional counselling, or other health services",
    "Extraordinary expenses for primary or secondary school education, or for educational programs that meet the child's particular needs",
    "Post-secondary education expenses",
    "Extraordinary expenses for extracurricular activities"
  ]}
/>

**The proportional sharing rule.** Section 7 expenses are not split 50/50. Each parent contributes an amount proportionate to their income, taking into account any child support table amount already being paid and any subsidies, tax deductions, or credits available for the expense. If one parent earns twice as much as the other, that parent's share of a section 7 expense will reflect that income difference.

**"Extraordinary" expenses.** For extracurricular activities and some education expenses, the Guidelines require that the expense be extraordinary before it qualifies for sharing under section 7. Courts consider the amount relative to the parents' incomes, the nature of the activity, and the child's particular circumstances. Routine swimming lessons may not qualify; intensive competitive training for a child with identified talent in a particular area may. What is extraordinary in one family's financial context is not extraordinary in another's.

**Prospective vs incurred expenses.** Parents can ask the court to address anticipated recurring section 7 expenses (like child care costs) as part of the initial order, or they can agree on a mechanism for sharing actual expenses as they arise. Both approaches are used. Where the parties cannot agree on whether an expense qualifies or what each parent's share is, a motion or application is required.

---

## How income is measured for section 7

Each parent's proportionate share of a section 7 expense is based on their respective incomes, using the same income-determination framework as the table amount. Where one parent's income has changed since the last order or agreement, the proportions may need to be recalculated.

---

## Child support is not negotiated away

Child support belongs to the child, not to the parents. Courts will not approve a separation agreement that purports to waive a child's support rights in exchange for something between the parents. The table amount is a floor, not a starting point for negotiation downward. A parent cannot agree to accept less support than the Guidelines require and have that agreement enforced against a future variation application.

---

## Updating support when income changes

Child support table amounts are based on current income. When either parent's income changes materially, either party may apply to vary the child support order. In Ontario, parties are required to annually exchange income information, Notices of Assessment, tax returns, or similar documents, so that the table amount can be reviewed and adjusted as needed.

## Connecting the broader picture

Child support calculations require financial disclosure from both parents. The financial statement filed is Form 13 for support-only proceedings, or Form 13.1 if property claims are also in issue.
