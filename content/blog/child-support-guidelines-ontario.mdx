Child support in Ontario is determined by the Federal Child Support Guidelines for cases under the *Divorce Act*, and by an equivalent provincial framework for non-divorcing cases under the *Children's Law Reform Act*. Both frameworks use the same tables and the same general structure.

The calculation has two parts: the base table amount and the special expenses under section 7. The first is largely mechanical. The second is where most of the substantive dispute lives.

## The legal basis

Under section 15.1 of the *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.)*, a court may make an order for the support of children of the marriage. The amount must be determined in accordance with the applicable guidelines — which, for most cases involving children habitually resident in Ontario, means the Federal Child Support Guidelines, SOR/97-175.

For non-divorce proceedings (common-law separations, custody disputes outside of divorce), Ontario's *Children's Law Reform Act* also requires courts to apply the Federal Child Support Guidelines framework.

---

## The table amount

The starting point for child support is the table amount. The Federal Child Support Guidelines include tables for each province and territory, organized by the number of children and the payor's gross annual income.

The table amount is the base amount owed by the payor to the recipient for the child's ordinary living expenses. It is not the full picture of child support. But it is the floor.

Three things determine the table amount:

1. **The province of residence** of the payor (or, in some cases, where the children live)
2. **The number of children** for whom support is being paid
3. **The payor's gross annual income**

The tables are updated periodically. Current tables are available at the Department of Justice Canada website ([justice.gc.ca](https://www.justice.gc.ca)). The guidelines provide a formula for incomes above the highest table entry.

<Callout type="note" title="Shared parenting time and table amounts">
  Where a child spends 40% or more of their time with the payor, the table amount can be adjusted. Under section 9 of the Federal Child Support Guidelines, courts in a split-time arrangement may consider the increased costs of the payor, the amounts set out in the applicable tables for each household, and the conditions and means of each party.
</Callout>

## Section 7 special or extraordinary expenses

Beyond the table amount, section 7 of the Federal Child Support Guidelines addresses special or extraordinary expenses. These are shared between the parents in proportion to their respective incomes, in addition to the table amount.

The expenses covered by section 7(1) are:

**(a)** Child care expenses incurred by reason of the employment, illness, disability, or education or training of the primary caregiver

**(b)** The portion of medical and dental insurance premiums attributable to the child

**(c)** Health-related expenses exceeding insurance reimbursement by more than $100 annually — including orthodontics, counselling, therapy, prescription drugs, hearing aids, and vision care

**(d)** Extraordinary educational expenses for primary or secondary school

**(e)** Post-secondary education expenses

**(f)** Extraordinary extracurricular activity expenses

## Why s.7 is where disputes happen

The table amount is determined by numbers that are relatively verifiable: income, number of children. The dispute over the table itself is usually about income — what is the payor's actual income, especially if they are self-employed or have a corporate structure.

Section 7 introduces two additional layers of dispute:

**Necessity and reasonableness.** Section 7 expenses must be "necessary" and "reasonable" given the means of both parties. A private-school tuition may be a section 7 expense if the family was in a private-school lifestyle before separation; it may not be if the family was not. Courts assess these questions case by case.

**Extraordinary vs. ordinary.** Not every extracurricular activity is a s.7 expense. The guidelines distinguish between ordinary expenses (covered by the table amount, which is already designed to cover the baseline costs of raising a child) and "extraordinary" expenses that go beyond that baseline. Whether a specific expense is extraordinary is a factual question that courts answer by looking at the amount, the nature of the activity, and the family's pre-separation pattern.

## Imputing income

Where a payor's declared income does not reflect their actual capacity to earn, courts may impute income — attribute a higher income figure to the payor for the purpose of the Guidelines calculation.

Grounds for imputation include: voluntary underemployment, failure to pursue available employment, unreasonable deductions claimed through a corporation, and other circumstances where declared income does not reflect earning capacity.

Income imputation is a significant issue in cases involving self-employed payors, business owners, and parties with corporate structures. The article on [financial disclosure in Ontario family law](/blog/financial-disclosure-family-law) addresses how income documentation works in this context.

## Child support does not require a lawyer to calculate the table amount

The table amount is mechanical. Once income and the number of children are established, the table produces a number. The Department of Justice's child support table lookup tool is publicly available and widely used.

Where a lawyer adds meaningful value is in contested income questions (imputation, corporate income), s.7 disputes, shared-time arrangements, and cases where a payor's income is difficult to establish from tax returns alone. The article on [optimizing legal spend as an SRL](/blog/optimize-legal-spend-srl-ontario) addresses those judgment points.
