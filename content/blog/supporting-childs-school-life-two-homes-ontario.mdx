When parents separate, a child's school does not automatically know what the new arrangements are. Most schools have one emergency contact form and one home address on file. The administrative reality of the child's life in two homes is often invisible to the school until a parent makes it visible.

This article addresses how school communication, records, and educational decisions work when parents are no longer living together — and what each parent can generally do to stay involved.

## Both parents generally have the right to school information

Unless a court order says otherwise, both parents generally retain the right to receive information about their child from the school, regardless of how parenting time is divided. This applies to report cards, school communications, teacher meetings, and access to the child's school records.

Ontario's *Education Act* and the *Divorce Act* section 16.8 both address information sharing with parents. Under the *Divorce Act*, a parent is entitled to information about the child's well-being — including education — regardless of whether they have decision-making responsibility, unless a court order restricts this.

<Callout type="note" title="What to tell the school">
  Schools cannot determine parental rights from a verbal description of the separation. The most practical step is to provide the school with a copy of any parenting order or agreement, notify the school office that two households are now involved, and ask the school's process for ensuring both parents receive communications.
</Callout>

Providing the school with a copy of the parenting order or separation agreement — or at minimum, with written notification that both parents are to receive communications — is the most reliable way to ensure both parents stay informed.

## Decision-making responsibility and education

Under the *Divorce Act* s.16.1, decision-making responsibility encompasses significant decisions about a child's education: which school the child attends, whether to pursue special education assessments, decisions about gifted programs or French immersion, major tutoring arrangements, and similar choices.

Who holds decision-making responsibility for education matters when the parents disagree. If one parent holds sole decision-making responsibility for education, that parent makes final decisions. If both parents hold shared decision-making responsibility for education, they must agree, or return to court if they cannot.

Day-to-day school decisions — whether the child does their homework at the kitchen table or in their room, what after-school snack they eat — are made by whichever parent the child is with. These are not "education decisions" in the legal sense.

## Practical elements to address in a parenting plan

A parenting plan or order that addresses school logistics specifically prevents a significant source of future conflict.

  heading="School-related provisions worth including in a parenting plan"

    "How the parents will communicate about school-related decisions, and what the process is if they disagree",
    "Which parent handles school pickup on which days under the regular parenting schedule",

## Parent-teacher meetings and school events

Unless a court order restricts contact, both parents are generally entitled to attend parent-teacher meetings and school events. Whether both attend the same meeting or separate meetings is a practical arrangement between the parents and the school.

Many schools will accommodate two separate parent-teacher appointments for separated parents who cannot share a meeting productively. Asking the school directly is the most direct path to arranging this.

School events — assemblies, concerts, sports days — present a different situation. Both parents may attend. The child benefits from both parents being present. The way the adults conduct themselves at these events is part of the evidence, informal as it is, about each parent's ability to put the child's interests first.

---

## When a parent is excluded from school information

If a parent discovers that the school is sending communications to only one parent and not the other, and no court order restricts that access, the first step is to contact the school directly and provide documentation of the parenting arrangement.

If a parenting order exists that grants information rights to both parents and the school is not complying, the relevant provision of the order can be provided to the school. Schools are not family courts and may not be familiar with the legal framework. Clear, written communication with documentation is more effective than conflict.

If one parent is actively preventing the other from receiving school information in violation of a court order, that is a matter for a lawyer and potentially a motion to enforce the order.
