A judge at a case conference has usually read your brief before you walk in. Their agenda is not to hear you explain everything. It is to identify what is disputed, what is agreed, and what happens next.

The SRL who knows this — and prepares for that specific agenda — is in a fundamentally different position from one who spent three days reviewing their full case history.

This article is built around three preparation questions, a breakdown of what different court events actually require, and a practical framework for the day before and the day of any appearance.

## Know what type of appearance this is

Different court events have entirely different purposes. Preparing the same way for all of them is a consistent SRL mistake. The judge at a case conference and the judge at a motion have different agendas. What you need to bring and what you will be asked to do differ significantly.

## The three-question method

Before every court appearance, three questions define what preparation is complete.

**Question 1: What specifically do I want the judge to do today?**

One sentence. Not "I want to explain my situation." Not "I want the judge to understand what has happened." A specific, concrete request: "I am asking for a parenting schedule of every other week." "I am asking for an order requiring the other party to produce their 2023 tax return." If the answer takes more than one sentence, the preparation is not complete.

**Question 2: What is my legal basis?**

Which rule, statute, or principle supports what you are asking for? For conference appearances, the purpose under Rule 17 of the *Family Law Rules*. For motions, the specific provision being relied upon. For financial matters, the relevant section of the *Divorce Act* or *Family Law Act*. Not a full legal argument — a one-sentence basis.

**Question 3: Which document proves it, and what tab is it at?**

Every factual claim you make should be in the record. If you say the other party has not filed a financial statement, you need to know where in the record that absence is documented (or provable by reference to what is not there). If you are relying on an email, it needs to be in an affidavit, in the record, at a tab you know.

If you cannot answer all three quickly, the preparation is not done. That is not harsh. It is the diagnostic.

## Conference briefs: what Form 17A is actually communicating

Form 17A is the case conference brief. It tells the judge, before the conference begins: the family history, what is agreed, what is disputed, what each party is asking for, and what disclosure has or has not been exchanged.

Most SRLs fill it out mechanically. The more useful approach is to understand what a judge is looking for in each section.

<Callout type="warning" title="Form 17F — confirmation of conference">
  Both parties must file Form 17F (the confirmation of conference) at least 3 days before any conference. Missing this is a common SRL error. The conference may be cancelled or the case may not be called. Build this into your calendar as a mandatory step, not an afterthought.
</Callout>

## Courtroom protocol: the factual basics

The Ontario Court of Justice's guide for self-represented litigants sets out the procedural expectations. Most of them are not complicated. All of them are visible.

- **Address the judge:** "Your Honour" (Ontario Court of Justice) or "Justice [surname]" (Superior Court of Justice)
- **Arrive early:** 30 minutes for in-person; 15 minutes for virtual. The court calls matters in order. Being absent or late when your matter is called creates a bad start.
- **Do not interrupt:** Wait to be invited to speak. When you want to respond to something, write it down. You will have a turn.
- **Speak slowly and clearly:** Judges take notes. Give them time to keep up.
- **Bring organized document copies:** The court cannot photocopy for you. Bring copies for the judge and the other party.

At trial specifically, there is a structural distinction that most SRLs do not know about until they get it wrong:

<Callout type="note" title="Witness box vs. counsel table">
  At trial, you speak from one of two places — the witness box (when giving evidence, where you can be cross-examined) or the counsel table (when making legal submissions based on evidence already in the record). You cannot do both at the same time. Introducing new facts during your submissions — rather than during your evidence — is a procedural error that damages credibility with the judge.
</Callout>

## The day-before preparation routine

<Checklist
  heading="Day-of-appearance checklist"
  items={[

    "Verify the correct court address, room number, and arrival time",
    "Pack: continuing record binder, copies for the judge and the other party, a pen and notepad",
    "Write out your three-question answers (what you want, your legal basis, where the document is) on a single card",
    "Prepare written answers to the three questions the judge is most likely to ask you",
    "If it is a virtual appearance: test the technology the night before; have your documents open and findable on screen",
    "If you have not visited the FLIC in the week before: go in and let duty counsel review your brief for completeness",
    "Read your brief aloud once. You will catch things silent reading missed.",
  ]}

The Family Law Information Centre at your courthouse is available before any court appearance. Duty counsel can review your brief for completeness — not provide ongoing representation, but check whether what you have prepared is procedurally sound. It is free. Most SRLs do not use it.

## The SRL-strategic view

Court preparation is not about knowing everything. It is about knowing exactly what you are asking for and being able to show the judge where the evidence for it lives.

The judge at a case conference is not trying to find against you. They are trying to move the case forward. An SRL who knows what they want, can explain it clearly, and can find the document that supports it is providing exactly what the conference is designed to produce.
