When people look up Legal Aid Ontario for the first time, they often encounter two terms — duty counsel and legal aid certificate — and assume they mean the same thing. They do not.

They are different services, with different income thresholds, different application processes, and different coverage. Someone who qualifies for one may not qualify for the other. Knowing the difference before you apply is the first step toward getting the right kind of help.

## The two main types of LAO service

Legal Aid Ontario provides two primary types of legal assistance for family law matters.

**Family duty counsel** is a lawyer at the courthouse, available to you on the day of your court appearance or before it. Duty counsel provides brief, same-day assistance: reviewing documents, answering procedural questions, helping you understand what is happening at your court event. It is not ongoing representation.

**A legal aid certificate** authorizes you to retain a private lawyer for ongoing representation. The lawyer is paid by LAO up to a specified number of hours. A certificate is ongoing assistance, not day-of assistance.

The income thresholds for these two services are different. This is the source of most confusion.

---

## Duty counsel: who qualifies

<Callout type="note" title="Duty counsel thresholds (effective March 3, 2025)">
  Net annual income threshold: $45,440 for families of up to 4 people. For families of 5 or more, the threshold is higher (confirm the current figure directly with LAO at 1-800-668-8258). Asset threshold: $15,000 regardless of family size.
</Callout>

These figures are net annual income — after taxes, not gross. The asset threshold is separate from income: you can have a relatively higher income and still be disqualified by assets over $15,000, or have low income but assets above the limit.

Certain amounts are excluded from the income calculation. Payments related to residential school settlements, Indigenous land claims, and federal health compensation programs do not count toward the income threshold.

## Legal aid certificates: who qualifies

The certificate income thresholds are substantially lower than the duty counsel thresholds. This is the part of LAO eligibility that most people do not know.

A person who earns $38,000 a year with a family of two may qualify for duty counsel at the courthouse. That same person likely does not qualify for a legal aid certificate for ongoing representation.

This gap is significant. It means a substantial group of Ontarians can get brief help at court but cannot get funded ongoing representation. Understanding which side of each threshold you fall on determines what to ask for when you call LAO.

---

## Certificate eligibility also requires merit

Financial eligibility alone is not enough to receive a certificate. The legal matter must also have merit. LAO assesses whether:

- The case involves a type of matter that LAO covers (custody, access, support, restraining orders, property in some circumstances)
- There is a reasonable basis for the position being taken
- The outcome would be material to the applicant

Where LAO issues a certificate to one party in a proceeding, they generally also issue one to the other party if that party is financially eligible and their case has merit.

---

## Priority groups

Regardless of income, LAO gives priority to:

- Individuals currently in custody
- Survivors of intimate partner violence
- People with mental health issues or addictions
- Indigenous and racialized community members
- Cases involving child protection
- Cases involving the safety of a spouse or child

Priority status does not eliminate the income threshold for certificates. But it affects how quickly and readily LAO responds to an application.

## How to find out if you qualify

The most direct path is to call the LAO Contact Centre at **1-800-668-8258**, Monday to Friday, 8am to 5pm. Service is available in over 300 languages. LAO also has an online application tool and a live chat function (Monday to Friday, 9am to 5pm) for people who prefer not to call.

The LAO website at [legalaid.on.ca](https://www.legalaid.on.ca) includes a service finder to locate duty counsel offices, legal clinics, and FLIC locations.
