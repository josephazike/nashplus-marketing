The gap between "I can't afford a lawyer" and "I know what I'm doing" is real and wide. Most people discover just how wide when they are standing at a court counter for the first time, holding a form they have never seen, unsure whether they are even in the right building.

This article does not start with the forms. It starts earlier — with the questions whose answers determine everything else.

## What kind of case do you actually have?

Not all Ontario family law cases are the same, and not all of them belong in the same court under the same law. Before anything else, it helps to know which category your situation falls into.

**If you were married and are seeking a divorce**, the governing legislation is federal: the *Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.)*. Only the Superior Court of Justice (or its Family Court branch, where available) can grant a divorce. The *Divorce Act* covers divorce itself, as well as parenting arrangements and support for married spouses who are separating by way of divorce.

**If you were married but are not seeking a divorce** — or if your case involves only support or property claims — the *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3* governs property and support between married spouses in Ontario. The Ontario Court of Justice can hear support and parenting matters; property and equalization claims require the Superior Court.

**If you were never married**, one of the most important things to know about Ontario law is this: common-law partners do not have automatic property equalization rights. The *Family Law Act* equalization scheme applies to married spouses only. Common-law partners may have claims under other legal principles — constructive trust, unjust enrichment — but those are different claims, with different tests, and they require the Superior Court. On parenting and child support, the *Children's Law Reform Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.12* applies regardless of whether you were married.

## Which court is yours?

Ontario has two main trial courts that hear family law matters.

**The Ontario Court of Justice** handles most parenting, access, child support, and spousal support matters. It does not have jurisdiction over property division or the equalization of net family property. For many straightforward support or parenting cases, it is the appropriate starting point.

**The Superior Court of Justice** has full jurisdiction, including property division, divorce, and anything the Ontario Court of Justice can hear. If your case involves property — the matrimonial home, RRSPs, pensions, business interests — the Superior Court is where it belongs.

In some Ontario locations, the Ontario Court of Justice and the Superior Court sit together as the **Family Court branch** of the Superior Court of Justice. Where this exists, one filing covers matters from both courts. Not every city has a Family Court branch; you can confirm which court sits in your location through the Ontario court finder at [ontario.ca/page/find-ontario-court-location](https://www.ontario.ca/page/find-ontario-court-location).

Filing in the wrong court does not end a case, but it creates delays, wasted filing fees, and a first impression that costs you nothing to avoid.

## The first step is not a form — it is a process

Before a case is filed in court, the *Divorce Act* (for married parties seeking divorce) requires parties to consider family dispute resolution — mediation, negotiation, collaborative family law — where it is safe and appropriate to do so. This obligation, added in the 2021 amendments to the *Divorce Act*, reflects what family law practitioners have known for years: most family disputes are resolved outside of a courtroom, and a negotiated resolution is almost always faster, cheaper, and less damaging to the family than litigation.

This does not mean court is wrong for your case. Some disputes cannot be resolved by agreement. Some situations — domestic violence, a party who refuses to engage — make dispute resolution inappropriate or unsafe. The law recognizes this. The point is to consider it deliberately, not to skip it by default.

Family mediation in Ontario ranges from free to income-tested subsidized rates at court-connected programs. The Family Law Information Centre at your local courthouse can provide referrals.

## What filing actually opens

When an application is filed, two things happen immediately that most self-represented litigants do not know about until later.

The first is service. The applicant must serve the filed application on the respondent within the time limits set out in the Family Law Rules. Service has specific rules — who can serve, how it must be done, and what proof is required. Getting this wrong delays the case.

The second is the continuing record. Under Rule 9 of the *Family Law Rules*, the applicant is responsible for creating the official court record — the single document that becomes the court's permanent file for the case. Every document filed after that must be added to it. It has two volumes: an endorsement volume (yellow cover) for orders and judicial endorsements, and a documents volume (red cover) for applications, financial statements, affidavits, and motions. The parties, not the court, are responsible for maintaining it.

This is the administrative spine of your case. A well-maintained continuing record makes every court appearance smoother. A disorganized one creates friction at the moments when you can least afford it.

## Free resources before you spend anything

There is more free help available than most self-represented litigants know about. The resources below are real, funded, and useful.

**Family Law Information Centres (FLICs).** Every Ontario family courthouse has one. Staff provide general information about the family law process, help identifying forms, and referrals to mediation and other services. Duty counsel lawyers, provided by Legal Aid Ontario, are available at FLICs to review documents and answer procedural questions. FLICs do not provide legal advice — they provide legal information. That distinction matters, but so does the access. It is free.

**Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel.** Duty counsel are lawyers at the courthouse, funded by Legal Aid Ontario, who provide brief assistance on the day of a court appearance. As of March 3, 2025, income eligibility for LAO family duty counsel services is a net monthly income threshold of approximately $45,440 per year for families up to four people, and an asset threshold of $15,000 regardless of family size. Duty counsel cover early and resolution stages of a case — case conferences, settlement conferences, procedural guidance. They do not provide assistance at trial, with property division, or with support matters where the client receives government benefits. If you think you may qualify, it is worth calling the LAO Contact Centre at **1-800-668-8258** (Monday to Friday, 8am to 5pm, available in over 300 languages) before your first court date.

**The Law Society Referral Service.** The Law Society of Ontario offers one free 30-minute consultation with a licensed lawyer or paralegal. This is not ongoing representation — it is one session to ask your most urgent questions. It is worth using. The referral service is available through the Law Society's website at [lso.ca](https://lso.ca).

**Steps to Justice.** The CLEO-produced plain-language guide to Ontario's legal system. The family law section includes flowcharts, plain-language explanations, and guided pathways for completing forms. Available at [stepstojustice.ca](https://stepstojustice.ca).

## Where this leads

Starting a family law case without a lawyer is not unusual. Research by the National Self-Represented Litigants Project at the University of Windsor has documented that the majority of Ontario family court appearances involve at least one party without counsel. The goal of this article is not to minimize what lies ahead. It is to give you the orientation that most guides skip: which court, which law, which first step — before the forms.
