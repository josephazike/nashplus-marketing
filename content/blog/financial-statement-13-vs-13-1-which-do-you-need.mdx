The question of which financial statement to file is one of the first practical decisions in an Ontario family law proceeding. Most people assume the answer depends on whether they were married. It does not.

The form is determined by the claims in the proceeding. Specifically: whether any property claim has been made by either party. That determination drives the form, regardless of relationship type.

## The governing rule

Rule 13 of the *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99* governs financial disclosure. The specific provision that determines which form applies is found in Rule 13(3.1):

- **Rule 13(3.1)(1)**: A party shall serve and file a Form 13 Financial Statement (Support Claims) when the only claims in the proceeding are for support.
- **Rule 13(3.1)(2)**: A party shall serve and file a Form 13.1 Financial Statement (Property and Support Claims) when the proceeding includes a property claim.

The logic flows from there.

## The common-law respondent with a property claim

A frequently misunderstood scenario: a common-law respondent who is faced with a constructive trust claim or another property claim must file Form 13.1, not Form 13.

The *Family Law Act* equalization scheme does not apply to common-law partners. But a property claim — a constructive trust claim, a claim for a share in jointly titled property, a claim for reimbursement of contributions — is still a property claim. The rule is claim-driven, not relationship-driven.

This matters because Form 13.1 is substantially more complex than Form 13. It requires full property disclosure at three points in time: the date of marriage (or the start of cohabitation in relevant cases), the date of separation, and today. A common-law respondent who files Form 13 when their case requires Form 13.1 will need to refile, causing delay.

## Filing the wrong form

Filing Form 13 when Form 13.1 is required is a correctable error, but it creates delay and may trigger a motion to compel proper disclosure. Courts take disclosure obligations seriously. A respondent who files the wrong form in a property proceeding may face a motion from the other party, a case conference where the gap is noted, and potentially a costs consequence if the failure is treated as non-compliance with Rule 13.

If you have already filed the wrong form and need to correct it, the remedy is typically to serve and file the correct form as promptly as possible and notify the court and the other party.
