Ontario's family court system requires a financial statement from parties in most proceedings involving support or property. The question is which form you file. The answer is determined by what is being claimed in your case, not by whether you are married, common-law, or in which court the proceeding is taking place.

## The rule

Rule 13 of the *Family Law Rules*, O. Reg. 114/99, governs financial statements. The specific provisions that determine which form applies are:

- **Rule 13(3.1)(1):** A party must serve and file Form 13 (Financial Statement, Support Claims) when the case involves a support claim but no property division claim.
- **Rule 13(3.1)(2):** A party must serve and file Form 13.1 (Financial Statement, Property and Support Claims) when the case involves a property claim, either alone or together with a support claim.

The form follows the claims. If property is in issue in any way, Form 13.1 is required.

---

## The claim-driven test

The form is not determined by your relationship status. A married person whose case involves only spousal and child support files Form 13. A common-law person whose case involves a property claim files Form 13.1.

This distinction matters and catches people off guard. Common-law partners are sometimes told, or assume, that equalization only applies to married people, so they conclude Form 13.1 does not apply to them. That reasoning is partially right: the *Family Law Act* equalization scheme applies only to married spouses. But property claims between common-law partners, unjust enrichment claims, constructive trust claims, and other claims arising out of the relationship, are still property claims. A common-law partner who files or responds to a claim involving property files Form 13.1, not Form 13.

## How to identify which claims are in your case

The claims in a proceeding are identified in the application or answer filed in court. The applicant sets out what they are claiming; the respondent's answer sets out any counter-claims or positions.

If the application or answer contains any reference to:
- Equalization of net family property
- A claim to the matrimonial home
- A trust claim (constructive trust, resulting trust)
- A claim to a share of any specific asset (real estate, pension, business interest, RRSP)
- Property division under the *Family Law Act* or general principles

Then property is in issue and Form 13.1 applies.

If the only claims are for child support, spousal support, or both, and there is no property claim anywhere in the proceeding, Form 13 applies.

The income and expenses sections of both forms are substantially similar. The difference is in the property sections. Form 13.1's Part 4 requires a full accounting of assets and debts at three separate dates, date of marriage (or start of cohabitation for common-law property claims), date of separation, and today. This three-column structure is what makes Form 13.1 significantly more complex.

The three-column property table is explained in detail in the article on [the three-column property table on Form 13.1](/blog/three-column-property-valuation-13-1-ontario).

## Both forms must be sworn

Regardless of which form applies, the financial statement must be sworn or affirmed before a commissioner of oaths before it is filed. This is not optional. Providing false information in a sworn financial statement has serious legal consequences: under the *Criminal Code*, it is perjury, an indictable offence. Under the *Family Law Rules*, courts have tools to address incomplete or inaccurate financial statements.

---

## When the form requirement changes mid-proceeding

Cases evolve. A proceeding that begins as a support-only matter may have a property claim added by amendment. If property is introduced as a claim after Form 13 has been filed, the party is required to serve and file Form 13.1 in place of the earlier Form 13. The disclosure obligation follows the claims.
