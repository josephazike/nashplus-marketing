Equalization measures what each spouse accumulated during the marriage. To do that, every asset and debt has to be valued as of a specific date. That date is the valuation date. It is not arbitrary: getting it right determines what goes into the calculation and at what value.

## The legal definition

Section 4(1) of the *Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3* defines the valuation date as the earliest of the following dates:

1. The date the spouses separate and there is no reasonable prospect that they will resume cohabitation
2. The date a divorce is granted
3. The date the marriage is declared a nullity
4. The date one spouse commences an application based on section 5(3) of the Act (improvident depletion of property) that is subsequently granted
5. The date before the date on which one spouse dies leaving the other surviving

In the vast majority of cases, the valuation date is the first one on that list: the date of separation.

---

## Why the date matters

Asset values change over time. Real estate prices move. Investments fluctuate. A defined benefit pension accrues value year over year.

Equalization uses the values as they stood on the valuation date, not the values at the time of settlement or trial. That can work in either direction:

- A spouse who stayed in the family home after separation does not capture post-separation appreciation for equalization purposes. That appreciation is not included in the NFP calculation.
- A pension that declined in value after the valuation date is still calculated at its valuation-date value.

This is why the valuation date is not a formality. It shapes the numbers in the calculation directly.

---

## Disputed valuation dates

Not every separation has a clean, agreed-upon date. Particularly in cases where one or both parties remained living in the family home after the relationship effectively ended, the question of when separation actually occurred can itself become a legal dispute.

Evidence that becomes relevant when a valuation date is contested includes: when the parties began maintaining separate finances, when they stopped sharing a bed or common areas of the home, when family and friends were told of the separation, and communications between the parties around the alleged date.

If the parties cannot agree, a court can make a finding on the valuation date as a preliminary issue before the equalization calculation proceeds.

---

## The date of marriage counterpart

The equalization formula uses two dates: the valuation date and the date of marriage. The date of marriage is relevant because the NFP calculation deducts the net value each spouse owned at the time they married. Only the wealth accumulated during the marriage is equalized. Pre-marital wealth goes back to the spouse who brought it in.

The matrimonial home is an important exception: its full value at the valuation date is included in NFP with no deduction for whatever it was worth at the date of marriage.
