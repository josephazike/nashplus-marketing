"I can't afford a lawyer" usually means "I can't afford a full retainer."

It does not mean "I can't afford any legal help." The difference between those two sentences is the entire premise of this article.

A full retainer puts a lawyer on every aspect of the case from start to finish. The three-model framework puts a lawyer on specific tasks at specific moments. The per-dollar value is different at each stage of a case. This article maps where that value is highest and how to sequence a limited budget to meet it there.

## The free baseline — before you spend anything

Before spending on any professional legal help, three free resources are worth using fully. They address different needs and have different availability.

**Law Society Referral Service.** One free 30-minute consultation with a licensed Ontario lawyer or paralegal. Available through [lso.ca](https://lso.ca). Use this for the most urgent question at the start of the case — usually the jurisdiction and law question — before spending on anything else.

**Family Law Information Centres (FLICs).** At every Ontario family courthouse. Duty counsel provided by Legal Aid Ontario is available to review documents, answer procedural questions, and provide brief guidance on the day of an appearance or before it. Not ongoing representation. But a quality check on your documents before you file them is not nothing.

**Legal Aid Ontario duty counsel.** For those who meet the eligibility threshold (net annual income approximately $45,440 for families up to four, asset threshold $15,000, as of March 3, 2025), duty counsel at the courthouse provides free brief assistance at case conferences and settlement conferences. Call LAO at **1-800-668-8258** before your first court date to confirm eligibility. Duty counsel is available at early and resolution stages — not at trial, not for property division, not where the client receives government benefits.

These resources are documented, funded, and free. Exhausting them before spending on limited-scope services is the baseline.

Each model addresses a different gap. Model A provides the strategic frame the SRL otherwise lacks. Model B catches errors before they become credibility problems. Model C provides ongoing guidance for the most complex or high-stakes moments.

The models are not mutually exclusive. A case might use Model A at the start, Model B for the financial statement and conference brief, and Model A again before the settlement conference — with a total spend well under what one hour at a settlement conference with a retained lawyer would cost.

## Model A: The strategic consultation in detail

The consultation is the highest-value model per dollar at two moments in a case: the very beginning and just before a settlement conference.

**At the start of the case.** One hour with a family lawyer who can map your specific facts against how Ontario courts decide cases like yours establishes the strategic frame for everything that follows. What is legally achievable? What is your realistic best and worst outcome at trial? What does the other side have the strongest argument on? Without this, an SRL is navigating with an incomplete map.

**Before the settlement conference.** The settlement conference is the highest-leverage moment in most Ontario family cases. Judges sometimes provide non-binding assessments of likely outcomes. Both parties are expected to engage seriously with settlement. An SRL who arrives without a realistic sense of their best alternative to a negotiated agreement is at a structural disadvantage. A pre-conference consultation that recalibrates the SRL's position against realistic case outcomes is low-cost relative to what a poor outcome at this stage means.

**What to prepare for any consultation:**
- A one-page written summary of your situation (parties, claims, current status, what is disputed)
- Your three most important questions — the ones where you most need a professional answer
- Any documents that are central to your questions (most recent order, financial statement, the specific clause or motion you are asking about)

## Model B: Document review in detail

Model B is most efficient when used before any document that is difficult to correct after it is filed.

**Financial statement (Form 13 or 13.1).** A sworn document. Errors do not stay errors at filing — they surface under cross-examination. Having a lawyer review a completed Form 13 or 13.1, specifically to identify errors and gaps, is a targeted task with a targeted value. The SRL does the work; the lawyer checks it.

**Affidavits.** The content of an affidavit determines what evidence is available at the motion or conference. An affidavit filed without the right evidence structure may not support the order being requested. A review before filing catches structural problems that cannot be fixed after.

**Draft orders.** The most important document review use case. A signed order is difficult to undo. Ambiguous language in an operative clause requires a motion to clarify or vary. A review before consent ensures that the language of the order reflects the actual agreement and will be enforceable in the intended way.

---

## Model C: Ongoing coaching in detail

Coaching is most efficient when the case is approaching trial or when the other side has recently retained counsel.

A coaching lawyer does not appear in court unless separately retained for a specific appearance. They advise on preparation, argument structure, and how to respond to what the other side raises. They help you understand what the judge is likely to focus on and how to address it.

The value is highest when evidence rules are in play — which they are at trial — because the rules of evidence are the area where the SRL capability gap is largest and where preparation by someone who knows the rules makes the most practical difference.

## Middle-income gap resources

Many Ontarians earn too much for Legal Aid Ontario but cannot afford a standard retainer. Two resources are specifically designed for this gap.

**JusticeNet** provides reduced-fee legal services for middle-income Ontarians. Income-tested. Available at [justicenet.ca](https://justicenet.ca). For those who qualify, the effective cost of Model A and Model B services is materially lower.

**The Law Society of Ontario lawyer directory** at [lso.ca](https://lso.ca) allows filtering by family law. Asking any family lawyer directly whether they offer limited-scope or unbundled services is worth doing — many do, and not all advertise it prominently.

## The case for early spending

Research by the National Self-Represented Litigants Project at the University of Windsor, summarized in Justice Canada's review of court data, shows that SRLs are more likely than represented parties to end up at trial — and fare worse when they do. That pattern reflects, at least in part, the difficulty of evaluating settlement options without a realistic picture of what a court would decide.

A consultation before the settlement conference that helps an SRL accurately evaluate their position — and settle within the realistic range of outcomes — is one of the most efficient uses of a limited legal budget. The alternative is a trial that costs significantly more, produces a worse expected outcome, and extends the case for additional months or years.

That is not a general warning. It is a factual observation about a documented pattern.
