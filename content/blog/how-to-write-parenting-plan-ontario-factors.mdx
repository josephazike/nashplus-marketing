Most parenting plan guides start with a template. Fill in the blanks. Cover the categories. Done.

What they do not address is the harder question: what makes a parenting plan actually work for the children involved? Not for a judge reading it once. For a parent reading it on a Wednesday night in February wondering who is responsible for the school pickup.

That question — what serves the children's daily experience — is also the legal question. Courts assess parenting arrangements by asking what serves the child's best interests. A plan written with that standard in mind from the start is both more useful and more likely to survive scrutiny if it is ever reviewed.

## The factors courts apply

Under section 16(3) of the *Divorce Act* and section 24 of the *Children's Law Reform Act*, courts must consider the best interests of the child as the only governing standard. No factor automatically outweighs the others. Courts consider the full picture.

The *Divorce Act* section 16(3) factors include:

- The child's needs given their age and stage of development, including their need for stability
- The nature and strength of the child's relationship with each parent, siblings, grandparents, and others who play an important role
- Each parent's willingness to support the child's relationship with the other parent
- The history of care of the child
- The child's views and preferences, giving appropriate weight to the child's age and maturity
- The child's cultural, linguistic, religious and spiritual upbringing and heritage, including Indigenous upbringing and heritage
- Each parent's plans for the child's care
- Each parent's ability and willingness to care for and meet the child's needs
- Each parent's ability and willingness to communicate and cooperate on matters affecting the child
- Family violence and its impact
- Any civil or criminal proceeding, order, or condition relevant to the child's safety, security and well-being

These are not a checklist to satisfy. They are the lens through which a court — and both parents, ideally — should assess whether a proposed arrangement actually serves the child.

## What to include in a parenting plan

A plan that addresses the following questions in specific, concrete terms is significantly more useful than one written in general language.

<Checklist
  heading="Core components of a parenting plan"
  items={[
    "The regular schedule: which nights the child is with which parent, described by day of week, not just proportions",
    "Transition times and logistics: where, when, and how transitions happen (school pickup, at the door, neutral location)",
    "Holiday and special occasion schedule: listed year by year or alternating, with specific start and end times",
    "Summer and extended school-break schedule: how it differs from the regular schedule",
    "Decision-making responsibility: who makes significant decisions about education, health, and extracurriculars, and whether jointly or by one parent",
    "Day-to-day communication method: how the parents will communicate about the child (text, email, co-parenting app)",
    "How the plan will be reviewed: trigger events (a new school year, a relocation, a change in work schedule) and process",
    "Travel and relocation provisions: notice requirements, passport consent, geographic restrictions if any",
    "Emergency medical decision-making: who is contacted, what the default process is",
    "Third-party care: who provides it when neither parent is available, and what the first right of refusal looks like",
  ]}
/>

## The sections most commonly left vague

The areas where parenting plans most often generate future conflict are the areas that were left abstract. Specificity prevents disputes.

**Holiday schedules** that say "holidays will be shared equally" without specifying which parent has which holiday in which year produce conflict every December. A plan that says "Parent A has the children Christmas Day in even years; Parent B in odd years, with transitions at 2pm on December 24" eliminates that dispute in advance.

**Decision-making** described as "joint" without a process for disagreement produces stalemates. A plan that specifies who makes the decision if the parents cannot agree after 14 days, or who has final authority in specific domains, prevents those stalemates.

**Transitions** described as "both parents agree to be flexible" produces negotiation friction every week. A plan that specifies where, when, and who provides transportation removes the friction.

None of this is a legal requirement. It is the difference between a plan that works and one that does not.

## Writing for the children's experience, not the court's record

The *Divorce Act* section 16(3) includes one factor that is particularly relevant to how a plan is written: each parent's ability and willingness to communicate and cooperate on matters affecting the child.

A plan written in adversarial language — designed to limit the other parent rather than to describe the children's lives — signals something to a court that reviews it. A plan written to describe how the children's lives will actually unfold signals something different.

The practical question to ask when drafting any provision is: what does a Tuesday look like for this child under this plan? If the answer requires re-reading the plan, the provision is too vague.

## Getting the plan reviewed

Once drafted, having a family lawyer review the plan before it is filed or agreed to is one of the highest-value limited-scope tasks available. A lawyer reading the draft for enforceability, gaps, and provisions that may not survive scrutiny is a focused task. It does not require a full retainer.
