Separation is among the most stressful life events most adults experience. The research consistently places it near the top of life-event stress scales — alongside bereavement, job loss, and serious illness. What most people going through it do not know is how many access points for mental health support exist in Ontario, and how many of them do not require a referral, private insurance, or significant financial resources.

This article maps what is available. It is navigational, not prescriptive. What any individual needs from the mental health system depends on circumstances that no article can assess.

<Callout type="note">
  This article provides information about mental health resources in Ontario. It is not a substitute for professional assessment or treatment. If you are in immediate crisis, call 9-8-8 (Canada's Suicide and Crisis Helpline) or go to your nearest emergency department.
</Callout>

## The difference between crisis support and ongoing support

The mental health system has two broad categories of service, and the access points for each are different.

**Crisis support** is immediate. It is for moments when the distress is acute — when you are not safe, when you cannot manage alone, when the situation is urgent. Crisis lines, emergency departments, and walk-in mental health services are the access points for crisis.

**Ongoing support** is for the longer arc: processing grief, building coping capacity, finding stability over time. This is where therapists, counsellors, community mental health agencies, and structured programs come in. Access typically takes longer. The benefit is sustained.

The article on [crisis support in Ontario](/blog/crisis-support-ontario-who-to-call) covers crisis resources in detail. This article focuses on ongoing and intermediate support.

## Free and low-cost access points

### ConnexOntario

ConnexOntario is a free government-funded service that provides information and referrals to Ontario mental health, addiction, and problem gambling services. It is not therapy — it is navigation. A trained specialist can help you identify what kind of support would be appropriate and find agencies or programs in your area.

**Phone:** 1-866-531-2600, available 24/7
**Text:** CONNEX to 247247
**Web chat and email:** available at connexontario.ca

ConnexOntario maintains a database of over 5,300 Ontario programs. It operates in more than 130 languages. This is usually the best starting point for someone who does not know what is available in their specific community.

### 211 Ontario

211 Ontario connects Ontario residents to social services, government programs, and community supports — including mental health and addictions services. Dial 2-1-1 from any phone. It is available in multiple languages and can help identify local community mental health agencies that may offer sliding-scale fees.

### BounceBack Ontario

BounceBack is a free, evidence-based program for adults and youth 15 and older experiencing mild to moderate depression, low mood, stress, or anxiety. It is delivered by trained coaches and is based on cognitive behavioural therapy principles.

**Phone:** 1-866-345-0224
**Web:** bouncebackontario.ca

BounceBack does not require a referral. A family doctor referral is one pathway in, but self-referral is accepted. It is OHIP-funded and has no cost to participants.

## OHIP-covered options

### Through your family doctor or nurse practitioner

Ontario's OHIP system covers visits to a family doctor or nurse practitioner, including visits to discuss mental health. A family doctor can provide a referral to a psychiatrist (also OHIP-covered), refer to a community mental health agency, or provide an assessment that opens other access points.

If you do not have a family doctor, Ontario Health has a patient enrolment tool for finding accepting physicians. Nurse practitioners at Ontario Health Teams may also be accessible without a long wait.

### Community mental health agencies

Every Ontario community has publicly funded community mental health and addictions agencies that provide individual counselling and group programs at no cost or on a sliding scale. These agencies receive provincial funding and are not based on insurance or income.

ConnexOntario or 211 will find the agencies in your specific geographic area.

## Sliding-scale private therapy

Private therapists and counsellors — psychologists, registered psychotherapists, social workers — are not OHIP-covered in Ontario. However, many practitioners offer sliding-scale fees for people who cannot afford standard rates.

Sliding scale means the fee is adjusted based on income. A standard session in Ontario might run $150 to $250. A sliding-scale session with the same practitioner might be $50 to $100, or less, depending on the practitioner's specific policy.

Asking directly is the only way to know what a specific practitioner offers. The question to ask is: "Do you offer sliding-scale fees, and if so, what is your range?"

## Employee assistance programs

Many employers provide Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs) as a workplace benefit. EAPs typically offer a set number of free confidential counselling sessions — often 4 to 8 — per year, available to employees and sometimes family members. The sessions are with a registered therapist or counsellor.

EAPs are underused. If your employer provides one, the sessions are confidential, do not appear on benefits records in most cases, and are often faster to access than other options. Check with your employer's HR department or benefits provider.

## What to look for when choosing a therapist

This article describes what is available, not what any specific person needs. The match between a person and a therapist matters, and what makes a good match depends on individual factors.

Some questions that help narrow the search:
