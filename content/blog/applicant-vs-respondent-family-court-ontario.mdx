When you file the first document in a family law proceeding, the court assigns you a role: applicant. When you receive one and file a response, your role is respondent. These are procedural labels, not moral ones. Understanding which one applies to you is the starting point for everything else.

## The legal definitions

The *Family Law Rules, O. Reg. 114/99* defines both terms in rule 2(1).

**Applicant** means "a person who starts an application."

**Respondent** means "a person against whom a claim is made in an application, answer or appeal."

These definitions are spare by design. The rules are about who moves first, not about who has the stronger case or who is responsible for the breakdown of the relationship.

---

## What each role looks like in practice

The applicant files the initial application, typically Form 8 or Form 8A depending on the nature of the claims. Filing that document opens the court file, assigns a court file number, and creates the obligation to serve the respondent.

The respondent receives the application by service and has 30 days to file an answer (60 days if served outside Ontario). The answer sets out which claims the respondent agrees with, which they dispute, and any counter-claims the respondent is making. A respondent who does not file an answer may have the case proceed against them on an uncontested basis.

## The continuing record obligation

The applicant's most significant procedural responsibility is the continuing record. Under rule 9 of the *Family Law Rules*, the applicant creates the official court file: the single bound document that becomes the permanent record of the proceeding. It has two volumes: an endorsement volume (yellow cover) for judicial endorsements and orders, and a documents volume (red cover) for applications, financial statements, affidavits, and motions.

Both parties are responsible for adding their documents to the record, but the obligation to create and maintain it falls on the applicant. A disorganized continuing record creates friction at every court event. It is worth getting right early.

---

## Does the label affect the outcome?

On substantive issues, the roles are equal. Courts assess parenting claims, support claims, and property claims on their merits. Being the applicant or the respondent carries no inherent advantage on any of those issues.

The label matters for process. The applicant controls the timing of filing and carries the continuing record obligation. The respondent controls the timing of the answer and can make their own claims within it.

One thing to note: a respondent who makes counter-claims in their answer becomes, in effect, an applicant for those claims. The original applicant then has the option to file a reply addressing those counter-claims.
