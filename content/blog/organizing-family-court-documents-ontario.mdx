At a settlement conference, a judge asks: "Can you point me to the financial statement?"

Two parties. One opens a binder to a tab. The other starts searching through a bag of papers.

The judge has already formed a view. Neither party has spoken yet.

That is not anecdote. It is the structural reality of what document organization communicates in a courtroom. This article is not primarily about the continuing record's technical requirements — though those matter. It is about the parallel personal system that lets you navigate the official record fluently, and what that signals to a judge before you say a word.

## The credibility signal no one talks about

Before any legal argument is made, a court forms an impression based on what it can observe. The impression is not about who is right. It is about who is prepared.

An SRL who can find any document in the record within 15 seconds, reference the correct tab number, and hand the judge a clean, organized binder communicates something specific: they understand their case. They have been doing the work. The administrative spine of the case is not chaotic.

This matters because family law cases involve judges who see dozens of self-represented litigants. The ones who are organized are treated differently — not because the law says so, but because organization is a visible proxy for competence and reliability.

The continuing record is the court's official file. Your parallel personal system is the version you can actually use in court and at your kitchen table. Building both, and keeping them aligned, is the organizational foundation of everything else.

## What the continuing record actually is

The continuing record is the court's permanent official file, maintained by the parties under clerk supervision. It is not a filing cabinet at the court counter. It is a structured document that the parties create and maintain under Rule 9 of the *Family Law Rules*.

It has two volumes:

- **Endorsement volume (yellow cover):** all court orders and judicial endorsements made in the case. This is where the official record of what has been decided lives.
- **Documents volume (red cover):** applications, answers, financial statements, affidavits, motions, and other filed documents. This is everything else.

Case conference briefs and settlement conference briefs are not part of the continuing record unless a judge specifically orders otherwise.

The applicant creates the initial continuing record at filing. After that, all parties are responsible for adding to it when they file documents. The table of contents must be updated every time a document is added.

<Callout type="note" title="Continuing record obligation">
  Under Family Law Rules, Rule 9, the applicant creates and is responsible for the continuing record. All parties must maintain it. Documents are filed in chronological order with numbered tabs. The court clerk supervises the record and may refuse documents that do not comply with format requirements.
</Callout>

## Your parallel personal system

The continuing record is the court's file. You need your own.

The parallel personal system is a binder you maintain separately from the official record, organized to mirror it exactly. It contains everything in the continuing record, in the same order, with the same tabs. You bring it to court. You use it at home. When the judge asks for tab 7, you find it in 10 seconds.

Building it correctly from the start costs a few hours. Rebuilding it from a bag of papers the night before a settlement conference costs something you cannot recover.

## The tab system and table of contents

Documents in the continuing record are filed in chronological order. The most recent document is at the back. Each document is identified by a numbered tab. The table of contents identifies, for each tab: the volume it is in, the tab number, the document type, which party filed it, and the date filed.

The table of contents is a navigation tool. Judges use it to find documents quickly. An SRL who maintains it diligently signals to the court that the record is reliable. An SRL whose table of contents is out of date or missing creates friction at the moments when friction is most costly.

## Financial statement freshness

Rule 13 of the *Family Law Rules* requires updated financial statements within:

- **60 days before any conference** (case conference, settlement conference, trial management conference)
- **40 days before trial**

This is one of the most commonly missed timing obligations. The update trigger is not the conference date — it is when the case gets a conference date. The moment a conference date is set, the financial statement update deadline is calculable and should go in the calendar immediately.

<Callout type="warning" title="Financial statement update trigger">
  Family Law Rules, Rule 13 requires updated financial statements within 60 days of any conference. Mark the update deadline the same day you receive or confirm a conference date. An outdated financial statement at a settlement conference creates procedural problems and a credibility issue.
</Callout>

## The strategic layer

The continuing record is not paperwork. It is the lens through which a judge sees the entire case, from the first document filed to the last order made. An SRL who maintains it correctly — and who can navigate it fluently in court — communicates competence before they have made a single legal argument.
