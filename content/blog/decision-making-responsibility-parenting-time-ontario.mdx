If you have been reading about family law for more than a few years, you may have encountered the terms "sole custody," "joint custody," and "access." These terms are no longer used in federal family law.

On March 1, 2021, the *Divorce Act* amendments came into force. The language of family law in Canada changed. Courts no longer make custody and access orders under the *Divorce Act*. They make parenting orders that allocate decision-making responsibility and parenting time.

This article explains what those terms mean, what the change was intended to accomplish, and what it does and does not affect.

## What changed and when

The *Divorce Act* was amended by the *An Act to amend the Divorce Act, the Family Orders and Agreements Enforcement Assistance Act and the Garnishment, Attachment and Pension Diversion Act and to make consequential amendments to another Act* (known as Bill C-78), which came into force on **March 1, 2021**.

The substantive change: the Act replaced the concepts of "custody" and "access" with "decision-making responsibility" and "parenting time." Courts making orders under the *Divorce Act* now use this language. The change applies to new orders made after March 1, 2021. Existing orders made before that date remain in effect and use the older language.

This change applies to the federal *Divorce Act*, which governs married spouses who are divorcing. Ontario's *Children's Law Reform Act* governs parenting arrangements for non-divorcing parties. That Act uses different terminology but addresses substantially similar concepts. When this article discusses the *Divorce Act* language, it applies to divorcing married spouses.

## What "decision-making responsibility" means

Decision-making responsibility refers to the authority to make significant decisions about a child's life. The *Divorce Act* section 16.1 gives courts the authority to allocate decision-making responsibility in a parenting order.

These decisions typically involve:

- Education (which school, what programs)
- Health care (medical treatment, therapy, medication decisions)
- Religious upbringing and significant cultural decisions
- Extracurricular activities (to the extent they are significant)

Day-to-day decisions during time spent with the child — what the child eats, what time they go to bed, routine childcare — are made by whichever parent the child is with at the time. Decision-making responsibility as a legal concept applies to the significant, lasting decisions.

Courts can allocate decision-making responsibility in different ways. One parent may hold it for all significant decisions. Both parents may share it for all decisions. The court may allocate responsibility differently for different domains — one parent responsible for health decisions, both responsible jointly for education, for example.

---

## What "parenting time" means

Parenting time is the time a child spends with a parent under a parenting order. During that time, the parent with whom the child is present makes day-to-day decisions about the child's care.

The term replaces "access." Under the old framework, the parent with custody had primary residence. The other parent had "access" — a word that described the parent's right to spend time with the child. "Parenting time" describes the same reality without the hierarchy implied by "access."

The *Divorce Act* section 16(6) states that the court must give effect to the principle that a child should have as much time with each parent as is consistent with the child's best interests. This is not a presumption of equal time. It is a principle that the court considers alongside all other factors.

## Why the terminology changed

The language change reflected a concern that the old terms implied winners and losers. A parent with "custody" had won. A parent with "access" had lost. That framing was seen as contributing to adversarial litigation and making it harder for parents to co-operate after separation.

The new language is intended to describe both parents as parenting — one may have the child more of the time, but both have parenting time, and neither is merely a visitor.

Whether the language change has meaningfully shifted outcomes is a question for researchers, not for this article. What it has changed is how court documents, parenting plans, and legal arguments are framed. If you are drafting a parenting plan or reading a court order made after March 1, 2021, you will encounter this language. Now you know what it means.

---

## What has not changed

The legal standard has not changed. Courts still apply the best interests of the child as the governing principle. The factors courts consider are now set out in *Divorce Act* section 16(3). The substance of those factors — the child's needs, the history of care, each parent's willingness to support the other parent's relationship with the child — predates the 2021 amendments.

Pre-2021 orders using the old language remain enforceable. You do not need to apply to court to have a 2019 custody order "updated" to use the new language. The order remains valid. When you apply to vary it, the new order will use current terminology.
