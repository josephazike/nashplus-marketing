Every parenting dispute that reaches a court in Ontario is resolved by applying the same standard: the best interests of the child.

Not what the parents prefer. Not what seems fair between adults. Not what the child asks for, though that is one of the factors considered. The governing question — the only question — is what arrangement serves the child's best interests.

That phrase sounds simple. The legal content of it is not. This article explains what the law requires a court to consider, how that list is structured, and what it means in practice.

## Two laws, one standard

Ontario parenting disputes fall under two different legislative frameworks depending on the circumstances.

The **federal *Divorce Act*** applies when married spouses are divorcing. Parenting orders made in the context of a divorce are governed by the *Divorce Act*, and the best interests factors are set out in section 16(3).

The **provincial *Children's Law Reform Act* (CLRA)** applies to parenting disputes outside of divorce proceedings — including disputes between common-law partners, disputes that arise after a divorce is complete, and parenting matters in the Ontario Court of Justice. The best interests factors are set out in CLRA section 24.

The two lists are similar but not identical. For most practical purposes the standard operates the same way: both laws require courts to consider only the best interests of the child. Both use a non-exhaustive list of factors. Neither list is a checklist where checking all boxes produces a result.

---

## The Divorce Act factors

Section 16(3) of the *Divorce Act* lists the factors a court must consider. The court must give **primary consideration** to the child's physical, emotional and psychological safety, security and well-being.

The full list of section 16(3) factors:

**(a)** The child's needs, given their age and stage of development, including the need for stability

**(b)** The nature and strength of the child's relationship with each parent, each of the child's siblings and grandparents, and any other person who plays an important role in the child's life

**(c)** Each parent's willingness to support the development and maintenance of the child's relationship with the other parent

**(d)** The history of care of the child

**(e)** The child's views and preferences, giving due weight to the child's age and maturity, unless they cannot be ascertained

**(f)** The child's cultural, linguistic, religious and spiritual upbringing and heritage, including Indigenous upbringing and heritage

**(g)** Any plans for the child's care

**(h)** The ability and willingness of each person to care for and meet the needs of the child

**(i)** The ability and willingness of each person to communicate and cooperate with the other parent on matters affecting the child

**(j)** Any family violence and its impact on the child and on the ability of the person who engaged in the violence to care for the child

**(k)** Any civil or criminal proceeding, order, condition, or measure that is relevant to the child's safety, security and well-being

*Divorce Act*, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), s. 16(3), as amended (in force March 1, 2021).

---

## What the list is not

The list does not tell a court how to weigh these factors against each other. Two cases with similar facts can produce different outcomes if the children's circumstances are different. The list is a framework for analysis, not a formula.

No single factor automatically determines the outcome. A parent who has been the primary caregiver has a significant factor in their favour under (d). But a court also looks at future plans for care under (g), communication capacity under (i), and safety under (j). The full picture governs.

<Callout type="note" title="The past-care factor">
  Factor (d) — the history of care — is often significant in practice. Courts take seriously who has been the primary day-to-day caregiver during the relationship. This is an observed pattern in how courts apply the standard. It is not a guarantee of any outcome in any specific case. That is not legal advice. It is information about how the standard operates.
</Callout>

## Factor (c): the willingness to support the other parent's relationship

Factor (c) — each parent's willingness to support the child's relationship with the other parent — is worth examining separately because it appears in almost every contested parenting case.

Courts look at whether each parent supports the child's relationship with the other parent or undermines it. A parent who speaks negatively about the other parent in front of the child, who interferes with communication, or who uses the child to gather information about the other parent is demonstrating the opposite of what this factor asks for.

That is not a moral judgment about what parents should do. It is a description of a legal factor that courts weigh. What parents do in this regard, over time and on the record, becomes relevant evidence.

---

## Factor (j): family violence

The 2021 *Divorce Act* amendments added explicit and detailed treatment of family violence in the best interests analysis. Section 16(4) lists specific considerations when assessing family violence, including the nature of the violence, whether it was directed at the child, and its impact on the child's safety and well-being.

The Act also states, in section 16(5), that a history of family violence does not, in itself, prevent a parent from having parenting time. But the nature and impact of that violence is a factor the court must consider. Courts assess whether the proposed arrangement adequately protects the child.

---

## The child's views

Factor (e) — the child's views and preferences — is often misunderstood. Children do not choose which parent they live with. Courts do not hand children a ballot.

What courts do is consider the child's expressed views as one factor, weighted according to the child's age and maturity. A 16-year-old's stated preference carries more weight than a 5-year-old's. Neither is determinative.

Where the court determines that a child's views should be formally assessed, it may request involvement of the [Office of the Children's Lawyer](/blog/office-of-the-childrens-lawyer-ontario), which can produce a report that includes the child's views alongside a broader assessment of the child's circumstances.

---

## The CLRA standard

For parenting disputes outside a divorce — common-law separations, Ontario Court of Justice proceedings — the governing standard is section 24 of the *Children's Law Reform Act*. The CLRA factors parallel the *Divorce Act* factors closely and apply the same central principle: the court's only consideration is the best interests of the child.

Where the CLRA applies, courts consider comparable factors around the child's needs, the nature of the parental relationship, history of care, the child's views, and family violence. The same framework, the same emphasis on the child's experience, and the same absence of a formula for how the factors are weighted.
