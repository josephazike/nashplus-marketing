There is a particular friction in trying to remember, in the middle of a court hearing, exactly what was agreed about a pickup time six weeks ago. Whether the agreement was in a text, in an email, or in a conversation that one parent says happened differently than the other recalls.

Documentation exists to resolve that friction. Not by proving one parent right and the other wrong — courts draw their own conclusions — but by replacing disputed memory with a timestamped record.

## Why the communication record is often the central evidence

In parenting disputes, what parents say happened is frequently disputed. One parent says a pickup was refused. The other says the request was unclear. One parent says an agreement was made. The other says no agreement existed.

Courts resolve these disputes by weighing evidence. Contemporaneous records — records made at or near the time of the event, not reconstructed afterward — are generally given more weight than later recollections. A text message from the day of the missed pickup, timestamped, unaltered, is a different quality of evidence than a parent's description of that day at a hearing six months later.

Under Rule 24 of the *Family Law Rules*, courts may award costs against a party who conducts litigation unreasonably — including bringing motions without adequate evidentiary basis. A clear communication record can provide that basis, or show that one did not exist.

---

## What makes a record useful in court

Not all documentation is equally useful. The qualities that make a communication record useful as evidence are:

**Contemporaneous.** Made at or near the time of the relevant event, not reconstructed months later. A note made the day of a missed parenting time, describing what happened, is more credible than a note made just before a court date.

**Specific.** Includes date, time, what was said or done, and by whom. "The children were not available for the scheduled 3pm Friday transition on May 9, 2026" is more useful than "the other parent frequently misses pickups."

**Factual.** Describes observable events, not inferences about the other parent's motivations or character. Courts look for what happened, not what it means about the other parent.

**Unaltered.** Records that cannot be edited after the fact carry more weight. A co-parenting app message cannot be deleted after sending. A screenshot of a text conversation can be cropped or edited. A platform-exported PDF with a certified record header is different from a screenshot.

**Preserved.** Records need to survive until they are relevant. Photographs, screenshots, and app exports should be stored somewhere that is not just the phone the other parent might damage or claim is unavailable.

## What to document

<Checklist
  heading="Events worth documenting with date, time, and factual description"
  items={[
    "Missed or refused parenting time transitions (date, time, what happened, any communication in the lead-up)",
    "Agreements made verbally about schedule changes (who agreed, when, terms — follow up in writing immediately)",
    "Concerns about the child's well-being observed at transitions (physical condition, statements made, demeanor — factual description only)",
    "School or medical decisions the other parent made without your involvement if joint decision-making applies",
    "Any violation of a court order or parenting agreement (cite the specific provision, describe the violation)",
    "Communication that escalates, contains threats, or is otherwise notable",
    "Positive interactions and agreements honored — the record should not only contain conflict",
  ]}
/>

That last item is worth pausing on. A documentation record that consists entirely of grievances, catalogued over months, tells a story to a judge. A record that also includes routine agreements honored, cooperative exchanges, and normal co-parenting activity tells a different and more credible story.

## The different documentation formats

**Written communication platforms.** Email, co-parenting app messages, and text messages are already documented — they exist in a logged form. The task is to preserve them, export them when relevant, and not alter them.

**A contemporaneous log.** A dated journal entry made at the time of a relevant event. This is the simplest form of documentation and requires no technology. The value is in the date and the specificity.

**Co-parenting app exports.** Dedicated platforms produce exportable PDF records. These are described in more detail in the articles on [co-parenting apps](/blog/coparenting-apps-compared-ontario) and the [specific platform overview](/blog/ourfamilywizard-appclose-2houses-neutral-overview).

**Photographs and video.** For concerns about the child's physical condition or environment, timestamped photographs may be relevant. These should be factual documentation — not curated to create a misleading impression — and stored somewhere secure.

## What documentation cannot do

Documentation captures what happened. It does not determine what a court will do about it.

A well-maintained communication record provides evidence. Courts weigh evidence. How a court weighs a given record depends on the full context of the case, the quality of all the evidence, the credibility of both parties, and the applicable law.

Documentation that consists of a parent cataloguing every minor disagreement as evidence of the other parent's unfitness may be read by a court as evidence of something different: a parent whose priority is litigation rather than the children's stability.

<Callout type="warning" title="Documentation that hurts">
  Courts have noted, in costs decisions under Rule 24, that a pattern of excessive, escalating motions — often grounded in an extensive grievance log — can reflect unreasonable litigation conduct. Documentation is most useful when it is proportionate and factual, not when it functions as a running prosecution.
</Callout>

## Rule 13 and financial disclosure

A different type of documentation obligation exists under Rule 13 of the *Family Law Rules*: both parties in most Ontario family law proceedings are required to make full financial disclosure. That obligation has its own documentation requirements — income, assets, expenses — that are separate from communication documentation.

The article on [building your disclosure package efficiently](/blog/building-disclosure-package-efficiently-ontario) in the SRL Playbook covers the financial documentation side.
